## History

Vocal jazz developed alongside instrumental jazz from the 1920s, with Louis Armstrong popularizing scat singing on tracks like "Heebie Jeebies" (1926) and laying the foundation for swinging, improvisational vocal style. The Swing Era of the 1930s and 1940s saw the rise of iconic vocalists including Ella Fitzgerald, Billie Holiday, and Sarah Vaughan, and in the post-World War II era from 1951 to 1968, vocal jazz flourished alongside the rise of the long-playing record, with Fitzgerald, Holiday, and Vaughan often grouped as jazz vocal's "Holy Trinity" — Vaughan celebrated particularly for her impeccable pitch and range. Jazz vocalists evolved during this period from background performers to genuine stars in their own right, introducing scat singing, blues inflection, and innovative phrasing that expanded jazz's emotional range and musical complexity well beyond what instrumentalists alone had established.

## Music Theory

Vocal jazz draws its harmonic vocabulary from the Great American Songbook tradition — standard ii-V-I progressions, turnarounds, and extended, altered chords (9ths, 11ths, 13ths, altered dominants) — providing sophisticated harmonic material for improvisation. Scat singing introduces genuine vocal improvisation using nonsense syllables that mimic instrumental solo lines, while blues inflections incorporating blue notes and sliding pitches add expressive color distinct from purely instrumental jazz improvisation.

## Arrangement

Performances typically state the "head" — the song's central, recognizable melody, often drawn from the Great American Songbook — before improvisatory choruses build harmonic and rhythmic interest across successive passes through the song form. A scat singing chorus, where the vocalist improvises as a genuine instrumental voice trading with or following band solos, is frequently the performance's technical and expressive highlight, with performances often closing on a tag ending — a repeated final phrase with fermata or ritardando — as a genre-signature device.

## Instruments

Accompaniment ranges from full big band (complete horn section) to small combo (piano trio plus vocalist), with the vocalist treated as an equal soloist alongside horns and piano rather than simply a melody carrier over accompaniment. Walking upright bass outlining the chord changes and swing-style drums (ride cymbal pattern or brushes for ballads) provide the rhythmic-harmonic foundation that vocal phrasing and improvisation is built against.

## Sound Design

As a fundamentally acoustic genre, vocal jazz's sound design comes entirely from natural vocal timbre, microphone technique, and combo/big-band instrumental blend rather than electronic processing. Vibrato, pitch bending, and expressive dynamic shading function as the vocalist's primary expressive devices, analogous to the modulation and articulation techniques instrumentalists use.

## MIDI Programming

Program a walking bass line outlining the chord changes and a swing-style ride cymbal pattern establishing authentic swing feel, writing or arranging the head melody closely following Great American Songbook tradition while leaving space for scat improvisation choruses. Natural swing-feel rubato, expressive phrasing, and vibrato are essential and central to the genre — a rigid, quantized vocal performance is fundamentally incompatible with vocal jazz's core expressive identity.

## Mixing

EQ should stay warm and natural on vocals, preserving the singer's full expressive range and tone balanced clearly against combo or big-band instrumentation, with light, transparent compression preserving natural vocal dynamics rather than aggressive loudness processing. Warm, natural room or hall reverb appropriate to a jazz club or concert-hall context is the standard spatial treatment.

## Mastering

Modern recording or reissue masters typically target -16 to -13 LUFS integrated, preserving high dynamic range appropriate to the genre's fundamentally expressive, acoustic performance character, with a warm, full-range vocal presence balanced naturally against the ensemble.

## Reference Artists

Ella Fitzgerald's 1947 live performance of "How High the Moon" showcases her legendary scat improvisation and three-octave range; Sarah Vaughan's "Lullaby of Birdland" demonstrates her bebop-influenced harmonic sophistication, while Billie Holiday's "Strange Fruit" and Louis Armstrong's "Heebie Jeebies" represent the genre's emotional depth and foundational scat-singing tradition respectively.

## Production Notes

Treat the vocalist as a genuine improvising instrumentalist, not simply a melody-and-lyrics carrier, since scat singing and expressive phrasing are the genre's core artistic content on par with instrumental soloing. Build authentic swing feel in the rhythm section first, since vocal phrasing depends entirely on a genuinely swinging foundation, and avoid treating vocal jazz as pop vocals over a jazz backing track or over-correcting vocal performance — both are fundamentally incompatible with the genre's essential expressive character.
