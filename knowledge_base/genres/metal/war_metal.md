## History

War metal (also called bestial black metal) emerged in the late 1980s and early 1990s as a chaotic, cacophonous fusion of first-wave black metal (particularly Brazil's Sarcofago and Vulcano) with the brutal intensity of death metal (via bands like Autopsy) and grindcore's relentless speed. Canada's Blasphemy codified the sound with their 1990 debut Fallen Angel of Doom, widely credited as the genre's foundational recording. Simultaneously, a Finnish scene developed a parallel style through bands including Beherit (whose 1993 album Drawing Down the Moon remains a genre landmark) and Archgoat, formed in 1989 and instrumental in establishing the raw, blast-beat-driven Finnish war metal sound. The term "war metal" itself is credited to members of Australian band Bestial Warlust, another early and influential act. The genre has remained a distinct, deliberately underground offshoot of blackened death metal, prioritizing chaos and raw aggression over the atmospheric, melodically legible approach of mainstream black metal.

## Music Theory

War metal deliberately rejects clear melodic or harmonic development. Tremolo-picked riffs function as dense, repetitive texture rather than a discernible chord progression, and the mix is engineered so that individual riffs are frequently difficult to pick out from the surrounding wall of noise. Where traditional black metal is, underneath its raw production, highly melodic and rooted in clear minor-key movement, war metal treats tonality as almost incidental to the genre's real goal: an overwhelming, cacophonous atmosphere of chaos.

## Arrangement

Songs favor sustained assault over conventional song structure — a wash of feedback or noise often opens the track before blast beats enter at or near full speed with little buildup, and the "verse" is frequently just a continuous barrage rather than a distinct formal section. War metal generally rejects hook-based choruses, occasionally allowing a sudden, crushing tempo drop for a few bars as its primary structural contrast device, before dissolving back into feedback or noise at the close.

## Instruments

The bass guitar is pushed forward and heavily distorted, frequently the most dominant low-end element in the mix — a direct inversion of traditional black metal's tendency to bury or omit the bass entirely. Guitars are heavily distorted and down-tuned, playing simple, repetitive riffs deliberately mixed to sound indistinct rather than the crisp, bright "wasp" tone of black metal. Drums are played at extreme blast-beat tempos but recorded and mixed to sound distant and murky rather than punchy, and vocals sit in a lower, death-metal-adjacent growl rather than black metal's high shriek.

## Sound Design

War metal's "sound design" is an intentional pursuit of murk and chaos: deliberately low-fidelity recording and mixing choices create a cacophonous, bass-heavy wash in which individual instruments are hard to distinguish. Reverb stays minimal, favoring a dry, suffocating proximity over black metal's cavernous atmosphere, while tape/analog distortion and saturation further obscure individual instrument definition rather than clarifying it.

## MIDI Programming

Program relentless blast beats at the top of the tempo range (180-280+ BPM), aiming for a dense, thudding, murky drum sound rather than the crisp, articulate hits common in modern death metal. Bass should be programmed with simple, heavy root-note patterns played with extreme distortion, competing with or dominating the guitar for low-end presence — the opposite of traditional black metal's buried bass approach. There is little discrete melody to program; riff fragments should function as repetitive texture within the overall wall of noise.

## Mixing

Mixing should be bass-heavy and deliberately murky, pushing low-mid and low-end weight rather than the treble-forward "wasp" tone of traditional black metal, obscuring individual instrument definition by design. Reverb stays minimal for a dry, claustrophobic intensity, and the stereo image reads comparatively narrow and dense — closer to a mono wall of noise than a wide, atmospheric spread.

## Mastering

Masters run loud and crushed, roughly -8 to -6 LUFS integrated, with dynamics kept undifferentiated in service of the genre's chaotic aesthetic rather than the atmospheric dynamic movement found in black or symphonic black metal. The frequency balance stays bass-heavy and murky, a direct sonic departure from traditional black metal's cold, thin, treble-dominant mastering approach.

## Reference Artists

Blasphemy's "Fallen Angel of Doom" (1990) is widely regarded as the recording that codified war metal's sound. Archgoat's early-1990s releases and Beherit's Drawing Down the Moon (1993) document the parallel Finnish development of the style, and Bestial Warlust (Australia) is credited with coining the genre's name.

## Production Notes

The clearest production distinction between war metal and traditional black metal is the low end: push the bass forward, heavy and distorted, rather than burying it, and aim the overall mix toward murky, bass-dominant chaos rather than black metal's cold, treble-forward clarity. Record vocals in a lower, guttural register rather than a high shriek, and resist the urge to clean up or clarify the guitar riffs — if individual riffs become easily discernible, the mix has likely drifted toward blackened death metal rather than genuine war metal chaos.
