# Modes in Popular and Genre Music

The three modes that recur constantly across this knowledge base's genre entries — Dorian, Mixolydian, and Phrygian — each carry a specific, well-documented emotional and stylistic association rather than being interchangeable "minor-ish" or "major-ish" color choices. Composer-facing genre files consistently reach for a particular mode because of what it does harmonically that the plain major or natural minor scale can't, and recognizing which mode a genre is actually built on (rather than approximating with natural minor) is often the difference between an idiomatic-sounding part and a generic one.

## Dorian: Minor With a Bright, Unresolved Sixth

Dorian is natural minor with a raised sixth degree, and that single altered note is responsible for its entire character — a minor-key sound that doesn't fully resolve into natural minor's darker gravity, staying harmonically "open" or unresolved in a way genres use for groove-first, less dramatic minor-key material. `house.md` and `grunge.md` both list Dorian explicitly among common scales, and `celtic.md` documents it as a defining mode for traditional dance-tune repertoire, with "i - VII (Dorian-mode vamps)" as a specific progression. `west_coast_hip_hop.md` and `g_funk.md` both cite Dorian as "funk-derived" — P-Funk's groove-first minor vamps lean on Dorian's characteristic sixth for warmth that pure natural minor doesn't offer. `modal_jazz.md` treats Dorian as a full compositional framework rather than an occasional color choice: its melodic characteristics describe "raised/lowered scale degrees like the Dorian natural 6th" as the actual substance of improvisation, with melodic lines built from "scalar/motion exploration rather than outlining fast-moving chord changes."

## Mixolydian: Major With a Flattened Seventh, for Groove Over Resolution

Mixolydian is major with a lowered seventh degree, which removes the strong pull the leading tone (the natural major 7th) creates toward the tonic — the result is a bright, major-feeling scale that can vamp indefinitely without needing to resolve, which is exactly why it recurs in groove-first and montuno-vamp genres. `celtic.md` lists it alongside Dorian as a core traditional-tune mode. `latin_salsa.md` cites "Mixolydian (montuno-influenced vamps)" directly — the piano montuno's repeating vamp figure depends on a scale that doesn't demand harmonic resolution the way a leading-tone-containing major scale would. `reggae.md` lists Mixolydian among its common scales for the same underlying reason: reggae's simple, repeated I-IV-V loops benefit from a scale color that can sit on a single chord for extended durations without feeling harmonically incomplete. `blues_rock.md` and `bebop.md`'s "bebop dominant scale" (Mixolydian plus a chromatic passing tone) both treat Mixolydian as the natural scale choice over a dominant/V chord specifically, reflecting its close relationship to dominant-seventh harmony.

## Phrygian: Minor With a Flattened Second, for Darkness and Exoticism

Phrygian is natural minor with a lowered second degree, producing the semitone-above-the-tonic relationship that gives the mode its distinctively dark, tense, "exotic" quality — this is the single most genre-differentiating mode in the knowledge base, appearing specifically wherever a genre wants intensity beyond what natural or harmonic minor provides. `flamenco.md` is the clearest and most complete documentation: "Phrygian mode (the 'flamenco scale') — the genre's defining harmonic/melodic color," built around "the tonic and flat-second relationship," with the closely related Andalusian cadence (i-VII-VI-V) as the genre's single most defining progression. `metal.md` and `metalcore.md` both cite "Phrygian dominant" (Phrygian with a raised third, common in flamenco and Middle Eastern music) specifically for its darker-than-harmonic-minor color in riff writing. `phonk.md` and `coldwave.md` both reach for Phrygian for a "darker, more aggressive" or unsettling modern color, and `dark_ambient.md` pairs Phrygian with Locrian (an even more unstable mode, built on a diminished tonic triad) specifically for its "unsettling intervals."

## Choosing a Mode Instead of Approximating With Natural Minor

Because Dorian, Mixolydian, and natural minor/major share most of their notes with their parent scale (differing by only one altered degree), it's easy to default to plain natural minor or major and lose the specific color a genre actually calls for. The practical test documented across these genre files: if a minor-key vamp needs to feel bright, unresolved, and groove-sustaining rather than dark and gravity-pulled, that's Dorian, not natural minor (see `house.md`, `g_funk.md`, `celtic.md`). If a major-key vamp needs to sit indefinitely on one chord without wanting to resolve, that's Mixolydian, not major/Ionian (see `latin_salsa.md`, `reggae.md`). If a minor-key idea needs to feel darker and more unsettling than harmonic minor already provides, that's Phrygian (see `flamenco.md`, `metal.md`).

## Common Mistakes

**Treating Dorian and natural minor as interchangeable.** The raised sixth is the entire point of choosing Dorian — writing a "Dorian" bassline that never actually uses the sixth degree, or uses a flattened sixth instead, produces plain natural minor with none of the intended brighter, unresolved character.

**Using Mixolydian's flat-seventh color but resolving it like a leading tone anyway.** Mixolydian's harmonic usefulness in vamp-based genres comes specifically from *not* creating strong resolution pressure — writing a chord progression that still functionally resolves as if the seventh degree were a leading tone defeats the purpose of choosing the mode.

**Reaching for Phrygian dominant without checking whether plain Phrygian (no raised third) is what the genre actually wants.** `flamenco.md` and `metal.md`/`metalcore.md` use related but distinct flavors of Phrygian color — flamenco's harmony is built on the Andalusian cadence's specific i-VII-VI-V shape, while metal's "Phrygian dominant" adds a raised third for an even more exotic, harmonic-minor-adjacent tension. Confirm which variant a given genre's harmonic language actually calls for.
