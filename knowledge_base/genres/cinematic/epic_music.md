## History

Epic music originated in the 1990s as licensing-only trailer music, produced internally by specialized houses for the film-advertising industry rather than for public release. As trailer campaigns demanded ever-larger sonic spectacles, these companies refined a recognizable toolkit — string ostinatos, heroic brass themes, taiko-styled percussion, dramatic choir, and sound-design impacts including the now-iconic "braaam" blast. Companies including Immediate Music, Two Steps From Hell (founded 2006 by Thomas Bergersen and Nick Phoenix), and Audiomachine popularized the style across Hollywood trailer campaigns, video game promos, and TV spots. The genre crossed into public commercial release when Globus's Epicon (2008) — featuring a 100-piece orchestra and 70-member choir — became available for purchase, followed by Immediate Music's Trailerhead. Two Steps From Hell's "Heart of Courage," composed by Bergersen and originally circulated only within the industry on the 2008 demo Legend, became publicly available on the group's first commercial album, Invincible (2010), and stands as one of the genre's most recognized tracks. The fanbase itself coined the name "Epic Music" as composers began marketing the style directly to listeners outside the film industry.

## Music Theory

Epic music favors minor-key harmonic movement (i-VI-III-VII, i-iv-v-i) that reads as heroic and dramatic within seconds, prioritizing immediate emotional legibility over harmonic complexity. Rather than functional cadences, the genre often relies on repeating, sustained orchestral "hits" — the braaam — that function more like a rhythmic/textural punctuation than a chord progression resolution. Melodies are bold, repeating, and built for instant impact within a short cue rather than the longer thematic development typical of feature film scoring.

## Arrangement

Cues are structured around a buildup-to-climax arc rather than conventional song form: a sparse, tension-building intro gives way to an ostinato-driven verse, progressively layering additional instrument sections — strings, then brass, then choir, then percussion — toward a full-ensemble climax. A breakdown to sparser texture often precedes a second, larger buildup into the track's ultimate peak, and the outro either cuts suddenly (trailer-style) or resolves on a sustained chord for standalone listening.

## Instruments

A full orchestral palette (strings, brass, woodwinds, and often large-scale choir) forms the acoustic foundation, layered with hybrid electronic elements — synth pulses, sub-bass, and processed percussion — for a modern, aggressive edge. Percussion is heavily layered and doubled, combining orchestral instruments like taiko drums and timpani with hybrid samples to achieve a scale beyond what any real percussion section alone could produce, and sub-bass synthesizers extend the low end beyond acoustic double-bass range.

## Sound Design

The genre's signature sound-design element is the "braaam" — a sustained, detuned brass/synth hit used at key structural moments for dramatic authority. Risers, downers, stingers, whooshes, and massive impact hits, none of them part of a standard orchestra, are layered alongside acoustic hits to push the sense of scale beyond natural instrument capability, and heavy convolution reverb creates the genre's massive cinematic sense of space.

## MIDI Programming

Bass parts should use sustained root-note pedal tones doubled with sub-bass synth for weight, and drum/percussion programming should layer multiple hit samples per beat rather than relying on a single instrument — taiko-style rhythmic patterns are a genre signature for build sections. Melodic themes should be bold and repeating rather than intricately developed, and velocity/dynamics should span an extreme range from near-silent intros to maximum-intensity climaxes; unlike traditionally humanized orchestral scoring, tight, precise ensemble hits are genre-appropriate and common.

## Mixing

Deliberately carve space for each layered element — orchestra, choir, hybrid percussion, and sub-bass — since the genre's core identity depends on simultaneously stacking many loud elements without collapsing into mud; sidechain or duck competing low-end sources against each other. Heavy parallel compression on percussion and hit layers maximizes perceived power, large cinematic convolution reverb is essential to the genre's sense of scale, and stereo imaging should spread the orchestra and choir very wide while keeping sub-bass and low-end elements centered.

## Mastering

Masters target roughly -9 to -7 LUFS integrated, with wide dynamic range preserved between intro/breakdown sections and full-ensemble climaxes even though the climaxes themselves are mixed loud and dense. The frequency balance favors extended sub-bass reinforcement, a full and present midrange for brass/strings/choir, and a bright, cutting top end for cymbals, choir sibilance, and hit transients.

## Reference Artists

Two Steps From Hell, founded by Thomas Bergersen and Nick Phoenix, is the genre's most widely recognized act, with "Heart of Courage" — originally an industry-only demo track later released on Invincible (2010) — standing as one of epic music's defining recordings. Globus's Epicon (2008), featuring "Preliator" alongside a 100-piece orchestra and 70-member choir, is credited as the album that launched epic music as a publicly purchasable commercial genre. Immediate Music and Audiomachine round out the genre's foundational production houses.

## Production Notes

- Structure cues around a clear buildup-to-climax arc, progressively layering strings, brass, choir, and percussion, rather than conventional verse-chorus songwriting.
- Layer multiple percussion and hit samples per impact event to achieve the genre's characteristic massive scale.
- Use the braaam deliberately and sparingly at key structural moments — overuse dates the track quickly.
- Blend a hybrid synth pulse or processed rhythm underneath acoustic string ostinatos for a modern, driving edge alongside the orchestral core.
- Avoid treating the genre as identical to film score — epic music is built for immediate, standalone dramatic impact within a short cue, not a feature film's subtler narrative pacing.
- Modern hybrid approach: use dedicated hybrid-orchestral sample libraries built for epic/trailer scoring, and automate riser/downer sweeps precisely to each buildup's length for professional, tight transitions.
