# Quartal Harmony: Chords Built From Fourths Instead of Thirds

Quartal harmony builds chords by stacking fourths rather than the thirds that construct conventional triads and extended tertian chords (documented in `knowledge_base/music_theory/chords/extended_jazz_chords.md`). This knowledge base documents quartal voicing as a specific, deliberate alternative to bebop's dense tertian stacks, tied closely to modal jazz's broader harmonic philosophy of open, ambiguous color over functional resolution.

## Quartal Voicings as Modal Jazz's Signature Comping Texture

`modal_jazz.md` names quartal harmony directly as the genre's defining keyboard voicing choice: "Acoustic piano is central — comping with quartal (fourths-based) voicings and open modal chords (e.g. Bill Evans' voicings on Kind of Blue) rather than dense bebop ii-V-I stacks." The file's professional tips reinforce this as a specific technique to actively choose rather than default away from: "Use quartal piano voicings (stacked fourths) rather than tertian bebop voicings for authentic comping texture." The explicit contrast with "dense bebop ii-V-I stacks" is the key functional point — quartal voicings are chosen specifically *because* they sound more open and less harmonically saturated than the tertian extended chords (9ths, 11ths, 13ths stacked in thirds) that define bebop's comping vocabulary.

## Why Fourths Produce a Different Harmonic Color Than Thirds

A chord built from stacked thirds (a standard triad, or an extended 7th/9th/11th/13th chord) has a clear tonal center and, in most voicings, an audible sense of major/minor quality from the intervals between adjacent chord tones. A chord built from stacked fourths lacks that same clear major/minor identity — the interval of a perfect fourth is harmonically more ambiguous than a third, which is exactly why quartal voicings suit modal jazz's broader aesthetic of extended, slow-moving modal color (documented in `knowledge_base/music_theory/scales/modes_in_popular_music.md` and the modal-bass-and-melody guidance in `knowledge_base/midi/patterns/bass_and_808_pattern_programming.md`'s modal jazz citations) rather than the harder-edged functional resolution bebop's tertian stacks are built to deliver.

## Quartal Harmony's Relationship to the Genre's Broader Restraint

`modal_jazz.md`'s bass-programming guidance documents a parallel restraint at the bass level: "sparse pedal tones and slow-moving root motion under the mode; avoid walking-bass-every-beat patterns typical of swing/bebop." Quartal comping voicings and this sparse, slow-moving bass approach work together as complementary expressions of the same underlying modal-jazz principle — reduce harmonic density and movement at every level (chord construction, bass motion, overall harmonic rhythm) to let the mode's own scalar color, rather than functional chord-to-chord motion, carry the music's harmonic interest.

## Common Mistakes

**Using quartal voicings in a bebop or functionally-driven harmonic context expecting the same resolution clarity as tertian chords.** Quartal harmony's ambiguity is the point in a modal context — using it where a clear ii-V-I resolution is actually needed (per `knowledge_base/music_theory/harmony/cadences_and_functional_resolution.md`) works against the harmonic clarity that context calls for.

**Building quartal voicings but keeping bebop's dense, walking-every-beat bass approach underneath them.** `modal_jazz.md`'s guidance shows quartal comping and sparse modal bass are meant to reinforce each other — pairing an open quartal voicing with a busy, harmonically active bass line undercuts the open, spacious character quartal harmony is chosen for.
