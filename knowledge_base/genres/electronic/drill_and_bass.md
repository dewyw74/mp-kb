## History

Drill and bass (also written "drill 'n' bass") developed in the mid-1990s as IDM artists began applying software-enabled, extremely detailed breakbeat editing to jungle and drum and bass's rhythmic vocabulary, producing frenzied, irregular beats that discouraged rather than invited dancing — a direct escalation of jungle's already-intricate breakbeat science pushed past danceable limits by the era's newly capable audio software. Aphex Twin's Hangable Auto Bulb EPs (1995), released under his AFX alias, and Squarepusher's Conumber EP (1995) are the style's pioneering releases. In 1996 the approach appeared in long-form album statements: Squarepusher's Feed Me Weird Things fused the extreme editing technique with his own virtuosic, jazz-fusion-influenced electric bass playing, while Aphex Twin continued developing the sound across the Richard D. James Album (1996), Come to Daddy (1997), and later Drukqs (2001). By the end of the 1990s the drill and bass label had largely dissipated as a distinct scene, though later artists such as Kid606 continued drawing on its techniques, and its extreme, jungle-derived editing approach fed directly into the development of breakcore, which took a more overtly frenetic, maximalist approach to the same jungle-breakbeat source material.

## Music Theory

Drill and bass spans a wide harmonic range within its rhythmic extremity: Aphex Twin's contributions often carry simple, sometimes melancholic melodic themes over chaotic drum programming, while Squarepusher's work draws directly on jazz-fusion harmonic vocabulary — chromatic, bebop-influenced bass lines and chord ideas — reflecting his background as a bassist. In both cases, a genuine compositional idea (melody, harmony, or a distinctive bass part) persists as the throughline beneath the extreme rhythmic deconstruction, distinguishing the genre's respected work from unmotivated chaos.

## Arrangement

Tracks typically establish a recognizable breakbeat (frequently Amen-break-derived) before progressively chopping it into smaller, re-triggered, re-pitched fragments, with intensity building through increasing edit density and unpredictability rather than a conventional filter/riser build. There is no danceable "drop" in the drum and bass sense — the genre frequently pushes rhythmic complexity well past the point of danceability, treating that extremity itself as the structural payoff — and pieces often end abruptly or dissolve the breakbeat into pure texture rather than resolving smoothly.

## Instruments

The genre's true defining instrument is the software sampler used for extreme breakbeat manipulation — chopping sampled breaks (especially the Amen break and other jungle/hardcore sources) and re-triggering them with a speed and density well beyond what hardware samplers of the earlier jungle era, or a live drummer, could achieve. Squarepusher's incorporation of live electric bass guitar, played with jazz-fusion technique (slap, fretless glissando) and then processed and rhythmically interlocked with the chopped drum pattern, is the genre's other major instrumental signature, distinguishing his catalogue from Aphex Twin's more purely synthesizer/sampler-based approach.

## Sound Design

Sound design centers on software-sequencer-driven breakbeat chopping — a technological capability that directly enabled the genre's extremity, since the precision and speed required exceeded what earlier hardware samplers or manual tape/vinyl editing could produce. Extreme, rapid pitch-shifting of individual drum hits is a core compositional technique here, more aggressively and frequently applied than in jungle's already-established practice of pitch-shifting breaks, and re-pitched/time-stretched break fragments are often used to generate pitched, quasi-melodic drum content.

## MIDI Programming

Programming (or, for Squarepusher's bass parts, live performance later integrated with programmed elements) prioritizes deliberately inhuman rhythmic complexity on drums — rapid snare rushes and rolls evoking a drill or jackhammer, with micro-timing manipulation at the individual-transient level well beyond human or DJ-performable limits. Melodic and bass material, by contrast, often carries more conventional musical intent and expressive dynamics, providing a compositional anchor beneath the extreme drum programming.

## Mixing

Because drill and bass arrangements are extremely dense and rapidly changing at the transient level, mixing requires detailed, surgical EQ to keep chopped drum-hit content distinct from bass and melodic material competing for the same attention. Compression is applied more aggressively to the drum bus for punch and density, while bass — especially Squarepusher's live playing — is often kept more dynamically expressive; reverb is used sparingly, reserved for dramatic moments rather than pervasive atmosphere, so the frantic drum programming stays clear and upfront.

## Mastering

Masters target a moderate -11 to -9 LUFS integrated, preserving enough dynamic range for the extreme transient detail of chopped breakbeats to read clearly rather than collapsing into an undifferentiated wall. Frequency balance is full-spectrum, with present sub-bass weight (particularly where live bass guitar features prominently) balanced against the dense, bright transient detail of the drum programming.

## Reference Artists

Aphex Twin, working under his AFX alias, pioneered the style with the Hangable Auto Bulb EPs (1995) and continued exploring it across the Richard D. James Album, Come to Daddy, and Drukqs. Squarepusher (Tom Jenkinson) is the genre's other essential figure, distinguished by his virtuosic, jazz-fusion-influenced electric bass playing integrated directly with extreme breakbeat editing, exemplified on Feed Me Weird Things (1996) and Big Loada (1997, featuring "Come On My Selector").

## Production Notes

The defining discipline of drill and bass production is pushing software-enabled breakbeat editing well past what jungle and drum and bass consider danceable, using extreme micro-timing manipulation and rapid pitch-shifting of individual drum hits as core compositional techniques rather than occasional effects. If incorporating bass as a lead voice, draw on jazz-fusion vocabulary in the Squarepusher tradition rather than a conventional electronic bassline. It is essential to distinguish drill and bass from mainstream drum and bass (which retains a danceable, DJ-mixable groove that drill and bass deliberately abandons) and from IDM broadly (drill and bass is a specific, jungle/breakbeat-rooted IDM subgenre, not a synonym for IDM's much wider stylistic range). The most common failure mode is randomizing drum edits without underlying rhythmic or compositional logic, producing noise rather than the deliberately controlled chaos that defines the genre's most respected work. Modern DAW slice-based sampling tools and generative/randomized MIDI approaches make the genre's extreme editing techniques far more accessible than the original 1990s hardware/early-software workflow allowed.
