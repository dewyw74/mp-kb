## History

Depressive suicidal black metal (DSBM) emerged in the late 1990s as a subgenre of black metal distinguished by its intense focus on depression, despair, suicide, and misanthropy rather than the Satanic or occult themes of traditional black metal. Shining vocalist Niklas Kvarforth coined the term "suicidal black metal" around this period, with pioneering acts including Shining, Silencer, and Bethlehem helping define the style's sound and thematic direction. The genre's most influential figures emerged as one-man projects in the early 2000s: American artists Xasthur (Scott Conner, formed 1995) and Leviathan (Jef "Wrest" Whitehead, formed late 1990s) developed atmospheric, lo-fi, deeply personal approaches, while Denmark's Nortt fused the style with funeral doom's crushing slowness.

## Music Theory

Melody is deliberately minimal, repetitive, and bleak rather than developed — raw, lo-fi tremolo riffing built on Aeolian and Phrygian minor-key material loops with little variation, designed to induce a numbing, hopeless trance rather than provide musical interest. Shrieked, often barely audible vocals function as an expression of raw anguish rather than a lead melodic voice, and in Nortt's work black metal tremolo riffing is combined with funeral doom's extreme harmonic deceleration for an even bleaker effect.

## Arrangement

Songs typically open with minimal, raw guitar texture or lo-fi ambient noise before settling into long, repetitive tremolo-picked sections that loop with minimal development, deliberately inducing numbness rather than building toward a hook. Slow, doom-influenced passages — most pronounced in Nortt's funeral-doom-adjacent output — provide the genre's most significant structural contrast, replacing conventional black metal blast-beat aggression with crawling, dirge-like weight, and songs frequently fade out or cut off abruptly with no cathartic resolution.

## Instruments

Raw, thin, heavily tremolo-picked guitar recorded with minimal fidelity is the genre's primary instrumental voice, a direct inheritance from Second Wave black metal's necro aesthetic that amplifies the sense of isolation and despair. The genre is overwhelmingly a one-man-band phenomenon — Xasthur, Leviathan, and Nortt are each solo projects — with bass typically minimal or absent and drumming ranging from raw blast beats to Nortt's very slow, funeral-doom-influenced dirges.

## Sound Design

Heavy, often muddy reverb and distortion are used to obscure rather than clarify the performance, with deliberate lo-fi murk treated as an emotional device rather than a technical limitation to be corrected. Intentional low-fidelity mixing choices — harsh clipping, buried vocals, minimal instrument separation — intensify the genre's bleak, claustrophobic atmosphere.

## MIDI Programming

Program bass minimally or omit it largely, consistent with the genre's thin sonic signature, and write raw, simple blast beats or very slow dirge-like patterns depending on whether the track leans toward Xasthur/Leviathan's rawer approach or Nortt's funeral-doom-influenced style. Melodic material should stay deliberately minimal and repetitive, looping with little development to induce numbness rather than melodic interest, with velocity kept low-to-moderate and largely monotone across the track.

## Mixing

EQ is thin, harsh, and deliberately unbalanced — bass minimized or absent, mids often muddy — favoring bleak rawness over any conventional "good" mix. Heavy, murky reverb on vocals and guitars alike buries rather than showcases the performance, and stereo imaging often leans narrow or mono, contributing to the genre's claustrophobic, isolated character rather than the wide, immersive spread used in atmospheric black metal.

## Mastering

Masters typically sit at -14 to -10 LUFS integrated and are frequently left intentionally under-mastered and raw, with little conventional dynamic shaping — inconsistent levels are treated as authentic rather than corrected. Frequency balance stays thin and treble/mid-skewed with minimal low end, deliberately avoiding the fuller balance of atmospheric black metal or the polish of modern metal mastering.

## Reference Artists

Xasthur's "Subliminal Genocide" (2006) and Leviathan's "Tentacles of Whorror" (2004) define the genre's lo-fi, atmospheric, one-man-project sound, while Nortt's "Gudsforladt" (2003) established its funeral-doom-influenced, extremely slow variant. Shining's Niklas Kvarforth coined the "suicidal black metal" term that gave the genre its name.

## Production Notes

Use repetition and deliberate under-production as the genre's central emotional tools rather than trying to clean up the sound, since raw murk is the point rather than a flaw to fix; keep arrangements minimal and repetitive by design, as the despairing effect comes from monotony and stasis rather than musical development. Do not confuse this genre with atmospheric black metal — DSBM's focus is intensely personal despair, suicide, and self-harm rather than atmospheric black metal's nature-centered, often more expansive and less claustrophobic scope — and avoid over-polishing the mix or writing developed, hook-driven riffs, both of which work against the genre's core aesthetic of instability and bleakness.
