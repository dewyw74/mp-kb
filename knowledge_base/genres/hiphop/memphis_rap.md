## History

Memphis rap originated in Memphis, Tennessee, during the mid-1980s, characterized by lo-fi, DIY production featuring heavy 808 bass, cowbell rhythms, triplet hi-hat patterns, dark minor-key melodies, and themes of horrorcore, gothic narrative, and the underground economy. Three 6 Mafia, formed in 1991, emerged as a horror-themed underground hip-hop group before achieving mainstream success, shifting to their eventual name in 1995 and releasing their debut album "Mystic Stylez" on Prophet, earning widespread recognition locally and across the Southern underground hip-hop scene with an intense, lyrically and musically dark "horrorcore" style built on synth-driven melodies and crunchy drum machine sounds (notably the BOSS DR-5) that quickly set them apart from other Dirty South gangsta rappers. The group's independent Memphis label Hypnotize Minds, launched in the '90s, released many of the scene's essential recordings, and the genre's raw, minimal, cowbell-and-808-driven sound would later become the direct foundation for phonk's 2010s global revival.

## Music Theory

Memphis rap harmony is deliberately minimal — often just one or two dark, minor-key chords or a simple descending synth line — prioritizing atmosphere and menace over harmonic development, with sample-derived progressions from horror film scores and classical music sources contributing gothic, unsettling color. This harmonic sparseness reflects both the genre's low-budget production origins and its thematic commitment to dark, horrorcore-adjacent atmosphere.

## Arrangement

Tracks establish mood through a dark, minimal synth loop combined with the genre's signature cowbell pattern before verses deliver double-time or triplet flows over heavy 808 bass with a distinctly lo-fi, DIY sonic character. The genre favors sustained, hypnotic repetition of the established dark loop over dynamic escalation, with hooks frequently built from a repetitive, chanted phrase functioning as the track's structural and memorable center.

## Instruments

TR-808-style drum programming with cowbell rhythms and rapid triplet hi-hat patterns forms the genre's signature, immediately recognizable rhythmic foundation, combined with heavy 808 sub-bass and minimal, dark, often eerie synth melody — sometimes sampled directly from horror film scores or classical music. This lo-fi, DIY, drum-machine-and-synth-based production approach, using accessible equipment like the Roland TR-808 and BOSS DR-5, defines the genre's distinctively raw, low-budget sonic character.

## Sound Design

Minimal, dark, eerie synth melody combined with 808-style drum machine synthesis and deliberately minimal filtering define the genre's raw, unpolished sonic identity — a defining aesthetic choice rather than a technical limitation. Sample chopping from horror film scores and classical music sources contributes to the genre's distinctively gothic, atmospheric color.

## MIDI Programming

Program heavy 808 sub-bass and TR-808-style cowbell-and-triplet-hi-hat drum patterns as the genre's essential, immediately recognizable rhythmic signature, writing minimal, dark synth melody lines to support double-time or triplet vocal flows. Deliberately raw and lo-fi programming feel is genre-appropriate — this low-budget, DIY sonic character is an intentional aesthetic that should be preserved, not polished away.

## Mixing

EQ should stay dark and bass-heavy, emphasizing the 808 low end and cowbell/hi-hat presence, with a deliberately raw, unpolished overall character reflecting the genre's low-budget production origins. Reverb and delay should stay minimal, contributing to the genre's tight, direct, lo-fi sonic identity.

## Mastering

Modern streaming or reissue masters typically target -10 to -8 LUFS integrated, though original era recordings sit rawer and less polished, with a frequency balance dominated by heavy low end from the 808 and a dark, minimal midrange.

## Reference Artists

Three 6 Mafia's "Tear da Club Up" and "Late Nite Tip" define the genre's foundational Hypnotize Minds-era sound; Tommy Wright III's "Killa's Klub" and Playa Fly's "Triple Six Clubhouse" showcase the genre's broader underground Memphis scene beyond Three 6 Mafia's mainstream crossover.

## Production Notes

Embrace the genre's lo-fi, DIY, deliberately low-budget sonic character as an authentic aesthetic choice, and use the cowbell-and-triplet-hi-hat drum pattern as the genre's essential rhythmic signature. Draw on dark, horror-film-adjacent atmosphere for synth and sample content, and avoid over-polishing the production or confusing this original 1990s sound with its later, more club-oriented drift phonk descendant.
