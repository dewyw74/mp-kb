# Horror and Tension Drone Design

This entry covers the dissonant, unsettling sustained textures used in horror and tension film/game scoring — the sound-design-specific application of `knowledge_base/sound_design/presets/drone_and_texture_design.md`'s general sustained-source techniques, narrowed to the specific interval/timbre choices that produce dread and unease rather than neutral or meditative atmosphere.

## Building the Sound

1. **Non-functional harmony — tone clusters over chords**: rather than any conventional chord voicing, stack pitches at dissonant intervals — minor 2nds, tritones, major 7ths — with no resolving cadence. `knowledge_base/genres/cinematic/horror_score.md` specifies this directly: "non-functional — tone clusters and dissonant intervals... replace conventional chord progressions."
2. **Detuned oscillator stacks for beating dissonance**: layering several oscillators tuned closely but not identically (much narrower than a supersaw's shimmer-width detune) produces audible, unsettling beating between voices — a direct synth translation of orchestral tone-cluster writing.
3. **Chromatic, whole-tone, or microtonal source material**: `horror_score.md` names chromatic scale, tone clusters, and whole-tone scale as the genre's common scale choices — deliberately avoiding the tonal center a diatonic scale would imply.
4. **Prepared/processed acoustic sources**: prepared piano (objects placed on or between strings) is a recurring horror-scoring technique for unsettling, non-piano-like timbres, alongside extended string technique (sul ponticello, harmonics) for orchestral sources.
5. **Infrasound layer**: very low frequency content below conscious hearing threshold is a well-documented horror sound-design technique for inducing physical unease without the listener perceiving an identifiable "sound" causing it.
6. **Contrastive space**: alternating extreme reverb and extreme dryness (rather than one consistent space) is used deliberately for disorientation, and silence itself is used as a structural device immediately before a stinger — the absence of sound builds as much tension as any sustained texture.

## Genre Grounding

- `knowledge_base/genres/cinematic/horror_score.md` is the primary source: "Non-functional — tone clusters and dissonant intervals (minor 2nd, tritone, major 7th) replace conventional chord progressions," "prepared piano... a recurring horror-scoring technique," "detuned, dissonant stacks for cluster-like synth textures echoing orchestral tone clusters," "extreme reverb or extreme dryness used contrastively for disorientation," and "infrasound/very low frequency content (below conscious hearing) used to induce unease." Bernard Herrmann's Psycho score and Krzysztof Penderecki/György Ligeti's avant-garde concert-hall dissonance (used by Kubrick in The Shining) are cited as the genre's foundational reference points.
- `knowledge_base/genres/electronic/dark_ambient.md` documents "static tone clusters, no functional progression" and "microtonal/atonal clusters" as its own harmonic approach, directly overlapping with horror scoring's technique set, plus "extreme detuning between voices for beating/dissonance" and "resonant low-pass driven near self-oscillation for eerie whines."
- `knowledge_base/genres/cinematic/war_film_score.md` documents tension material favoring "extended low and high extremes (sub-bass drones, high-register string sustains) with a comparatively sparse midrange, similar in spirit to horror scoring's frequency approach but without horror's dissonant intent" — a useful contrast showing the same frequency-extremes technique used for solemnity rather than dread when the dissonant interval content is removed.
- `knowledge_base/genres/cinematic/noir_score.md` explicitly distinguishes itself from this technique: "confusing noir scoring with horror scoring's atonal dissonance — noir relies on tonal, chromatically colored jazz/orchestral harmony... rather than horror's non-functional tone clusters," a useful negative-space confirmation of what makes horror scoring's approach distinct.
- `knowledge_base/genres/cinematic/giallo_score.md` documents its "stalking"/murder cues breaking from an established tonal groove "into sudden avant-garde dissonance — dissonant synth clusters, prepared-piano-like prog keyboard textures" — the same tone-cluster/prepared-piano vocabulary deployed as a sudden intrusion into an otherwise tonal, groove-driven score rather than as the sustained default texture.

## Common Mistakes

**Relying on melody and functional harmony instead of texture and dissonance.** Per `horror_score.md`'s own explicit warning, horror scoring "is fundamentally texture-first, not tune-first" — a well-written melodic theme, however dark in key, does not substitute for genuine non-functional dissonance.

**Confusing horror scoring's atonal dissonance with noir scoring's tonal, chromatically-colored jazz harmony.** These produce superficially similar "dark" moods but through opposite harmonic mechanisms — noir stays tonal and cadences functionally; horror does not.
