## History

Trap emerged in late-1990s/2000s Atlanta as an evolution of Southern hip-hop, taking its name from the "trap house" (a house used for drug dealing) that dominated the genre's early lyrical themes, with T.I. and Gucci Mane among its foundational artists. The genre's 808-and-hi-hat-roll production template, refined through the 2000s and 2010s by producers like Lex Luger, Mike WiLL Made-It, and Metro Boomin, became the dominant sound of mainstream hip-hop by the 2010s and has since spread its influence deeply into pop, R&B, and EDM production worldwide.

## Music Theory

Trap harmony favors dark, simple, minor-key melodic loops (often just a 2-4 note phrase) rather than functional chord progressions, using harmonic minor color for a moody, dramatic edge. Melodic material stays deliberately minimal, leaving maximal rhythmic space for the interplay between the 808 bass, rolling hi-hats, and the rapper's flow — the genre's real musical sophistication lives in that rhythmic relationship rather than harmonic development.

## Arrangement

Trap song structure follows a verse-hook form, often with a distinctive "beat switch" — a new instrumental section within the same track — used as a structural device to refresh energy on longer songs (a technique popularized by Travis Scott's "SICKO MODE"). Builds are minimal, typically just an increase in hi-hat roll density or a brief filter movement signaling an approaching hook.

## Instruments

The 808 drum machine (or its modern software emulation) is the genre's defining instrument, providing both the deep, melodically-tuned sub bass and the sharp snare/clap, with rapid 16th/32nd-note hi-hat rolls forming the genre's signature rhythmic texture. Dark, often orchestral-adjacent synth leads, bells, and choir-like pads create the moody atmospheric character trap is known for.

## Sound Design

808 pitch-gliding between bass notes is trap's most recognizable sound-design signature, paired with dark wavetable/FM synthesis for plucks and leads and sampled orchestral textures (strings, choir, bells) for dramatic emphasis. Hi-hat roll subdivision changes (straight 16ths shifting to triplets or 32nd notes) function as the genre's primary rhythmic "modulation" device.

## MIDI Programming

808 bass patterns are melodically active and slide between notes, syncopating tightly against a sparse, syncopated kick/snare pattern rather than following a fixed grid. Hi-hats carry deliberate, extensive velocity variation to create the genre's characteristic rolling groove even at very high subdivision speeds, while 808 and hi-hat programming generally stays tightly quantized for a driving, mechanically precise feel.

## Mixing

Mixing prioritizes a deep, controlled 808 low end alongside a vocal boosted for presence in the 2-5kHz range to cut through dense hi-hat activity, with heavy compression/saturation on both 808s and vocals for modern loudness and punch. Sidechain compression manages potential low-end collisions between 808 and kick, while hi-hats, ad-libs, and melodic layers spread wide in the stereo field for an immersive, atmospheric image.

## Mastering

Masters typically target -8 to -6 LUFS integrated for streaming and club competitiveness, balancing a deep, heavy low end against a bright, present vocal and hi-hat top end.

## Reference Artists

T.I.'s "Rubber Band Man" and Gucci Mane's early catalog established trap's foundational lyrical and sonic template; Future's "Mask Off" showcases the genre's melodic, atmospheric mainstream evolution; Travis Scott's "SICKO MODE" (produced with input from multiple hitmakers) exemplifies the beat-switch structural technique; Migos' "Bad and Boujee" (produced by Metro Boomin) represents trap's triplet-flow and dark-melodic mainstream peak.

## Production Notes

Build the beat around the 808-and-hi-hat relationship first, treating melody as secondary atmosphere rather than the main compositional focus. Use hi-hat roll variation as the primary source of rhythmic interest and groove, and consider a mid-track beat switch as a structural tool to refresh energy on longer songs. Avoid under-tuning the 808 to the track's key (causing dissonance) and neglecting sidechain/EQ management between 808 and kick, which results in a muddy, masked low end.
