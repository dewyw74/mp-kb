## History

Nu jazz emerged in the early 1990s as electronic music artists began incorporating jazz elements — improvisation, complex harmonies, samples of old records, various textures — into their production across the world in the early advent of electronic music production software, with musicians from both jazz and electronic dance music backgrounds implementing their respective expertise to create jazz compositions suited to electronic dance club and neo-lounge settings. French producer Ludovic Navarre, recording as St. Germain, combined funky, hypnotic grooves with instrumental licks from traditional jazz and blues, while Norwegian collective Jaga Jazzist merged electronic soundscapes with acoustic instruments including guitar, bass, vibraphone, and brass/woodwind, integrating post-rock elements with jazz improvisation and electronic texture on albums like "A Living Room Hush" (2001) to create expansive, orchestral soundscapes blending live instrumentation with experimental production.

## Music Theory

Nu jazz harmony draws on genuine jazz-derived extended harmony — ii-V-I progressions, modal vamps, extended chord tensions — programmed or performed with a steady, electronic-production-influenced groove rather than traditional jazz's more free-flowing swing feel. This combination of sophisticated jazz harmonic vocabulary with house and downtempo's more repetitive, groove-centered structural approach is the genre's defining musical characteristic.

## Arrangement

Tracks typically establish a steady, electronic-production-driven groove before jazz instrumental themes enter and develop over it, with acoustic and electronic elements blended throughout rather than alternating between distinct sections. The genre's climactic moments typically combine dense electronic texture with expressive jazz instrumental improvisation, reflecting genuine fusion rather than a maximalist EDM-style drop structure.

## Instruments

A genuine hybrid of traditional acoustic jazz instrumentation — saxophones, trumpets, double bass — with electronic elements including synthesizers and drum machines defines the genre's core instrumental approach, venturing considerably further into electronic territory than the closely related acid jazz. Programmed electronic drum machine patterns, rather than more organically played grooves, are a key distinguishing rhythmic element setting nu jazz apart from acid jazz.

## Sound Design

Wide-ranging electronic synthesis, spanning warm analog-style pads to more digital IDM-influenced textures, blends directly with acoustic jazz instrumentation, with electronic filter sweeps and modulation borrowed directly from house and downtempo production. The genre's compositional process typically blends live jazz performance or samples with electronic production software from the earliest creative stages.

## MIDI Programming

Program house, downtempo, or IDM-influenced electronic drum patterns as the genre's rhythmic foundation, writing genuine jazz instrumental themes integrated directly with electronic texture and modulation. Precisely programmed electronic elements should be blended with genuinely expressive, human jazz instrumental performance — this contrast is central to the genre's distinctive character.

## Mixing

EQ should integrate acoustic jazz instrumentation and electronic production elements as equal partners rather than treating one as foreground and the other as background, with moderate-to-heavy compression on electronic elements and lighter treatment preserving acoustic instrumentation's natural dynamics. Electronic, often rhythmically synced delay is a common genre production technique.

## Mastering

Masters typically target -9 to -7 LUFS integrated for streaming/club release, balancing electronic production loudness standards with jazz instrumental expressiveness, with a full-spectrum frequency balance integrating warm acoustic instrumentation and clear electronic texture.

## Reference Artists

St. Germain's "Rose Rouge" represents the genre's house-influenced French lineage; Jaga Jazzist's "Animal Chin" showcases the Norwegian post-rock-jazz-electronic fusion approach, while Nils Petter Molvær's "Khmer" and The Cinematic Orchestra's "Channel 1 Suite" demonstrate the genre's atmospheric, downtempo-adjacent branches.

## Production Notes

Treat acoustic jazz instrumentation and electronic production elements as genuinely equal partners throughout the arrangement, emphasizing steady, groove-centered rhythm beneath sophisticated jazz harmony. Venture further into electronic territory than acid jazz deliberately, and avoid treating electronic elements as mere backing or confusing nu jazz's more electronically integrated approach with acid jazz's more organic, live-band-oriented groove.
