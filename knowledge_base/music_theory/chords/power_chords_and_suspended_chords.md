# Power Chords and Suspended Chords: Harmonic Ambiguity as a Weight and Texture Device

Power chords (root and fifth, with the third omitted entirely) and suspended chords (a chord's third replaced by a 2nd or 4th) share a structural trait that shows up as a deliberate genre-level device across this knowledge base's rock and electronic genre files: both remove or replace the third, the interval that normally decides whether a chord reads as major or minor. Genres reach for that ambiguity for very different reasons — raw weight in metal and hard rock, dreamy unresolve in indie rock and chillwave.

## Power Chords: Weight and Harmonic Ambiguity as Heaviness

`metal.md` names the ambiguity directly as a functional feature, not an incidental simplification: "power chords — root-fifth voicings that emphasize weight and ambiguity (neither major nor minor) over full triadic harmony." The absence of the third isn't a limitation the genre works around; it's specifically what lets a riff read as harmonically weighty and tonally open rather than committing to a brighter major or more melodically "resolved" minor color. `hard_rock.md` documents the same voicing with an added-octave variant: "power chords (root-fifth, often with the octave), palm-muted riffing" — the octave doubling adds fullness without reintroducing the third's major/minor commitment. `grunge.md` and `pop_punk.md` both use power chords as a dynamic-contrast device specifically: `grunge.md`'s riffs built "from power chords or pentatonic fragments," and `pop_punk.md`'s "palm-muted verses opening into ringing, full power chords at the chorus as the primary dynamic device — it's simple but highly effective." In both cases, the chord type itself (root-fifth, no third) is constant, but its dynamic treatment (muted vs. ringing) is what carries the song's energy arc.

## Suspended and Added-Tone Chords: Unresolved, Hazy Color

`indie_rock.md` documents sus chords for a specifically unresolved, open texture: "Modal/open-tuning-derived vamps with added second/suspended chords for a jangly, unresolved color." `chillwave.md` extends the same principle to a softer, more atmospheric genre: "Extended/added-tone chords (add9, sus2) for a hazy, soft-focus harmonic color." In both cases, the sus chord's missing (or reassigned) third produces a chord that sounds like it's waiting to resolve to something more definite — a state of harmonic suspension that both files treat as a desirable end state in itself rather than a transitional chord that needs to resolve to a triad.

## The Common Mechanism: Removing the Major/Minor-Deciding Interval

Power chords omit the third entirely; sus2/sus4 chords replace it with a different scale degree. Both moves remove the single interval responsible for a triad's major/minor identity, and every genre citation above reaches for that same removal for a genre-appropriate reason: metal and hard rock want the resulting ambiguity to read as weight and darkness; indie rock and chillwave want it to read as openness and hazy unresolve. The same harmonic mechanism produces opposite emotional readings depending entirely on the surrounding genre context (tempo, distortion, arrangement) it's placed in.

## Common Mistakes

**Adding a third back into a power-chord voicing "for fullness."** This defeats the entire purpose of the voicing — `metal.md`'s ambiguity is a feature the third's absence creates, not a gap needing to be filled.

**Treating sus chords as chords that must resolve.** `indie_rock.md` and `chillwave.md` both use suspended/added-tone chords as a stable harmonic color to sit in, not as suspensions in the strict classical voice-leading sense that demand resolution to a following triad.
