## History

Minimal synth grew directly out of the same late-1970s/early-1980s DIY moment as coldwave and early industrial, but pushed the austerity further: where coldwave still had full bands and studio access, minimal synth was frequently a solo bedroom project built around one or two budget monosynths and a cheap drum machine, self-released on cassette or tiny private-press vinyl runs of a few hundred copies. Suicide (Alan Vega and Martin Rev) is the essential American precursor, proving as early as 1977 that a synthesizer and a drum machine alone could carry a full, emotionally intense song. The genre remained almost entirely underground in its own era — most of it undocumented outside tape-trading networks — until 2000s-2010s reissue labels (Dark Entries, Minimal Wave, Vinyl-on-Demand) excavated the catalog, cementing "minimal synth" as a retroactively-named genre and fueling a substantial modern DIY revival scene.

## Music Theory

Harmonic movement is intentionally minimal, often reduced to a single sustained chord or a two-chord oscillation (i-VII being common) under a melody built from a tiny handful of repeated notes. Where coldwave still develops recognizable verse-chorus harmonic contrast, minimal synth frequently abandons functional harmony altogether in favor of a static modal drone, letting rhythm, timbre, and repetition carry the entire expressive weight of the piece.

## Arrangement

Songs rarely exceed three or four simultaneous elements — drum machine, one bassline, one synth melody or arpeggio, and vocal — and arrangement change across a song's duration is minimal by design. There's essentially no "build" in the pop-arrangement sense; a track's emotional intensity comes from sustained repetition rather than added layers or dynamic lift, and refrains distinguish themselves through vocal emphasis or subtle rhythmic accent rather than new instrumentation.

## Instruments

The genre's sonic identity is inseparable from its budget gear: Korg MS-10/MS-20, Roland SH-09, Wasp, and similar single- or dual-oscillator monosynths for melody and bass, paired with cheap drum machines like the Korg Mini Pops, Roland CR-8000/TR-606, or Boss DR-55. Guitar is essentially absent, and the total instrument count per track is often just two or three voices — a hard budget and technical constraint that became the genre's defining aesthetic choice.

## Sound Design

Patches are deliberately thin: single-oscillator sawtooth or square waves through a basic low-pass filter with static or minimally-swept cutoff, little to no detuning, and sparse modulation reflecting the limited routing options of the source synths. This rawness — rather than the lush, chorused polish of synth pop — is the genre's defining sonic signature, and reproducing it means resisting the urge to thicken or modernize the patches.

## MIDI Programming

Bass and melody parts are extremely repetitive one-to-four-note cells, quantized rigidly with essentially no swing or humanization, reflecting both the mechanical nature of the source drum machines and the genre's hypnotic, trance-inducing aesthetic. Melodic development across a song, when it occurs at all, happens glacially — a single pitch change introduced only after many bars of repetition.

## Mixing

Mixes should stay raw and largely unprocessed: minimal EQ shaping, little to no bus compression, small boxy spring reverb instead of lush halls, and a narrow, near-mono stereo image consistent with the genre's 4-track cassette recording origins. Resisting modern full-spectrum polish is essential to preserving authenticity.

## Mastering

Target a noticeably lower loudness and looser dynamic range than synth pop or new wave masters (roughly -14 to -11 LUFS integrated), avoiding heavy limiting that would erase the genre's inherently raw, lo-fi character. Frequency balance should stay thin and mid-forward rather than full and airy.

## Reference Artists

Suicide is the foundational reference for the entire genre; The Klinik, Pink Industry, early Kirlian Camera, Das Ding, and Vice Versa represent the core minimal synth canon uncovered by 2000s-2010s reissue culture. "Ghost Rider" and "Frankie Teardrop" by Suicide remain essential studies in how far a two-instrument arrangement can be pushed for sustained hypnotic and emotional intensity.

## Production Notes

The discipline required is almost the inverse of most pop production: fewer elements, less processing, less dynamic movement, and a comfort with raw, budget-gear timbres rather than lush, modern polish. Modern producers chasing this sound should use single-oscillator synth patches, keep the instrument count to three or four voices, quantize rigidly without humanization, and resist adding conventional arrangement dynamics — the hypnotic power of minimal synth comes precisely from what it refuses to add.
