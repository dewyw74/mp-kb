## History

Berlin School, also known as Kosmische Musik ("cosmic music"), emerged in the early-to-mid 1970s among West Berlin-based musicians as an offshoot of krautrock that eschewed rock instrumentation entirely in favor of cutting-edge, synthesizer-based composition. The term "Kosmische Musik" was coined in 1971 by Tangerine Dream's Edgar Froese and later adopted by producer Rolf-Ulrich Kaiser as a marketing label for acts including Ash Ra Tempel, Tangerine Dream, and Klaus Schulze; Tangerine Dream's Alpha Centauri (1971) is the record that prompted the term's coining. The genre's defining technical innovation arrived with Tangerine Dream's Phaedra (1974), on which Christopher Franke introduced electronic sequencers to the group's sound — a development with lasting impact on electronic music broadly. Klaus Schulze, who had passed through Tangerine Dream, Ash Ra Tempel, and the Cosmic Jokers before launching a solo career spanning more than 60 albums, released the sequencer-driven Timewind (1975), dedicated to Richard Wagner, which earned the Grand Prix du Disque International from L'Académie Charles Cros in 1976. By the mid-1970s, albums including Phaedra, Rubycon, Timewind, and Moondawn had defined the idiom's signature sound: interlocking Moog sequences, Mellotron choirs and strings, and vast reverbs.

## Music Theory

Berlin School favors minimal harmonic movement anchored by pedal points rather than conventional chord progressions, built on extended forms that prioritize gradual evolution over verse-chorus songwriting structure. Modal or scalar lead lines weave over pulsing, repeating arpeggiated sequences, with the sequencer pattern itself functioning as the primary musical material — evolving gradually across extended durations rather than serving as mere accompaniment to a separate melody.

## Arrangement

Pieces typically open with an extended atmospheric passage — synthesizer drones, Mellotron textures, or sound effects — before a repeating sequencer arpeggio establishes the rhythmic and harmonic foundation. Rather than a conventional song-form dynamic build, the composition evolves gradually over many minutes through added layers and timbral shifts, with peak intensity emerging from accumulated density rather than a repeated melodic hook, before a gradual reduction back to atmospheric texture closes the piece.

## Instruments

Analog modular synthesizers and step-sequencers are the genre's foundational tools, with compact lead keyboards and analog drum machines added as the technology evolved through the 1970s. Notably, the genre eschews rock instrumentation entirely — no guitars, and often no conventional drums in early work — relying instead on the sequencer's pulse for rhythmic drive, while Mellotron-simulated choir, flute, and string textures provide atmospheric, orchestral-adjacent coloration without real orchestration.

## Sound Design

The genre's defining sound-design technique involves playing a sequence slower than it actually sounds, with the rhythmic pulse filled in by tape delay set to a mathematical division of the tempo — a distinctly Berlin School approach to generating rhythmic complexity from a slow-moving source pattern. Vast reverbs create the genre's spacious, otherworldly atmosphere, and slow filter sweeps on sustained pads and sequencer layers provide gradual timbral evolution across extended durations.

## MIDI Programming

Program the sequencer's lower-register pattern as both rhythmic and harmonic foundation, evolving gradually over extended time. Drum programming should stay minimal or absent in early-style compositions, supporting the sequencer's pulse rather than becoming a foreground rhythmic element. Lead lines should be modal or scalar, weaving over the arpeggiated sequence with a priority on atmosphere and gradual motion, and humanization should stay minimal on the sequencer pattern itself while allowing more expressive movement on lead and textural elements.

## Mixing

Balance the mix warmly and spaciously to support the genre's analog synthesizer character, giving sequencer patterns clear presence without overwhelming atmospheric pad and Mellotron layers. Reverb should be vast and spacious — a defining genre characteristic central to its cosmic atmosphere — while tape delay functions as a core structural device (not just an effect), filling in rhythmic pulse from a deliberately slow-played sequence. Extensive, slow-moving automation across long timeframes realizes the genre's gradual evolution.

## Mastering

Masters target roughly -16 to -13 LUFS integrated, preserving a wide, natural dynamic range consistent with the genre's meditative, atmosphere-focused character. The frequency balance stays warm and spacious, representing the analog synthesizer and Mellotron textures genuinely without artificial brightness.

## Reference Artists

Tangerine Dream's Phaedra (1974) stands as the genre's most historically pivotal recording, introducing the sequencer-driven approach that would define Berlin School's signature sound. Klaus Schulze's Timewind (1975) and Ash Ra Tempel round out the genre's most influential foundational figures.

## Production Notes

- Build the composition around a repeating, evolving sequencer arpeggio as the primary musical material.
- Use the genre's signature slow-sequence-plus-delay technique to generate rhythmic complexity from a slow-moving source pattern.
- Favor extended forms over conventional song structures, letting the composition evolve gradually over many minutes.
- Layer Mellotron-style choir, flute, or string textures for atmospheric color.
- Avoid adding conventional rock instrumentation or using a fixed, static sequence without gradual evolution — both work against the genre's defining all-electronic, slowly-evolving identity.
- Modern hybrid approach: use analog-modeled modular synth plugins with genuine step-sequencing, and recreate the slow-sequence-plus-delay technique with a precisely tempo-synced delay plugin.
