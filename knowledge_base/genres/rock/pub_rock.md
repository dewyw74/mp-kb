## History

Pub rock emerged in London around 1971-1972 as a direct reaction against the perceived excess, bombast, and studio polish of contemporaneous progressive rock and arena rock, with bands deliberately returning to small pub venues to play direct, energetic, R&B- and rock and roll-rooted music stripped of elaborate production or technical showmanship. Dr. Feelgood, with Wilko Johnson's percussive, choppy guitar style and Lee Brilleaux's raw, urgent vocal delivery, became the genre's most influential and commercially successful act, embodying the scene's stripped-down, high-energy ethos on records like "Roxette" and "Back in the Night." Brinsley Schwarz (featuring a young Nick Lowe), Eddie and the Hot Rods, and later Ian Dury and the Blockheads and Graham Parker and the Rumour further developed the scene's back-to-basics, live-performance-focused sensibility through the mid-1970s. Pub rock is widely and directly credited as a crucial precursor to and enabling infrastructure for UK punk rock — many of punk's earliest London venues, promoters, and even some musicians came directly out of the pub rock circuit, and its rejection of progressive rock's perceived pretension anticipated punk's more explicitly confrontational version of the same impulse.

## Music Theory

Pub rock harmony stays deliberately simple and direct — I-IV-V progressions and 12-bar blues variants dominate, in the tradition of foundational rock and roll and rhythm and blues, with minimal harmonic elaboration or extended chord vocabulary. Vocal melody is direct and unpretentious, delivered with a live, immediate energy reflecting the genre's small-venue origins rather than studio-crafted vocal sophistication. Instrumental melody favors tight, R&B-informed riffing (Wilko Johnson's choppy, percussive guitar style is the genre's clearest reference point) over extended soloing, prioritizing raw impact and rhythmic drive over harmonic or melodic complexity.

## Arrangement

Songs are short and immediate, frequently opening with just a count-in or a couple of bars of riff before the full band commits, reflecting the genre's live, unpretentious, get-to-the-point ethos. Verses stay direct and energetic, built on a tight, groove-locked rhythm section with minimal harmonic or arrangement complexity, and "builds" are essentially absent — pub rock's energy is typically high and consistent throughout a track rather than shaped by a studio-crafted dynamic arc. Bridges are often minimal or entirely absent, and songs frequently end on a cold stop or a quick tag ending, consistent with the genre's tight, no-nonsense, live-set-oriented structure.

## Instruments

The core lineup is guitar, bass, drums, and often piano — a lean, small-venue-appropriate combo explicitly reacting against the elaborate, orchestrated instrumentation of contemporaneous progressive and arena rock. Guitar tone is direct and moderately driven, favoring tight rhythm playing and concise, R&B-informed lead lines (again, Wilko Johnson's style is the genre's touchstone) rather than extended virtuosic soloing. Drums and bass stay tight, energetic, and groove-locked, prioritizing live-band drive over technical complexity, and boogie-woogie or R&B-informed piano appears frequently as a core, energetic ensemble voice in many pub rock bands; synths and orchestration are entirely absent.

## Sound Design

Pub rock's sound design is defined by what it deliberately avoids — minimal effects overall, with light amp overdrive or crunch typically the only "effect" beyond the instrument's natural tone, reflecting a direct reaction against the era's more elaborate progressive and arena rock production. Reverb favors live-room ambience over engineered studio reverb, consistent with the genre's small-venue, back-to-basics ethos, and amp EQ stays direct and present without heavy scooping or studio shaping. Recordings generally aim to capture a band's live set energy as directly and unadorned as possible, with minimal studio processing overall serving as the genre's core production philosophy.

## MIDI Programming

Program simple, driving, groove-locked bass lines that lock tightly with the drums, avoiding melodic elaboration in favor of a solid, live-band foundation. Drum programming should stay tight, energetic, and straightforward, with a strong backbeat prioritizing drive and immediacy over technical complexity. Sketch concise, R&B-informed guitar riffs rather than extended solos, and keep vocal melody direct and unpretentious. Humanization should be moderate to heavy throughout, since the genre's identity depends on a raw, live-band feel that tightly quantized, studio-polished programming works directly against.

## Mixing

Mixes stay direct and present across guitar, bass, drums, and vocal, avoiding heavy shaping or studio-polish EQ moves in favor of a live-band, in-the-room character. Compression stays light to moderate, preserving the natural dynamics and immediacy of a live performance rather than smoothing them into studio polish, and reverb stays minimal — a dry, live-room character is the genre's aesthetic signature, explicitly reacting against the more elaborate, reverb-heavy production of contemporaneous progressive and arena rock. Stereo imaging stays modest and natural, reflecting a genuine live-band arrangement, and automation stays minimal, since the genre's energy is consistently high and direct throughout a track.

## Mastering

Masters typically sit around -11 to -9 LUFS integrated, with moderate dynamics preserved to keep the live-band energy and immediacy intact rather than heavily compressed or polished. Frequency balance stays direct and present in the midrange (guitar, vocal, piano) with a natural, unhyped low end and top end, consistent with the genre's stripped-down, anti-studio-excess aesthetic.

## Reference Artists

Dr. Feelgood's "Roxette" and "Back in the Night" define the genre's percussive, urgent, R&B-rooted template, driven by Wilko Johnson's choppy guitar style. Eddie and the Hot Rods' "Do Anything You Wanna Do" shows the scene's harder-edged, punk-adjacent energy, and Nick Lowe's "So It Goes" (with Brinsley Schwarz) reflects the scene's songwriting craft alongside its raw performance ethos.

## Production Notes

Capture a genuine live-band energy and immediacy above all else — pub rock's core value is direct, unpretentious performance, and over-produced recordings work directly against the genre's entire ethos. Keep arrangements lean and stripped-down rather than reaching for elaborate instrumentation or studio effects, and prioritize a strong backbeat and tight rhythm section pocket, since the genre's energy depends on a live-band-tight groove more than any other single production element. Avoid over-producing with modern studio effects, which contradicts the genre's back-to-basics ethos; avoid treating pub rock as identical to punk rock, since it retains a rootsier R&B/rock and roll foundation and more melodic songwriting even as it directly enabled and anticipated punk; and avoid over-polishing performances, which undercuts the genre's essential raw, live-set energy.
