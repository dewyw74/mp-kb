## History

Medieval music spans roughly a thousand years, from the codification of Gregorian chant in the early Christian Church through the rhythmic and notational innovations of the 14th-century Ars Nova. Chant remained monophonic and unmetered for centuries before musicians at Notre Dame in Paris — Léonin and Pérotin, roughly 1150-1250 — began adding independently moving voices above chant melodies, a technique called organum, which gradually developed into fully notated rhythmic polyphony. The Ars Nova, inaugurated by the Roman de Fauvel (1310-1314) and epitomized by Guillaume de Machaut, introduced more flexible rhythmic notation and complex isorhythmic structure, while composers like Hildegard von Bingen produced original, expressively wide-ranging melody outside the chant tradition entirely.

## Music Theory

Medieval music theory is built on the eight church modes rather than major/minor tonality, and medieval polyphony constructs vertical sonority from parallel or oblique motion at the fourth, fifth, and octave rather than triadic chords. The Ars Nova's isorhythmic motet technique layers an independent rhythmic pattern (talea) against an independent melodic pattern (color) in the tenor voice, producing structural complexity through mathematical/proportional relationships rather than harmonic progression as later eras would understand it.

## Arrangement

Chant is structured through call-and-response alternation between a soloist (or small group) and the full choir, following the liturgical text's own structure rather than a musical form imposed on it. Organum thickens this foundation by layering increasingly independent moving voices above a slow chant-derived tenor, while secular courtly song adopts the formes fixes (rondeau, ballade, virelai) — fixed poetic-musical refrain forms that gave composers a repeatable structural container outside the liturgy.

## Instruments

Sacred music in this period is overwhelmingly vocal and unaccompanied, with instruments largely confined to secular and dance contexts: bowed vielle, plucked lute and gittern, recorder and shawm for melody, frame drum and tabor for rhythm, and portative or positive organ appearing in both courtly and later liturgical settings. There is no dedicated bass instrument; the chant-derived tenor voice itself serves as the harmonic anchor around which other voices are built.

## Sound Design

"Sound design" in the modern sense does not exist in this period — timbral character comes entirely from vocal blend, instrument choice, and, critically, the natural acoustic decay of the stone cathedral or chapel spaces the music was composed for. Melisma (extending a single syllable across many notes) is chant's primary expressive device, functioning as the era's equivalent of ornamentation or modulation.

## MIDI Programming

Chant melody must be programmed with free, unquantized rhythm that follows Latin text stress rather than a metric grid, and the tenor voice should be treated as a sustained modal anchor rather than a rhythmic bass part. Organum voices move largely in parallel perfect intervals with contrary motion reserved for cadence points, and heavy manual timing/rubato is essential — quantized chant immediately reads as inauthentic.

## Mixing

Mixing should be warm and midrange-forward with minimal low end, since the style is exclusively vocal/modal with no bass-driven content, and long convolution reverb modeling real cathedral acoustics is the single most important production decision for authenticity. Compression should be minimal to none, preserving the natural dynamic swell and decay of unamplified voices in a resonant physical space.

## Mastering

Masters should preserve maximum dynamic range (roughly -20 to -16 LUFS integrated) with a midrange-centered, warm frequency balance and no true sub-bass content, since heavy limiting or bass reinforcement destroys the fragile, intimate character that defines the style.

## Reference Artists

Hildegard von Bingen's "O Virtus Sapientiae" showcases original, wide-ranging melodic invention outside the standard chant tradition; Pérotin's "Viderunt Omnes" demonstrates early Notre Dame organum's layered, independently moving voices above a chant tenor; Guillaume de Machaut's "Messe de Nostre Dame" is the first known complete polyphonic mass setting by a single composer, while his "Douce Dame Jolie" exemplifies secular Ars Nova courtly song in virelai form.

## Production Notes

Treat the chant-derived tenor voice as the structural and modal backbone of any polyphonic texture rather than a modern harmonic bass line, compose within a single church mode rather than major/minor tonality, and use cathedral-scale reverb as a compositional parameter the music was designed around, not a post-hoc effect. Avoid quantizing chant to a rigid grid or introducing functional tonal harmony that did not yet exist — both immediately break the modal, text-driven authenticity that defines medieval style.
