## History

Doom metal emerged in the early 1980s from the pioneering slow, heavy riffs of Black Sabbath, crystallizing as a distinct subgenre with the release of Candlemass' *Epicus Doomicus Metallicus* (1986) and Saint Vitus' *Born Too Late* (1986). The sound spread to the United States via bands like Sleep and Electric Wizard, while Scandinavia contributed a bleak, melancholic edge through My Dying Bride and Katatonia. Over three decades the genre has diversified into funeral‑doom, stoner‑doom, and sludge‑infused variants, but the core aesthetic—extremely slow tempos, down‑tuned guitars, and an oppressive atmosphere—remains constant.

## Music Theory

Doom metal relies heavily on natural minor, Phrygian, and harmonic minor scales, exploiting the dark intervals of the b2 and b6. Chord progressions favor the i–bVI–bVII movement, creating a sense of inevitable descent, while occasional use of diminished chords adds tension. Riffs are built on sustained power chords with heavy vibrato, often employing tritone intervals to heighten dissonance. Melodic vocal lines typically sit in a baritone range, using minor thirds and occasional chromatic passing tones.

## Arrangement

The genre’s structures are deliberately spacious: an intro of droning guitars or organ pads establishes a foreboding mood, followed by a minimalist verse that repeats a simple riff. Pre‑choruses introduce subtle harmonic shifts, leading to a massive chorus where guitars, bass, and drums converge into a wall of sound. Bridges often provide a contrasting clean arpeggio or acoustic interlude before a blistering solo. The outro usually returns to the opening drone, allowing the track to fade into a lingering resonance.

## Instruments

Drums are tuned low with a focus on the floor tom and kick, delivering a half‑time groove that emphasizes weight over speed. Bass guitars are typically 4‑string instruments tuned down to B♭ or C, providing sustained low‑end foundation. Guitars are typically 6‑string, down‑tuned to B or C standard, and run through high‑gain tube amps with thick distortion; clean passages often feature reverb‑drenched arpeggios. Keyboards include Hammond organ or vintage analog synth pads for sustained chords, and occasional piano or acoustic guitar for atmospheric interludes. Orchestration may incorporate strings, choir samples, or funeral‑march brass to heighten the epic scope.

## Sound Design

Synthesizers are used for thick, evolving pads (analog saws with slow LFO sweeps) and organ tones that sit beneath the guitars. Filters are swept slowly to create rising tension during builds. Modulation includes tremolo on guitars and subtle chorus on organ patches. Effects chain typically features long plate reverb, analog tape saturation, and occasional spring reverb on clean sections. Parallel distortion on guitars and multiband compression on drums help maintain clarity while preserving the genre’s crushing density.

## MIDI Programming

Bass patterns consist of sustained root notes held for whole measures, with occasional octave jumps to mirror guitar riffs. Drum programming follows a half‑time pattern: kick on beat 1, snare on beat 3, with tom fills every 4–8 bars and cymbal swells for transitions. Lead melodies are programmed with legato portamento, using minor pentatonic and harmonic minor scales to emulate the vocal style. Velocity is kept low to medium for a deliberate feel, and slight timing humanization (10–20 ms) prevents a mechanical sound.

## Mixing

EQ emphasizes sub‑bass (60–80 Hz) for weight, cuts the 200–300 Hz region to avoid mud, and boosts 800 Hz–1.2 kHz for guitar bite. Compression uses slow attack on drums to let transients cut through, with parallel compression on guitars for sustain. Reverb employs long plate/hall settings on vocals and leads, while drums receive a subtle room ambience. Delay is used sparingly on clean guitars and organ lines. Stereo imaging spreads guitars wide with mid‑side techniques, keeping bass and kick centered. Automation rides filter cutoffs and volume to accentuate climactic moments.

## Mastering

Masters target -10 to -8 LUFS integrated, preserving a wide dynamic range to retain the genre’s crushing impact. Emphasis is placed on a solid sub‑bass foundation, full midrange presence for guitar heft, and a controlled high‑end to keep cymbals crisp without harshness. Gentle tape saturation and harmonic exciters add analog warmth without sacrificing clarity.

## Reference Artists

Candlemass, Saint Vitus, Electric Wizard, Sleep, My Dying Bride, and Katatonia are the cornerstone acts whose albums define the sonic template. Tracks such as Candlemass' “Solitude,” Saint Vitus' “Born Too Late,” Electric Wizard’s “Dopethrone,” Sleep’s “Dragonaut,” My Dying Bride’s “The Cry of Mankind,” and Katatonia’s “My Twin” serve as essential listening for production reference.

## Production Notes

- Record guitars with a mic’d cabinet (SM57 + ribbon) and blend a DI for low‑end clarity.  
- Use high‑gain amps (Mesa/Boogie Dual Rectifier) with a slow‑attack compressor to sustain notes.  
- Layer subtle organ or synth pads beneath riffs for depth.  
- Apply tape saturation on the master bus for vintage warmth.  
- Avoid over‑compressing drums; preserve the slow, deliberate punch.  
- Keep guitars dark and low‑mid focused; excessive brightness destroys the doom aesthetic.  
- Modern techniques: mid‑side widening, cathedral convolution reverb, and harmonic exciters for low‑tuned guitars.
