## History

Reggae emerged in Kingston, Jamaica in the late 1960s as ska and rocksteady slowed in tempo and developed a new rhythmic emphasis — the "one drop" drum pattern and offbeat guitar/keys "skank" — creating a distinctly laid-back yet rhythmically sophisticated groove. Bob Marley and the Wailers brought the genre to international prominence through the 1970s, fusing Rastafarian spiritual and political themes with reggae's instrumental innovations, while the genre's production techniques (particularly through dub engineering pioneers like King Tubby and Lee "Scratch" Perry) profoundly influenced the development of remix culture, electronic music, and hip-hop production worldwide.

## Music Theory

Reggae harmony stays deliberately simple — two-to-four-chord loops (I-IV-V and close variants) repeat throughout a track, shifting musical interest away from harmonic development and toward the interplay between rhythm and a prominently melodic bassline. This harmonic simplicity is a deliberate choice, leaving space for the genre's rhythmic sophistication (the one-drop pattern, offbeat skank) to carry the primary musical interest.

## Arrangement

Reggae song structure follows a straightforward verse-chorus form built around a continuously sustained groove, with an instrumental or dub-style breakdown (stripping the arrangement back to emphasize bass and drums) a common structural device. Builds and dynamic escalation are minimal by design — the genre's power comes from a sustained, hypnotic groove rather than dramatic arrangement development.

## Instruments

The "one drop" drum pattern (kick and snare together on beat 3, with beat 1 left conspicuously empty) is reggae's most structurally essential and identifiable rhythmic device, paired with a deep, melodically active bassline that functions as a genuine lead instrumental voice. The offbeat guitar "skank" — a tight, percussive chord stroke on the upbeats — and organ "bubble" rhythm complete the genre's signature rhythmic texture.

## Sound Design

Reggae's sound design, especially as filtered through dub engineering practice, relies heavily on spring reverb and tape/analog echo as expressive, often rhythmically deployed effects rather than incidental polish. These techniques — reverb throws, delay feedback, drop-outs — originated in reggae/dub production and went on to profoundly influence remix culture and electronic music production more broadly.

## MIDI Programming

The one-drop drum pattern must be programmed precisely to capture the genre's essential rhythmic signature, while the bassline should be treated as melodically active and structurally prominent rather than simple harmonic support. Significant humanization — particularly the genre's characteristic relaxed, "behind the beat" timing — is essential and should never be rigidly quantized away.

## Mixing

Mixing gives the bass prominent, well-defined low-end space as a genuine lead instrumental voice, while vocals sit relaxed within the mix rather than aggressively upfront. Heavy spring reverb and tape-style echo, especially in dub-influenced production, are defining genre techniques, often used expressively and rhythmically through effects-send automation ("throws") rather than as static, constant processing.

## Mastering

Masters preserve moderate dynamics, typically -13 to -11 LUFS integrated, supporting the genre's relaxed, natural groove character, with a deep, present bass and a smooth, non-aggressive top end.

## Reference Artists

Bob Marley and the Wailers' "One Love" and "Redemption Song" represent the genre's international commercial and spiritual peak; Peter Tosh's "Legalize It" showcases reggae's political dimension; Toots and the Maytals' "Pressure Drop" demonstrates the genre's soul/R&B-influenced vocal tradition.

## Production Notes

Program the one-drop rhythm precisely, since it is the genre's single most identifiable and essential rhythmic signature, and give the bassline genuine melodic prominence and low-end space rather than treating it as simple harmonic support. Use the offbeat guitar/keys skank as a core rhythmic element and lean on spring reverb/tape echo as expressive tools, particularly for dub-adjacent reggae production. Avoid rigidly quantizing vocal and instrumental timing, which strips away the genre's essential relaxed, "behind the beat" feel.
