# Cadences and Functional Resolution: Authentic, Deceptive, and the ii-V-I Turnaround

A cadence is the harmonic gesture that signals a phrase's end — how strongly and how predictably it resolves is itself a genre-coded compositional choice, not a fixed rule. This knowledge base documents three distinct cadential/turnaround approaches, each associated with a different genre lineage and a different relationship to resolution.

## The Authentic Cadence: Maximum Resolution

`classical_period.md` documents the strongest, most conclusive cadence type as a structural default: "I - V7 - I (strong authentic cadences closing phrases)." The authentic cadence (dominant resolving directly to tonic) is the harmonic gesture with the least ambiguity about where a phrase has landed — it's the cadence type against which the deceptive cadence below defines itself by contrast.

## The Deceptive Cadence: Delayed Resolution as Expressive Device

`romantic.md` documents the deliberate frustration of that same resolution as an emotional tool: "Deceptive cadences (V - vi) used for emotional/expressive delay of resolution." Where an authentic cadence (V-I) resolves the dominant's tension directly to the expected tonic, a deceptive cadence redirects that same dominant to the relative minor (vi) instead — the tension built by the dominant chord is real and the listener expects tonic, but the arrival is redirected somewhere related-but-unexpected. `romantic.md` frames this explicitly as an emotional technique: "secondary dominants, diminished-seventh chords, and deceptive cadences delay and complicate resolution for maximum emotional effect," part of a broader Romantic-era harmonic language that treats resolution itself as something to be earned and prolonged rather than delivered immediately.

## The ii-V-I Turnaround: Jazz's Structural Backbone

Distinct from either cadence type above, the ii-V-I progression (and its minor-key cousin, ii-V-i) is documented across an unusually wide span of jazz-descended genres as the fundamental harmonic building block rather than an occasional cadential gesture: `latin_jazz.md`'s "ii-V-I bebop cycles layered over clave-based rhythm," `bossa_nova.md`'s "ii-V-I with extended 7th/9th voicings" alongside "circle-of-fifths turnarounds with substitute dominants (tritone subs)," `cool_jazz.md`'s "ii-V-I with extended/color tones (9ths, 11ths, 13ths) emphasized over chromatic alteration," and `smooth_jazz.md`'s softened "ii-V-I with extended 9th/11th/13th voicings" alongside the pop-jazz "I-vi-ii-V" turnaround. The ii-V-I's persistence across bebop-descended, Latin jazz, bossa nova, cool jazz, and smooth jazz — each with a different voicing/extension treatment but the identical underlying root-motion pattern — establishes it as jazz harmony's load-bearing structural unit, functioning the way a single authentic cadence does in classical phrase-ending, but repeated continuously as the genre's basic harmonic engine rather than reserved for phrase endings.

## The ii-V-I's Afterlife in Sample-Based and Vintage-Referencing Genres

Because so much of hip-hop and its descendants draw on sampled jazz and soul source material, the ii-V-I turnaround appears inherited (rather than freshly composed) across several non-jazz genre files: `boom_bap.md`'s "looped sampled jazz/soul chord changes (often ii-V-I-derived from source material)," `east_coast_hip_hop.md`'s identical framing, `lo_fi_hip_hop.md`'s "jazz's extended chord vocabulary (major 7ths, minor 7ths, ii-V-I motion) looped in simple two-to-four-chord vamps," and `vaporwave.md`'s "inherited directly from sampled source (ii-V-I jazz-pop movement is common)." In each case the progression isn't being composed by the hip-hop/electronic producer at all — it's a harmonic artifact of the jazz or soul recording being sampled, looped, and repurposed, which is why `boom_bap.md` and `east_coast_hip_hop.md` both describe their harmony as "almost entirely sample-derived" rather than independently written.

## Common Mistakes

**Using a deceptive cadence as a phrase's final ending.** Its entire function per `romantic.md` is to delay resolution, not provide it — a deceptive cadence needs a subsequent authentic cadence (or another resolution) to actually close the phrase; ending on the deceptive cadence itself leaves the listener's expectation genuinely unresolved.

**Treating the ii-V-I as a single cadential gesture in a jazz context rather than the genre's continuous structural engine.** Unlike classical music's authentic cadence (reserved for phrase endings), jazz's ii-V-I typically recurs throughout a tune as its basic harmonic motion, not just at structural boundaries.
