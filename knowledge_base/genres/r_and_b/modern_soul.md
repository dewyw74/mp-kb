## History

Modern soul emerged within the UK's Northern Soul scene as a way of distinguishing mid-1970s-to-1980s American soul and funk records — too recent and too sonically sophisticated to qualify as classic 1960s "Northern" stompers — from the earlier repertoire that had defined the movement. The pivotal moment came at Blackpool Mecca's Highland Room, where DJ Colin Curtis began a weekly residency in 1973 (after the closure of the Golden Torch) and was joined by Ian Levine in a partnership that lasted until 1978. By 1974-75, Levine and Curtis had begun deliberately broadening the Highland Room's playlist to include contemporary disco, funk, and jazz-funk alongside older soul sides, a shift that effectively split the wider Northern Soul movement into rival camps often described as "oldies" versus "newies," or "stompers" versus "moderne." The newer, smoother strand of programming became known as modern soul, and it persisted as its own recognized scene at venues and dedicated "modern rooms" (such as the Garage room at the Carlton) well beyond Blackpool Mecca's own closure.

## Music Theory

Modern soul draws on the more harmonically developed vocabulary of mid-1970s American soul and jazz-funk — extended 7th and 9th chord voicings and more active ii-V-I movement — in contrast to Northern Soul's simpler I-IV-V, Motown-derived changes. The tempo sits lower and the groove sits smoother (90-115 BPM) than Northern Soul's driving 100-130 BPM stomp, reflecting the scene's absorption of contemporary funk and jazz-funk influence.

## Arrangement

Tracks favor a groove-focused hook and a more restrained vocal build than Northern Soul's immediate, all-out stomp, layering backing vocals, horns, and keyboard fills in keeping with mid-to-late-1970s American soul production values. Instrumental or jazz-funk-flavored breaks are common, and songs typically close cleanly to keep DJ sets moving — the same practical dancefloor function Northern Soul serves, applied to a smoother repertoire.

## Instruments

Instrumentation follows whichever mid-1970s-to-1980s American soul, funk, or jazz-funk session produced the original record — tighter, more syncopated drumming, a more melodically active bass line, and electric piano/organ harmonic fill typical of the era's more developed studio production, in contrast to Northern Soul's leaner 1960s combo instrumentation.

## Sound Design

As with Northern Soul and rare groove, modern soul is fundamentally a curatorial DJ practice rather than a studio production style of its own; its sound design is whatever mid-1970s-to-1980s American soul studios produced, later selected and sequenced by UK tastemakers rather than reprocessed.

## MIDI Programming

Program a smoother, more melodically active bass line drawing on jazz-funk vocabulary and a tighter, more syncopated mid-tempo groove (90-115 BPM), allowing more dynamic contrast and pocket-focused restraint than Northern Soul's sustained high-energy stomp.

## Mixing

EQ should stay warmer and more midrange-balanced than Northern Soul's vocal-and-horn-forward push, reflecting the more developed mid-to-late-1970s American studio production the source material came from, with a wider, more detailed stereo image than Northern Soul's narrow, largely mono source pressings.

## Mastering

Modern reissue/compilation masters typically target -10 to -8 LUFS integrated, preserving the moderate dynamic range of the original mid-to-late-1970s American soul and jazz-funk source recordings, with a warmer, more midrange-detailed balance than Northern Soul's brighter 1960s pressings.

## Reference Artists

Frankie Beverly's pre-Maze group Raw Soul, with the single "You Left Me" / "Open Up, Your Heart," is a widely cited modern soul touchstone, while Al Johnson's 1980 Columbia single "I'm Back for More" (a duet with Jean Carn, produced by Norman Connors, reaching #26 R&B) exemplifies the smoother, more polished soul sound the scene championed. DJs Ian Levine and Colin Curtis, whose Blackpool Mecca Highland Room residency drove the "modern" split from Northern Soul, are the genre's key tastemakers.

## Production Notes

Understand modern soul as a UK DJ-curatorial term describing mid-1970s-to-1980s American soul and funk records too recent or smooth for the Northern Soul canon but too soulful for straight disco or funk filing, born from Ian Levine and Colin Curtis's Highland Room playlist shift. Avoid confusing it with Northern Soul (earlier, faster, 1960s-sourced stompers) or rare groove (a later, London-centric, breakbeat-and-sampling-focused scene) — modern soul occupies its own specific place in the Northern Soul lineage, defined by smoother, more harmonically sophisticated source material and its own dedicated club "modern rooms."
