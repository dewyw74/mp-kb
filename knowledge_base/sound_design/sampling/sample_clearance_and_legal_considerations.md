# Sample Clearance and Legal Considerations for Commercial Release

Every other entry in `knowledge_base/sound_design/sampling/` covers the technical craft of sampling. This entry covers a different, non-technical but essential production consideration: whether a sample can legally be used in a track released commercially, and what alternatives producers reach for when it can't. This is documented as a recurring, practical concern across a striking number of this knowledge base's sample-descended genre files — not a rare edge case. Genre files consulted for grounding: `knowledge_base/genres/hiphop/gangsta_rap.md`, `jazz_rap.md`, `instrumental_hip_hop.md`, `boom_bap.md`, `east_coast_hip_hop.md`, `uk_drill.md`, `chicano_rap.md`, `knowledge_base/genres/edm/house.md`, `jazz_house.md`, `chicago_house.md`, `french_house.md`, `disco_house.md`, `afro_house.md`, `knowledge_base/genres/electronic/future_funk.md`, `plunderphonics.md`.

## The Legal Basics: Two Separate Licenses

Commercially releasing a track containing a sample of another recording generally requires two distinct licenses, each covering a different underlying right: a **master use license**, granting permission to use the actual recorded audio (typically held by a record label, the original artist, or an estate/catalog owner), and a **mechanical/composition license**, granting permission to use the underlying song — melody, lyrics, arrangement (typically held by the songwriter or their publisher). Both are required; obtaining one without the other does not clear a sample for use. Clearance is commonly structured as an upfront fee (which can range widely, from low four figures to well over $25,000 per license) or as an ongoing percentage of the finished track's royalties, and both master and publishing rights are typically negotiated and paid separately. *(Source: web search of current music-industry licensing guidance.)*

A landmark U.S. court ruling — *Bridgeport Music, Inc. v. Dimension Films* (6th Cir., 2005) — held that any recognizable digital sample of a sound recording requires clearance regardless of length or how heavily it's altered, famously stating "get a license or do not sample," and effectively eliminating a "de minimis" (too small to matter) defense for sound-recording sampling within that circuit. Other U.S. circuits have not adopted this rule identically (the Ninth Circuit's *VMG Salsoul v. Ciccone*, 2016, preserved a de minimis defense), so the exact legal risk of an uncleared sample can depend on jurisdiction — but the practical, safest assumption across the industry is that any recognizable sample of a copyrighted recording needs clearance before commercial release. *(Source: web search of case-law summaries; both rulings are US federal appellate decisions.)*

## Interpolation and Re-Playing as Clearance-Avoidance Alternatives

Several genre files document a specific technical workaround: re-performing (rather than directly sampling) the material that inspired a track, which sidesteps the master use license entirely because no original recording is actually used, though the mechanical/composition license may still apply if the melody itself is recognizably derived from a copyrighted song:

- `disco_house.md` documents this directly: "Re-playing/re-recording disco-style bass, guitar, and string parts (rather than sampling) to avoid clearance issues while preserving the genre's sound, a common nu-disco-adjacent approach."
- `french_house.md` documents the same principle applied to its own signature source material: "Recreating the filter-sweep aesthetic with fully original (non-sampled) chord loops to avoid clearance issues while preserving the sound."
- `jazz_rap.md` documents a hybrid solution combining new performance with the original sampling *technique*: "Use high-quality jazz sample libraries or live jazz musicians recorded specifically for chopping, when direct sampling isn't cleared for use" — the material is newly recorded, but recorded and chopped using the same production method a genuine vintage sample would have received.
- `instrumental_hip_hop.md` documents the same principle stated as a general modern production adaptation: "Modern producers extend the technique using software samplers and legally cleared or interpolated material where original vinyl sampling faces clearance issues, while preserving the same chop-and-recombine compositional approach."
- `afro_house.md` documents this specifically for vocal sample content: "Layering sampled vocal chants (cleared or royalty-free packs) with pitch and formant processing to create original melodic hooks without direct sampling issues."

## Royalty-Free and Cleared Sample Packs as a Sourcing Alternative

`boom_bap.md` and `east_coast_hip_hop.md` both document commercial sample-pack libraries — pre-cleared for use — as the dominant modern substitute for direct vinyl sampling: "Digging through royalty-free or cleared sample packs inspired by classic soul/funk/jazz breaks for modern boom-bap revival production." This is a sourcing-stage decision (see `sample_library_curation_workflow.md`) rather than a post-hoc legal fix — the clearance question is resolved before the material is even selected, by choosing a source category (a pack explicitly licensed for producer use) that doesn't require individual negotiation per track.

## When Uncleared Sampling Is the Deliberate Point

`plunderphonics.md` documents the one genre in this knowledge base where uncleared sampling of recognizable copyrighted material is treated as the artistic and cultural core of the practice rather than a risk to manage: the file recommends producers "consider the genre's historical relationship to copyright and fair-use debates when working with recognizable copyrighted source material," and lists "ignoring the genre's historical and ongoing legal/ethical dimension around uncleared sampling" as a named common mistake — a defining part of its identity and controversy rather than an oversight to correct. This is the exception that clarifies the rule: every other genre file above treats clearance as a practical constraint to solve around, while plunderphonics treats confronting that same legal boundary directly as the genre's actual statement.

## A Practical Decision Framework

1. **Identify whether the source material is copyrighted and recognizable.** Per the Bridgeport ruling's practical takeaway, assume any identifiable sample of a copyrighted recording needs clearance for safe commercial release, regardless of how short or heavily processed it is.
2. **Decide whether clearance, interpolation/re-recording, or a pre-cleared substitute fits the project.** Direct clearance (`gangsta_rap.md`, `jazz_house.md`) preserves the exact original source; re-recording (`disco_house.md`, `french_house.md`) preserves the sound without the master rights issue; pre-cleared packs (`boom_bap.md`, `east_coast_hip_hop.md`) and royalty-free vocal chants (`afro_house.md`) sidestep the question at the sourcing stage.
3. **Remember both license types if pursuing direct clearance.** A cleared master recording without the matching mechanical/publishing clearance (or vice versa) does not fully clear a sample for commercial release.
4. **Treat the decision as genre-informed, not purely legal.** `plunderphonics.md` shows that for some creative projects, engaging with uncleared sampling's legal/ethical tension directly is itself the point — the "safe" alternatives aren't universally the correct creative choice, only the lower-risk one.

## Common Mistakes

**Assuming a short or heavily processed sample is automatically safe from clearance requirements.** Per the Bridgeport ruling, this assumption doesn't hold in every U.S. jurisdiction — recognizability, not length or processing amount, is generally the operative question.

**Clearing only the master recording or only the composition, not both.** Both the master use license and the mechanical/composition license are independently required for a fully cleared commercial sample.

**Treating clearance as a legal-team-only concern disconnected from production decisions.** Per the genre files above, clearance shapes concrete sourcing and technique choices made throughout production (pre-cleared packs, interpolation, re-recording) — it's most efficiently addressed during sourcing (`sample_library_curation_workflow.md`) rather than discovered as a blocker after a track is finished.
