## History

Synthwave (also called retrowave) emerged in the late 2000s among internet-native electronic producers reconstructing the sound of 1980s film scores, video game soundtracks, and synth-pop records. Early foundational releases include Kavinsky's "Nightcall" (2010, later prominently featured in the film *Drive*, 2011) and the French artist's broader catalog, alongside College and Electric Youth. Mitch Murder in Sweden and Com Truise in the US developed the genre's more melodic, nostalgic strand through the early 2010s. The sound draws explicitly on John Carpenter's minimal-synth horror scores, Vangelis's *Blade Runner* soundtrack, Tangerine Dream's film work, and Italo disco's driving arpeggios and gated drum machines. The genre grew almost entirely through internet labels, YouTube channels, and Bandcamp rather than traditional label infrastructure, and by the mid-2010s had split into recognizable branches — outrun (driving, upbeat), darksynth (aggressive, horror-tinged), and a more melancholic, dreamlike mainstream strand exemplified by The Midnight and Timecop1983.

## Music Theory

Synthwave leans heavily on natural minor and Dorian modes, favoring progressions like i-VI-III-VII that evoke both 80s pop balladry and horror-film scoring without requiring complex jazz harmony. Chord voicings tend to be simple triads or add9 chords played on chorused pads, letting the arpeggiator or lead melody carry harmonic interest through movement rather than dense voicing. Lead melodies favor wide, singable intervals — fourths, fifths, and octave leaps — often with portamento/glide between notes, a direct inheritance from monophonic analog lead synths of the actual 1980s that could only play one glided note at a time.

## Arrangement

A typical synthwave track opens by establishing its core arpeggio or bass pulse alongside a wide analog pad, frequently with a gated-reverb snare hit announcing the beat's entrance — a structural nod to 1980s film title sequences. Verses stay comparatively sparse, built from the arp/bass groove plus a restrained lead or vocal, so the chorus can expand with the full driving arrangement: dense arpeggio, gated drums, wide pad, and the main hook at full density. A breakdown bridge often strips back to just pad and solo lead, sometimes with a key change for emotional lift, before a final chorus and a fade-style outro that mirrors the opening texture.

## Instruments

The rhythm section is built almost entirely from emulated vintage drum machines — LinnDrum (LM-1) and Simmons SDS-V-style electronic kits, with the gated-reverb snare being the genre's most recognizable single sound. Bass is typically a simple sawtooth or square analog-style patch playing driving root-fifth-octave patterns locked tightly to the kick. Lead and pad synths — sawtooth/supersaw leads and chorused string-machine or Juno-style pads — form the melodic and harmonic core, with 16th-note arpeggios a near-universal genre signature. Guitar appears occasionally for solos with 80s-style chorus and delay, but the palette stays overwhelmingly synthetic.

## Sound Design

Analog subtractive synthesis (Roland Juno-60/106 and Jupiter-8 style patches, whether hardware or software emulations like u-he Diva, TAL-U-No-LX, or Arturia's V Collection) forms the backbone, with sawtooth and pulse-width-modulated square oscillators as the primary waveforms. Chorus/ensemble effect (emulating units like the Roland Dimension D) is applied almost universally across pads and leads — it's arguably the single ingredient most responsible for making a mix read as authentically "80s analog." Resonant lowpass filters with slow envelope sweeps add movement to pads, while gated and plate reverb (heavily on drums, more subtly on leads) defines the genre's spatial character alongside tape/analog-style saturation for overall warmth.

## MIDI Programming

Drum and arpeggio patterns are programmed on a strict, largely un-swung 16th-note grid — machine-precise timing is a deliberate period-correct aesthetic choice, not something to be smoothed over with humanization. Bass follows simple, driving root-note patterns on straight 8ths or 16ths locked to the kick for a motorik, propulsive feel inherited from Italo disco and 80s synth-pop. Lead melodies use generous portamento/glide between notes and wider velocity/expression variation than the mechanically even drum and arp parts, giving leads a "played" quality against the sequenced rhythm section.

## Mixing

Sidechain compression from kick into bass and pads creates the pumping rhythmic feel heard on classic Italo disco and 80s pop records, and is a defining synthwave mix technique as much as a dynamics tool. Gated reverb on the snare is the genre's signature effect; plate reverb is used more subtly elsewhere for a sense of 80s studio space. Stereo width is pushed hard on chorused pads and doubled leads, spread wide left-right, while kick, snare, and bass stay centered for translation and punch. Eighth-note or dotted-eighth delays on lead lines add rhythmic interest and width without cluttering the core groove.

## Mastering

A target of roughly -10 to -8 LUFS integrated preserves the punch of gated drums and the pumping sidechain feel rather than flattening them the way modern loudness-war mastering would. Frequency balance favors a warm low-mid character (part of the analog aesthetic) against a bright, present top end for arps and leads, with a controlled — not sub-bass-dominant — low end, distinguishing synthwave's tonal balance from modern bass-heavy EDM.

## Reference Artists

Kavinsky's "Nightcall" remains the genre's most widely recognized track and a useful reference for driving arpeggios, gated drums, and vocoded vocals. The Midnight represents the melodic, emotionally warm mainstream strand of synthwave; Com Truise leans more melancholic and textured; Mitch Murder and Gunship draw more directly on 80s action-film and video-game scoring conventions. Timecop1983 is a strong reference for the dreamy, nostalgic ballad side of the genre.

## Production Notes

Chorus/ensemble effect on nearly every synth layer is the single technique that most reliably pushes a mix from "generic modern electronic" to "authentically 80s" — don't skip it. Keep drum programming grid-locked with minimal swing; the mechanical precision is period-correct, not a flaw needing correction. Sidechain the kick into bass and pads to recreate the pumping feel of classic Italo disco and 80s pop production. Common mistakes include reaching for overly pristine modern supersaw/EDM lead patches that sound too wide and clean compared to genuine vintage analog character, skipping gated reverb on the snare, and making the low end too modern and sub-heavy, which pulls the track toward EDM/bass-music territory instead of period-correct 80s tonal balance. When authentic hardware or dedicated gated-reverb plugins aren't available, a noise gate placed after a reverb send on an aux bus recreates the effect convincingly.
