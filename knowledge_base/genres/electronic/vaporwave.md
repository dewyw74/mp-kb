## History

Vaporwave crystallized as a distinct genre around 2010-2012, growing out of the hypnagogic pop and chillwave scenes and drawing directly on plunderphonics' tradition of sample-based composition. Daniel Lopatin's work as Chuck Person (*Chuck Person's Eccojams Vol. 1*, 2010) is widely cited as a foundational document, slowing and looping 1980s pop samples into hypnotic, disorienting fragments. Ramona Xavier's Macintosh Plus project — particularly the album *Floral Shoppe* (2011), whose opening track "リサフランク420 / 現代のコンピュー" (built from a slowed Diana Ross sample) became the genre's single most iconic and widely recognized piece — codified the aesthetic's combination of chopped-and-screwed R&B/smooth-jazz sampling, anime and consumer-electronics imagery, and a self-aware critique of late-capitalist consumer culture and corporate muzak. James Ferraro's *Far Side Virtual* (2011) pushed a more overtly satirical, "corporate ambient" strand. The genre spread almost entirely through Bandcamp, Tumblr, and early meme culture rather than traditional media, and by the mid-2010s had spawned numerous offshoots (mallsoft, focused on shopping-mall ambiance; future funk, a more upbeat, dance-oriented cousin built from disco/funk samples).

## Music Theory

Vaporwave's harmonic content is almost entirely inherited from its sampled sources — smooth jazz's extended ii-V-I movement, 80s R&B's lush seventh and ninth chords, or corporate muzak's simple, consonant progressions — rather than composed from scratch. The genre's defining theoretical gesture is not harmonic but temporal and timbral: pitching down and slowing a sample (often together, in the chopped-and-screwed tradition borrowed from Houston hip-hop) detunes it from equal temperament and stretches its transients, producing a warped, "melting" quality that transforms familiar consonant harmony into something uncanny and dreamlike without altering the underlying chords themselves.

## Arrangement

Vaporwave arrangement rejects conventional song structure almost entirely. Tracks are frequently loop-based, built from a single sampled passage repeated with subtle processing changes (added reverb, a layer dropping in or out) rather than a composed verse/chorus form, and often begin mid-phrase as if the listener has tuned into a broadcast already in progress. There is typically no build-and-drop structure — the genre deliberately rejects EDM's tension-and-release logic in favor of static, hypnotic repetition — and tracks commonly end with an abrupt cutoff or simple fade rather than a musical resolution, reinforcing a "found tape" aesthetic over a composed one.

## Instruments

Instrumentation in vaporwave is overwhelmingly sample-derived: sampled drum breaks, bass lines, Rhodes and DX7 electric piano, synth-string pads, and smooth-jazz guitar or saxophone licks lifted directly from 1980s-1990s pop, R&B, jazz-fusion, and corporate/elevator-music source recordings. Original instrumentation, when added, deliberately mimics cheap, dated sound sources — GM soundfont presets, early ROMpler timbres, Casio-style drum machines — to stay sonically consistent with the sampled material rather than introducing modern, high-fidelity elements that would break the aesthetic.

## Sound Design

The primary "synthesis" method in vaporwave is sampling and manipulation rather than oscillator-based sound generation: slowing a source recording (lowering pitch and tempo together, in the chopped-and-screwed tradition), heavy reverb to simulate physical spaces like a shopping mall or elevator, lowpass and bandpass filtering to emulate cassette, VHS, or AM-radio frequency response, and layered degradation effects — tape hiss, vinyl crackle, bitcrushing, pitch/tape-speed wobble — to build a convincing found-media texture. These effects are typically stacked rather than used individually, since the genre's atmosphere depends on cumulative, layered degradation rather than any single processing move.

## MIDI Programming

Original MIDI programming plays a minor role in vaporwave compared to sampling. Where it appears, drum and bass parts are kept deliberately simple and unexpressive, mimicking cheap 80s drum-machine presets at an unhurried tempo, and any composed melodic material imitates dated ROMpler or General MIDI soundfont timbres rather than aiming for expressive, modern synth programming. Velocity is typically flat and unvarying on original parts, consistent with the genre's embrace of "cheap," unpolished sound sources — the expressive "feel" of a vaporwave track comes from the instability of the sampled, degraded audio rather than from performed timing nuance.

## Mixing

Vaporwave mixing intentionally narrows and degrades the frequency response — rolling off extreme lows and highs to emulate cassette, AM radio, or mall PA-system playback — rather than pursuing modern full-range clarity. Reverb is central and generous, simulating a physical space (mall, elevator, parking garage) around the sampled material, while compression stays light, letting the dynamics baked into the original sample largely define the track's feel. Stereo imaging is often intentionally narrower than modern productions, emulating consumer cassette or VHS stereo limitations, and automation is minimal — a slow filter sweep or reverb-send change is typical, rather than the dense automation common in modern electronic production.

## Mastering

Mastering deliberately resists the loudness-war aesthetic: a target of roughly -16 to -12 LUFS integrated leaves the track quiet and dynamically unprocessed relative to modern commercial releases, preserving the "unmastered found tape" quality central to the genre's identity. Frequency balance stays narrow and mid-forward by design — full-range, hi-fi clarity would directly undercut the lo-fi, found-media aesthetic vaporwave depends on.

## Reference Artists

Macintosh Plus's *Floral Shoppe* is the genre's essential reference point, particularly for its chopped-and-screwed R&B sampling and iconic use of a slowed Diana Ross vocal. James Ferraro's *Far Side Virtual* represents the more satirical "corporate ambient" strand, built from simulated notification sounds and consumer-technology sonic tropes. Saint Pepsi (later Skylar Spence) and 2 8 1 4 show the genre's more melodic, disco/funk-sampling and ambient-mallsoft offshoots respectively.

## Production Notes

Source selection is the primary compositional act in vaporwave — choosing the right smooth jazz, corporate muzak, or 80s R&B sample matters far more than any synthesis or mixing technique applied afterward. Slow the source's pitch and tempo together, rather than time-stretching without pitch change, for the authentic "melting cassette" quality the genre is known for, and layer multiple degradation effects (reverb, filtering, tape wobble, bitcrushing) rather than relying on just one. The most common mistakes are over-polishing the mix or mastering to modern loudness standards (which destroys the deliberately degraded, nostalgic character that defines the genre), reaching for high-fidelity modern samples instead of period-appropriate corporate or smooth-jazz sources, and treating vaporwave as simply "slowed-down music" without the accompanying textural degradation that actually gives it its identity. Modern producers often use tape-emulation and cassette-simulator plugins to recreate physical-media degradation without needing real hardware, and some chain multiple low-bitrate codec re-encodes for authentic digital-era degradation alongside analog-style tape effects.
