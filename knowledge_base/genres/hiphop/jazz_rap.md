## History

Jazz rap developed in New York City in the late 1980s and early 1990s as an attempt to fuse African-American musical traditions of the past with hip-hop's newly dominant present, paying tribute to jazz while expanding hip-hop's sonic vocabulary. The style developed closely alongside — and overlapped heavily with — the Native Tongues collective, co-founded in 1988 by the Jungle Brothers, De La Soul, and A Tribe Called Quest and fostered by Kool DJ Red Alert, known for Afrocentric, positive-minded lyrics and pioneering eclectic, jazz-influenced sampling. Gang Starr released the genre-defining debut single "Words I Manifest" in 1989, sampling Dizzy Gillespie's 1952 "A Night in Tunisia," followed by their debut LP No More Mr. Nice Guy the same year and later "Jazz Thing" (1990, for the Mo' Better Blues soundtrack). A Tribe Called Quest's "Can I Kick It?" (1990, from People's Instinctive Travels and the Paths of Rhythm) became one of the genre's most recognizable recordings, built around a prominent sample of Lou Reed's "Walk on the Wild Side" — notably, Reed demanded and received 100% of the song's royalties, meaning the group earned no money from the single despite its lasting popularity. Digable Planets later extended the genre's reach into the early-to-mid 1990s.

## Music Theory

Jazz rap's harmonic character comes directly from its sampled source material — extended jazz chords (major 7ths, minor 7ths, dominant 9ths) and ii-V-I turnarounds inherited from the original jazz recordings, rather than newly composed progressions. Melodic interest largely stems from the sophistication of the sampled instrumentation itself (trumpet, saxophone, double bass, piano), with rap verses delivered over these looped, harmonically rich phrases rather than a simpler, more static hip-hop backing.

## Arrangement

Tracks typically open with a looped jazz sample — a horn or bass figure — establishing a head-nodding groove before the MC enters, with verses layered over the loop and boom-bap-derived drums at a relaxed, mid-tempo pace. Rather than dramatic dynamic escalation, the genre favors sustained groove, often incorporating an instrumental break that foregrounds the jazz sample alone, letting the sampled musicianship breathe without a rap verse overlaid.

## Instruments

Boom-bap-style drum programming (typically sampled breakbeats) provides the rhythmic foundation, while sampled jazz double bass supplies warm, walking harmonic movement distinct from more static hip-hop bass approaches. Sampled jazz piano and horns — trumpet, saxophone — provide the genre's core textural and harmonic color, inherited directly from source recordings, particularly those by artists like Miles Davis, John Coltrane, and Herbie Hancock; synthesizers are largely absent from the genre's classic form.

## Sound Design

The genre's sound design is fundamentally a sampling practice rather than a synthesis-based approach — the sonic palette comes from carefully chopped and looped jazz recordings, often preserving vinyl warmth and the source recording's own studio ambience. Careful sample chopping to isolate a compelling phrase, and layering multiple samples (horn, bass, drums from different sources) into a cohesive new groove, are the genre's defining production techniques.

## MIDI Programming

When not sampling directly, emulate a walking jazz bass line with genuine harmonic movement rather than a static hip-hop root-note pattern, and program boom-bap-style breakbeat drum patterns with a relaxed, head-nodding swing feel. Horn or piano phrases should carry genuine modal/harmonic sophistication (maj7, min7, 9th chords) rather than simplified loops, with light swing and micro-timing looseness supporting the genre's laid-back pocket.

## Mixing

Give sampled horns and piano warm, present midrange space, keep vocal presence clear for the rap verses, and let the (often sampled) bass provide support without overpowering the mix. Light to moderate compression preserves the sampled material's natural dynamics, which the genre values over the heavier, more aggressive compression common elsewhere in hip-hop, and reverb is often simply inherited from the sample itself.

## Mastering

Masters target roughly -11 to -9 LUFS integrated, with a moderate dynamic range that preserves the warmth and natural character of the sampled jazz source material rather than pursuing maximum loudness. The frequency balance favors warm low-mids and midrange from sampled horns/bass/piano, clear vocal presence, and a natural, non-hyped top end consistent with vintage jazz recording character.

## Reference Artists

A Tribe Called Quest's "Can I Kick It?" (1990) and Gang Starr's "Words I Manifest" (1989) stand as the genre's most essential reference recordings, both built directly on jazz samples that define their harmonic and melodic character. De La Soul, the Jungle Brothers, and Digable Planets round out the genre's most significant acts, with the broader Native Tongues collective providing the movement's foundational Afrocentric, sample-eclectic ethos.

## Production Notes

- Sample or emulate genuine jazz harmonic sophistication (extended chords, modal movement) rather than simplifying it down to basic hip-hop loops.
- Let the sampled jazz material breathe with instrumental-only passages, not just as a constant backing loop for rapping.
- Use a walking, harmonically active bass line rather than a static root-note hip-hop bass approach.
- Keep the overall groove relaxed and head-nodding (80-95 BPM) rather than pushing toward more aggressive tempos.
- Avoid treating a jazz sample as mere texture, and avoid over-compressing the sampled material — both lose the genre's essential warmth and harmonic depth.
- Modern hybrid approach: use high-quality jazz sample libraries or live musicians recorded for chopping when direct sampling isn't cleared, and layer multiple isolated jazz stems for an original, cohesive groove.
