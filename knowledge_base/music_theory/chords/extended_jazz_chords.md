# Extended Chords (7ths, 9ths, 11ths, 13ths) Outside Jazz

Extended chords — sevenths, ninths, elevenths, and thirteenths stacked above a basic triad — are documented across this knowledge base not just as jazz vocabulary but as a specific, deliberate harmonic choice that a wide range of non-jazz genres reach for when they want warmth, sophistication, or a "lush" quality that plain triads can't deliver. The consistent pattern across every genre file citing them: extended chords are described as adding color and richness to what is often still a very simple, repetitive harmonic structure (a one- or two-chord vamp), rather than as part of a more harmonically complex progression.

## House and Deep House: Extended Voicings on a Static Vamp

`house.md` documents the technique directly: "Single chord vamp with extended 7th/9th voicings," explaining that "harmonic movement is often a single chord or a slow two-to-four-chord vamp built from jazzy 7th/9th/11th voicings sampled from disco and soul records, looped rather than developed." `deep_house.md` pushes further into the extended-chord vocabulary specifically as a genre-differentiating signature: "Lush, jazz-influenced extended chords (9ths, 11ths, min7) played on warm electric piano or pad patches carry the harmonic weight," with its production notes stating plainly: "Lean into extended jazz chord voicings (9ths, 11ths, min7) on Rhodes/pad patches — this harmonic sophistication is what separates deep house from generic house." This is a useful general principle beyond house specifically: extended chords are frequently what distinguishes a genre's more "sophisticated" or "musical" strand from its more stripped-down sibling.

## Liquid Drum and Bass: Extended Chords as Emotional Signal

`liquid_dnb.md` uses nearly identical language to `deep_house.md`, again positioning extended harmony as what separates a more musical subgenre from its more aggressive relatives: "Rich, jazz- and soul-derived extended chords (7ths, 9ths, 11ths) voiced on Rhodes-style keys or warm pads carry genuine harmonic movement across the track... liquid prioritizes emotional warmth and musicality over the rhythmic aggression that defines the genre's darker subgenres." Its production notes make the connection to authenticity explicit: "Invest real jazz/soul harmonic knowledge into chord voicings (7ths, 9ths, extended ii-V-I movement) — this is what separates authentic liquid from generic drum and bass with a soft lead."

## Yacht Rock: Extended Chords as a Genre-Defining Sophistication Signal

`yacht_rock.md` treats extended chords as close to the single most important harmonic identifier of the genre: "Extended jazz-voiced chords (maj7, min7, 9ths, 13ths) over conventional pop progressions," with production notes stating "Use extended jazz harmony (maj7, min7, 9th, 13th chords) rather than simple triads to achieve the genre's smooth, sophisticated harmonic signature" and naming the inverse directly as a common mistake: "Using simple triadic harmony instead of the genre's signature jazz-informed extended chords." This is a clean example of extended chords functioning as a genre's core harmonic signature layered on top of otherwise unremarkable pop chord progressions — the progression (I-IV-V-vi and similar) stays conventional; it's the *voicing* of each chord in the progression that carries the genre's identity.

## Bedroom Pop and Lo-fi Hip-Hop: Extended Chords for Warmth and Intimacy

`bedroom_pop.md` and `lo_fi_hip_hop.md` both cite the identical chord vocabulary (major 7ths, minor 7ths, added 9ths) for a mood-setting rather than harmonically complex purpose. `bedroom_pop.md`: "Harmony draws on jazzy extended chords (major 7ths, added 9ths) borrowed from R&B and indie pop, used more for warm mood-setting than functional pop drama." `lo_fi_hip_hop.md`: "Harmony draws directly on jazz's extended chord vocabulary (major 7ths, minor 7ths, ii-V-I motion) looped in simple two-to-four-chord vamps, favoring warmth and mellow color over harmonic drama." Both genre files explicitly separate the *chord vocabulary* (jazz-derived, extended) from *harmonic complexity* (deliberately simple, static, repetitive) — the extended chords are doing coloristic work on top of a structure that stays intentionally basic.

## The Common Thread: Color on Top of Simplicity, Not Complexity Itself

Across every genre cited here, extended chords are used to add richness to a harmonically simple foundation, not to build more complex progressions. `house.md`'s vamp, `deep_house.md`'s "carry the harmonic weight" phrasing, `yacht_rock.md`'s "over conventional pop progressions," and both `bedroom_pop.md`/`lo_fi_hip_hop.md`'s "simple two-to-four-chord vamps" all describe the identical pattern: keep the chord progression itself simple and repetitive, and let the *voicing* (which extensions are added to each chord) carry the harmonic sophistication.

## Common Mistakes

**Assuming extended chords require a more complex progression to sound "jazzy."** Every genre documented here achieves a sophisticated harmonic color while keeping the underlying progression deliberately simple — the complexity lives in the voicing, not in adding more chord changes.

**Using extended chords inconsistently within a genre that treats them as a defining signature.** `yacht_rock.md`'s common-mistakes section names this directly: falling back to simple triads in a genre where extended voicing is the primary harmonic identifier undermines the entire stylistic point.

**Choosing extensions without matching the genre's specific vocabulary.** `deep_house.md` and `liquid_dnb.md` specifically cite 9ths/11ths/min7 (not just any extension), while `yacht_rock.md` specifically cites maj7/min7/9ths/13ths — the exact combination of extensions cited differs by genre and is worth matching rather than defaulting to a single "jazzy chord" preset.
