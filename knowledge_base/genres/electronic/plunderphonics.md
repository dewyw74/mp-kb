## History

Plunderphonics originated with Canadian composer John Oswald, who began making plunderphonic recordings in the 1970s, influenced by William S. Burroughs' cut-up literary technique, before coining the term itself in 1985 in his essay "Plunderphonics, or Audio Piracy as a Compositional Prerogative." Oswald's original conception specifically indicated a piece created from samples of a single artist and no other material. In 1989, he released a greatly expanded CD version of Plunderphonic containing twenty-five tracks, each built entirely from one artist's material, with sources listed in the packaging but never authorized by the original rights holders — including Michael Jackson, whose song "Bad" was chopped into tiny pieces and rearranged into "Dab." After a threat of legal action from the Canadian Recording Industry Association on behalf of several affected artists, all undistributed copies of the release were destroyed. The technique has since been used by the Residents (drawing on Beatles material), Negativland, the Dust Brothers, and DJ Shadow, who is frequently cited as a pivotal figure carrying plunderphonic technique into wider influence starting with his 1996 album Endtroducing.....

## Music Theory

Plunderphonics has no independent harmonic language of its own — its musical material comes entirely from pre-existing recordings, with the compositional act consisting of juxtaposition, layering, and structural reconfiguration of that source harmony rather than original composition. Unlike conventional sampling, which typically uses short, unrecognizable snippets as texture, plunderphonic works deliberately foreground recognizable material — pop hits, classical excerpts, commercials, voice-overs — treating the recognizability itself as compositional content.

## Arrangement

Works typically introduce a recognizable fragment of source material before transformation processes — pitch-shifting, time-stretching, chopping — begin altering it, subjecting the recording to increasingly intensive manipulation as variable-speed alteration and dense multi-source layering build structural tension. Rather than a conventional chorus, structural interest comes from the tension between recognizability and transformation, which can peak either at maximum manipulation or at moments of unexpected clarity where the source material briefly resurfaces recognizably.

## Instruments

Every instrumental element in plunderphonics — drums, bass, melody, harmony — derives entirely from sampled source recordings rather than original performance or programming; the compositional practice is fundamentally one of selection, juxtaposition, and transformation rather than instrumental performance. The samples themselves function as the composition's primary instrument, driven by the composer's sonic intentions, rather than serving as an added extra within an otherwise conventionally composed track.

## Sound Design

Time-stretching and pitch-shifting are the genre's core transformative techniques, applied to entire recordings or substantial portions treated as foundational raw material rather than brief, unrecognizable samples. Reverb, delay, and distortion alter the sonic characteristics of source material as part of the transformation process, and dense multi-source layering combined with structural reconfiguration — reordering, chopping, rearranging — constitutes the genre's essential compositional method.

## MIDI Programming

Plunderphonics does not use conventional MIDI programming in the traditional sense — all musical content is derived from manipulated sampled source material rather than originally composed or performed parts. The compositional work happens through selecting, juxtaposing, and processing existing recordings (via pitch-shift, time-stretch, and structural rearrangement automation) rather than through note-by-note programming of new material.

## Mixing

Mix decisions serve the specific transformation being applied to source material, ranging from preserving the original recording's tonal character to radically altering it as a deliberate compositional statement — there is no fixed genre convention, since each work's approach depends on its specific source and conceptual goal. Extensive automation of pitch, time, and layering parameters is central to realizing the genre's transformation-based compositional approach.

## Mastering

Mastering approach is variable and determined by each specific work's conceptual and sonic goals rather than a standardized genre target, since the source material and the transformations applied to it differ fundamentally from piece to piece.

## Reference Artists

John Oswald's "Dab" (1989), a radical rearrangement of Michael Jackson's "Bad" from the expanded Plunderphonic CD, stands as the genre's most historically significant and legally consequential recording — its release led to the destruction of all undistributed copies following industry legal action. The Residents, Negativland, and DJ Shadow (starting with 1996's Endtroducing.....) represent the genre's broader lineage and its evolution into wider musical influence.

## Production Notes

- Foreground recognizable source material rather than disguising it as unrecognizable texture — the tension between recognition and transformation is central to the genre's power.
- Treat entire recordings or substantial portions as raw compositional material, not brief, incidental samples.
- Use variable-speed alteration, dense multi-source layering, and structural reconfiguration as primary compositional techniques.
- Consider the genre's historical relationship to copyright and fair-use debates when working with recognizable copyrighted material.
- Avoid treating samples as mere texture, and avoid using only brief, unrecognizable fragments — both miss the genre's defining recognition/transformation tension.
- Modern hybrid approach: use advanced time-stretching and pitch-shifting software for deep manipulation, while considering fair-use implications explicitly given the genre's historical legal controversies.
