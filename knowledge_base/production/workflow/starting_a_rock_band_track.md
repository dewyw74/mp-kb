# Starting a Rock/Band Track From a Blank Project

Rock and metal genres in this knowledge base are consistently documented as riff-first writing traditions — the guitar riff functions as the primary compositional unit, with bass, drums, vocal, and arrangement built to support and frame it, in contrast to EDM's loop-first or hip-hop's beat-first conventions covered in `knowledge_base/production/workflow/starting_an_edm_track.md` and `knowledge_base/production/workflow/starting_a_hip_hop_track.md`.

## Goal

Get a solid riff, locked to bass and drums, with the riff's dynamic treatment (muted vs. ringing) already decided, before vocal and full arrangement work begins.

## 1. Set tempo and feel by subgenre character

Per the genre-specific guidance across this knowledge base's rock files, tempo alone doesn't capture a subgenre's actual feel — grunge and stoner rock often want a heavier, slightly dragging pocket even at a moderate BPM, while pop-punk and arena rock want a tighter, more forward-driving feel at a comparable tempo.

## 2. Write the riff first

`metal.md` states the principle directly for its genre family: "a repeated, rhythmically powerful guitar riff... functions as the primary compositional unit, with vocal melody and guitar solos built around and against it." Writing the riff before anything else — rather than building drums or a chord progression first and fitting a riff to them afterward — keeps the track's actual compositional core in the driver's seat from the start.

## 3. Lock bass to the riff before adding drums

Per `knowledge_base/genres/metal/djent.md`'s guidance (documented in `knowledge_base/midi/patterns/bass_and_808_pattern_programming.md`), rock and metal bass typically follows the guitar's low-string riffing closely rather than running independently — establishing this relationship before programming drums means the drum pattern can be built to support an already-solid riff-plus-bass foundation rather than the other way around.

## 4. Build drums around the riff's accents

Program kick placement to align with the riff's palm-muted/power-chord hits rather than choosing a generic rock beat and fitting the riff to it — the riff's rhythmic accents should drive where the kick and snare land, consistent with the riff-as-primary-unit principle from step 2.

## 5. Decide the riff's dynamic treatment early

`pop_punk.md`'s "palm-muted verses opening into ringing, full power chords at the chorus as the primary dynamic device" and `grunge.md`'s contrast between "quiet verses and loud choruses" both use the muted-versus-ringing (or quiet-versus-loud) contrast as their primary structural dynamic tool. Deciding this contrast at the writing stage — not as a later mixing-stage automation fix — means the riff itself is composed with the contrast built in.

## 6. Add vocal after the riff foundation is locked

Per `knowledge_base/production/songwriting/topline_and_vocal_hook_arrangement.md`'s general space-leaving principle, writing the vocal melody against an already-decided riff and drum foundation (rather than the reverse) lets the vocal be phrased around the instrumental's actual rhythmic and dynamic shape.

## Common mistakes

Writing a full chord progression or drum pattern before the riff exists, which inverts rock's actual compositional priority and often produces a riff that sounds like an afterthought layered onto pre-built harmonic/rhythmic material. Also common: deciding the muted-vs-ringing dynamic contrast late, as a mixing automation fix, rather than composing it into the riff itself from the start.

## Alternatives

For vocal-melody-first songwriting within a rock context (common in more pop-adjacent rock subgenres like arena rock or power pop), start from a vocal/chord sketch on acoustic guitar or piano instead, and build the full-band riff arrangement around the finished vocal melody.
