## History

Jerk rap grew out of Jerkin', a street-dance and hip-hop subculture that originated in urban California in the late 2000s, gaining mainstream attention through Inland Empire-based groups New Boyz and Audio Push. New Boyz debuted in spring 2009 with the viral hit "You're a Jerk" from their debut album Skinny Jeanz and a Mic — the first song to bring the jerkin' style to national attention, peaking in the top thirty of the Billboard Hot 100 and performed at the 2009 BET Awards, where the group's energetic routine showcased jerkin' to millions of viewers. Audio Push followed with "Teach Me How to Jerk," while Arista-signed group Rej3ctz and dance crews Cold Flamez and Pink Dollaz also gained recognition; Cali Swag District's closely related "Teach Me How to Dougie" (2010) extended the same West Coast teen dance-rap moment nationally. Jerk rap and jerkin' spread along the West and East Coasts and into Europe (notably France and Germany) before fading as a mainstream trend, though it remained heavily derided in the Southern United States at the time.

## Music Theory

Jerk rap's harmonic vocabulary is deliberately minimal — simple, sparse two-to-four-note synth ostinatos or static single-note vamps rather than functional chord progressions — leaving maximum rhythmic and visual space for dance-cue-driven percussion and quick footwork choreography.

## Arrangement

Tracks establish a bright, minimalist synth hook immediately, often built to cue a specific dance move, with light, bouncy verses name-checking jerkin' moves, skinny-jeans fashion, and teen-party scene details. The genre favors consistent, danceable energy throughout rather than dramatic structural build, with hooks functioning as dance-instructional, chantable calls-to-action.

## Instruments

Jerk rap's instrumental core is tight, minimalist 808/909-style drum programming and a simple 808 bass ostinato, paired with bright, minimalist synth stabs that are simpler and often higher-pitched than hyphy's more eccentric riffs. This minimal, producer-driven electronic approach was built for dance-video clarity rather than club sound-system weight.

## Sound Design

Bright, minimalist synth stabs and clean, simple riffs are the genre's defining sound-design signature, paired with 808-style sub-bass and minimal additional processing that favors a clean, direct, dance-video-ready sound over heavy club processing.

## MIDI Programming

Program a simple, often single-note or two-to-four-note 808 bass ostinato alongside tight drum patterns with crisp claps on 2 and 4 and stuttering snare fills that leave clear rhythmic cues for dance transitions. Melodic material should stay bright and simple, functioning as the track's central hook without hyphy's more eccentric complexity.

## Mixing

EQ stays bright and clean, emphasizing synth-stab presence alongside a controlled, punchy 808 low end, with moderate compression supporting danceable impact without the heavier aggression found in hyphy or crunk. Reverb stays minimal, keeping the mix tight and direct for dance-video and party-ready clarity.

## Mastering

Masters target a moderate-to-loud -9 to -7 LUFS integrated with consistent, punchy energy suited to dance and party playback, and a bright, present synth/vocal midrange balanced against a controlled rather than dominant 808 low end.

## Reference Artists

New Boyz's "You're a Jerk" is the genre's foundational, chart-breaking track; Audio Push's "Teach Me How to Jerk" and Rej3ctz's "Cat Daddy" showcase the broader movement, with Cali Swag District's "Teach Me How to Dougie" representing the closely related West Coast teen dance-rap moment of the same period.

## Production Notes

Build tracks around a simple, bright synth hook and minimal drum/808 pattern designed explicitly to cue specific dance moves, keeping lyrical content light and scene-focused rather than dense. Avoid confusing jerk rap with hyphy — despite shared West Coast, synth-driven, dance-oriented roots, hyphy's synth riffs are more eccentric and its club-oriented function differs from jerk rap's lighter, teen-party, dance-video-first character — and don't overcomplicate the arrangement, since the genre depends on minimalism and clear dance cues.
