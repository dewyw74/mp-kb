## History

Melbourne bounce originated in Melbourne, Australia's nightlife scene circa 2010-2012, as local producers reworked electro house and Dutch house motifs into a more direct, festival-ready sound. Melbourne DJ Will Sparks is widely credited as the genre's creator, with his November 2012 single "Ah Yeah!" cited by In the Mix's Katie Cunningham as giving rise to the new electro house sub-genre, though Deorro and Joel Fletcher were creating similarly-sounding music around the same time. The genre gained popularity from mid-to-late 2012, rising steadily through 2013, with other Australian DJs including Timmy Trumpet, Reece Low, and Krunk! following, alongside LA-based TJR and Deorro contributing to its global expansion. Tracks like "Ah Yeah!", "Ode to Oi", and "I'm a Trumpsta (Djuro Remix)" propelled the genre to mainstream popularity from 2013 to 2015, making it a significant part of the mid-2010s festival EDM scene. Although the genre's heyday faded after the mid-2010s, it remained relevant through producers like LUM!X and B3nte.

## Music Theory

Melbourne bounce favors big, riff-like hooks over lush chord progressions, with simple, functional harmony subordinate to the bassline's rhythmic bounce. Melodic content comes from punchy lead stabs, brass-like synths, and chantable vocal chops rather than harmonic development, built for immediate festival-crowd recognition and catchiness over sophistication.

## Arrangement

Tracks open with a highly DJ-friendly, beat-only intro for easy mixing, building through tension-generating sections (noise sweeps, risers, pitched stabs) into an explosive drop. The drop itself features a driving 4/4 kick with short, off-beat bass stabs creating the genre's signature rubbery "bounce" groove, punctuated by punchy lead stabs and brass-like synth hits, with a breakdown section providing contrast before the next build-drop cycle and a DJ-friendly outro closing the track.

## Instruments

Aggressive, sawtooth-based synth timbres dominate the genre's instrumental palette — punchy lead stabs, brass-emulating synth hits, and riff-like hooks define the drop's melodic character. A driving 4/4 kick paired with short, off-beat, heavily sidechained bass stabs forms the rhythmic foundation, with tom/snare fills punctuating drop sections; guitars, acoustic keys, and orchestration are entirely absent.

## Sound Design

Sawtooth-based lead and stab synths provide the genre's punchy, cutting character, often processed with brass-emulating patches for horn-like drop hits. Heavy sidechain compression is the genre's core, defining production technique, creating the signature pumping, bouncy feel between kick and bass/synth elements, while noise sweeps and risers build tension into each drop.

## MIDI Programming

Program short, off-beat bass stabs (deliberately not on the downbeat) to create the genre's signature rubbery bounce groove, heavily sidechained against the kick. Drum patterns should use a driving 4/4 kick with tom/snare fills at key structural moments, and lead melodies should be punchy, riff-like hooks rather than developed melodic lines. Keep programming tight and machine-precise for the genre's driving, festival-ready energy.

## Mixing

Balance powerful low-end from the kick/bass interplay against gritty, present midrange for sawtooth synth stabs and bright highs for cutting lead presence. Heavy sidechain compression is essential, creating the genre's defining pumping groove, while moderate reverb on lead stabs and vocal chops adds size without washing out the drop's punchy, direct impact. Lead stabs and synth hooks spread wide across the stereo field for festival-system impact.

## Mastering

Masters target roughly -8 to -6 LUFS integrated, loud and energetic, built for maximum festival and club system impact. The frequency balance favors powerful low end, gritty and present midrange for sawtooth synth stabs, and bright, cutting highs for lead presence.

## Reference Artists

Will Sparks' "Ah Yeah!" (2012) stands as the genre's foundational, name-giving recording. Deorro and Joel Fletcher were key parallel figures in the genre's early development, and Timmy Trumpet became a significant figure in its mid-2010s festival-scene expansion.

## Production Notes

- Build the drop around short, off-beat bass stabs for the genre's signature rubbery bounce groove.
- Use aggressive, sawtooth-based synth timbres for punchy lead stabs and brass-like hits.
- Apply heavy sidechain compression throughout for the genre's defining pumping feel.
- Keep intros and outros DJ-friendly and beat-focused for easy festival/club mixing.
- Avoid placing the bassline on the downbeat or using lush chord progressions — both undercut the genre's core bounce identity and direct simplicity.
- Modern hybrid approach: use dedicated sidechain compression plugins with precise control, and structure builds/drops with automated filter sweeps and risers for festival-ready tension and release.
