## History

Drone metal was first established by Earth, a project from Olympia, Washington formed in 1989 by Dylan Carlson, who fused the slow, heavy sludge of the Melvins with the minimalist drone composition traditions of La Monte Young, Terry Riley, and Tony Conrad. Earth's early-1990s recordings, particularly 1993's Earth 2: Special Low Frequency Version, established the template of extremely slow, sustained, riff-minimal heaviness that would define the genre. Sunn O))), formed in Seattle in 1998 by Greg Anderson and Stephen O'Malley initially as a tribute act to Earth, became the genre's most prominent and influential act, blending doom metal, drone, black metal, and dark ambient into a distinctive, visually theatrical live and recorded body of work built around slow tempos, alternate low tunings, and resonant amplifier feedback.

## Music Theory

Drone metal largely sets aside conventional harmony and melody in favor of sustained tones held for extreme durations — a single chord or note may function as the entirety of a piece's harmonic content for many minutes. Where doom metal moves, however slowly, through a riff-based minor-key harmonic progression, drone metal treats harmonic movement as a rare, glacial event, with the overtone and feedback content of a single sustained tone functioning as the primary musical interest rather than any scale or chord sequence.

## Arrangement

There is effectively no verse/chorus structure; pieces unfold as a single continuous drone or sustain that begins mid-tone (as if already sounding) and evolves almost imperceptibly over the piece's duration. "Builds" happen through the slow addition of layers — a second guitar, an organ tone, increasing distortion — rather than rhythmic or dynamic escalation, and structural contrast is achieved through extended silence or a shift to feedback-only passages rather than a change in riff or tempo.

## Instruments

Drums are frequently absent entirely or reduced to occasional single struck notes, a defining departure from doom and funeral doom's retained slow but steady drum pulse. Electric guitars, heavily distorted and often down-tuned (Sunn O))) has used tunings as low as drop A), sustain single notes or chords for extended durations, treating amplifier feedback and volume as compositional material. Dark ambient synth drones frequently supplement or partially replace conventional band instrumentation.

## Sound Design

The genre's sound design centers on amplifier feedback functioning as the primary sound source, more so than any synthesizer — physical volume and stacked amplification (a hallmark of Sunn O)))'s live performances) are treated as core compositional and even conceptual elements. Long, deep reverb reinforces a physically overwhelming, cavernous sense of scale, and modulation is generally avoided in favor of near-static, slowly evolving tone.

## MIDI Programming

Program single sustained low notes held for extremely long durations rather than rhythmic patterns; drums, if used at all, should consist of single, widely spaced struck notes rather than any repeating groove. There is essentially no discrete melody to program — focus instead on the slow harmonic evolution of sustained tones and their feedback/overtone content, and use subtle amplitude or pitch drift rather than conventional timing humanization.

## Mixing

Mixing emphasizes extreme low-end and sub-bass weight, with high frequencies comparatively de-emphasized, for a physically overwhelming, monolithic low-mid mass. Compression is used sparingly to preserve the raw, uncontrolled decay of amplifier feedback, while long, deep reverb reinforces scale. The stereo image is extremely wide, filling the field uniformly rather than using panning to separate distinct instrumental parts.

## Mastering

Masters run comparatively quiet and dynamic for a metal subgenre, roughly -14 to -10 LUFS integrated, since the style's impact depends on the contrast between near-silence and overwhelming volume — heavy limiting destroys this essential dynamic range. The frequency balance stays extremely low-end and sub-bass focused, the direct opposite emphasis of black metal's treble-forward mastering approach.

## Reference Artists

Sunn O)))'s "Big Church" (from 2005's Black One) and "My Wall" (from 2002's Flight of the Behemoth) exemplify the band's stacked-amplifier, feedback-driven sound that defines contemporary drone metal. Earth's "Seven Angels" (from 1993's Earth 2: Special Low Frequency Version) documents the genre's foundational template under Dylan Carlson, predating Sunn O)))'s own founding by nearly a decade.

## Production Notes

Treat amplifier feedback and stacked volume as the genre's primary instrument and compositional material rather than as a mixing problem to tame — this is the single clearest distinction from doom metal and funeral doom, both of which retain a steady, if slow, drum pulse and riff-based structure that drone metal frequently does away with entirely. Give sustained tones room to breathe across extremely long durations, resist rushing harmonic movement, and protect dynamic range aggressively at mastering, since heavy limiting is one of the fastest ways to undo the genre's physically overwhelming quiet-to-loud impact.
