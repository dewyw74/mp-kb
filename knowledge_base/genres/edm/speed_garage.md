## History

Speed garage emerged in London around 1996-1997 as DJs began pitching down and time-stretching US garage house records, slowing their tempo slightly while adding the heavy, sub-driven Reese basslines borrowed directly from the parallel jungle and drum and bass scenes. This gave UK garage a darker, heavier, more bass-weight-focused sound than its US source material, while retaining a straight four-on-the-floor house pulse rather than adopting jungle's breakbeat patterns. Tracks like 187 Lockdown's "Gunman" and Double 99's "RIP Groove" (both 1997) defined the sound's commercial peak, and speed garage briefly became one of the UK's most prominent club and chart phenomena before the scene fragmented — its DJ-culture pitch-down technique and heavy vocal-sample usage fed into the broader UK garage continuum, while its four-on-the-floor-plus-Reese-bass approach represents an important bridge between the earlier garage house tradition and the later, more syncopated 2-step and bassline subgenres.

## Music Theory

Speed garage is harmonically minimal by design — tracks are frequently built on a single sustained chord or a simple i-VI-VII/i-iv-v vamp borrowed wholesale from the house source material being reworked, since the compositional focus sits almost entirely on bass weight and groove rather than harmonic development. What functions as "melody" is often literally the pitched-down, time-stretched vocal or instrumental hook from an existing US house or garage record, repurposed and recontextualized through the pitch-drop technique rather than freshly composed.

## Arrangement

A speed garage track frequently opens at or near the pitch/tempo of its house source material before a pitch-down transition introduces the sub-heavy Reese bassline — a structural device directly inherited from the genre's DJ-culture origins of physically pitching down house records on turntables. Once the bassline enters, the track settles into a driving four-on-the-floor groove with chopped vocal hooks riding on top, moving through filter-sweep builds into full-weight drop sections, brief bass-and-vocal breakdowns, and a DJ-mix-friendly loop-style outro suited to continuous club and pirate-radio sets.

## Instruments

The straight four-on-the-floor kick is speed garage's key rhythmic differentiator from 2-step's broken-kick approach, even though both trace back to the same UK garage lineage. The Reese-style bassline — built from detuned, layered sawtooth oscillators imported directly from jungle and drum and bass production — is the genre's true defining instrument, often derived from pitching down and time-stretching an existing house or garage bassline rather than composed from scratch. Vocal material, similarly, is frequently sampled and repitched from US house and garage source tracks rather than newly recorded, with simple pads or organ/piano stabs providing minimal harmonic support.

## Sound Design

Reese bass construction is the genre's central sound-design discipline: multiple detuned sawtooth oscillators are layered and run through a resonant lowpass filter, producing the thick, slightly dissonant "beating" texture that defines the genre's low end, often reinforced with a clean sub-sine layer underneath for translation on smaller systems. The genre's origin technique — pitching down and time-stretching full house tracks to source both bassline and vocal material — remains a defining production approach even in original compositions, giving speed garage its characteristically heavier, darker character relative to its US house sources.

## MIDI Programming

The kick is programmed as a straight, driving four-on-the-floor pattern, keeping speed garage clearly distinct from 2-step's broken-kick syncopation despite shared UK garage ancestry. The Reese bassline typically uses longer, sustained note values with movement generated through filter and distortion automation rather than dense rhythmic note activity, differentiating it from bassline's more syncopated, note-active low end. Hats and snares carry a moderate garage-derived shuffle, present but less extreme than 2-step's pronounced syncopation, keeping the overall feel closer to a heavy house/garage hybrid.

## Mixing

The Reese bassline is given a dominant, wide low-end-through-low-mid pocket to accommodate its detuned harmonic content, with the kick's decay trimmed short so the two elements interlock rather than mask each other. Heavy sidechain compression from kick into bassline creates the genre's driving, pumping groove, while vocal samples receive reverb for space and atmosphere against an otherwise dry, tight low end. Kick, bass, and core rhythmic elements stay centered and mono for maximum club-system translation, with vocal material and pads widened for interest.

## Mastering

Speed garage masters target club-ready loudness around -8 to -6 LUFS integrated, preserving low-end punch for maximum Reese-bass impact on large sound systems — more aggressive than straight UK garage but generally less extreme than the later bassline subgenre. Frequency balance is heavily low-end and sub-focused, a direct legacy of the genre's pitched-down, bass-weight-emphasized approach to reworking house material, with a clear top end maintained for hats and vocal presence.

## Reference Artists

187 Lockdown's "Gunman" and Double 99's "RIP Groove" remain the genre's most widely cited commercial-peak reference tracks, both showcasing the classic pitched-down, Reese-bass-driven speed garage sound. Tuff Jam and Dreem Teem represent the genre's core club/DJ-culture identity, while Booker T's "Ghetto Booty" and DJ Luck & MC Neat's chart singles show the genre's broader crossover reach in the late 1990s UK charts.

## Production Notes

Keep the kick strictly four-on-the-floor — this, more than any other single choice, distinguishes speed garage from its broken-beat 2-step sibling within the wider UK garage family. Build the bassline as a proper layered, detuned Reese patch rather than a thin single-oscillator sub; the beating texture between detuned layers is the genre's core low-end signature and is not optional. Sidechain the kick into the bassline aggressively — the pumping interaction between kick and Reese bass is a defining rhythmic-dynamic feature of the sound, not just a technical fix for masking. The most common mistakes are thinning out the bassline into a generic sub patch and breaking the kick pattern into 2-step-style syncopation, both of which erase the genre's specific identity within the UK garage family tree.
