## History

Taarab — from the Swahili/Arabic word meaning "to move the heart" — emerged in Zanzibar in the late 19th century, with local tradition crediting Sultan Seyyid Barghash bin Said (r. 1870-1888) with popularizing the form after inviting an Egyptian musical ensemble to perform at his palace. Mohamed Ibrahim, upon returning from Egypt, founded the Zanzibar Taarab Orchestra, establishing taarab as a structured musical form modeled on Arabic court and orchestral practice. The genre reached wider popularity through Siti binti Saad (c.1880-1950), taarab's first major star and the first East African vocalist to release commercial recordings, who — unlike earlier Arabic-language court taarab — sang in Swahili, bringing the genre out of the palace and to ordinary Swahili-speaking Zanzibaris; she recorded over 250 phonograph records, including sessions in Bombay in the late 1920s. Later singers such as Bi Kidude (Fatuma binti Baraka, c.1910-2013), who performed extensively with Zanzibar's Culture Musical Club and was influenced directly by Siti binti Saad, carried the tradition forward, earning international recognition including a WOMEX lifetime achievement award in 2005. From the 1990s, "taarab ya kisasa" (modern taarab) — pioneered by groups like East African Melody and Jahazi Modern Taarab — introduced keyboards, electric bass, and drum machines, adapting the genre for contemporary dance-floor contexts while preserving its poetic Swahili lyric tradition.

## Music Theory

Taarab is built on the Arabic maqam system rather than Western major/minor tonality — melodies follow the characteristic scale-and-phrase structures of maqamat such as Rast, Bayati, and Hijaz, adapted through East African and Swahili performance practice. This modal foundation, rather than functional chord progressions, generates the genre's harmonic and melodic movement, with quarter-tone inflections (especially on qanun and violin) and melismatic vocal ornamentation carrying the genre's expressive weight. Lyrics follow classical Swahili poetic forms (shairi), often layering witty double meanings and social commentary (mipasho) beneath surface-level romantic themes.

## Arrangement

A taarab performance typically opens with an instrumental introduction in the Arabic taqsim tradition, loosely establishing the maqam before the vocalist enters with melismatic, ornamented verses. Call-and-response passages between the lead singer and chorus punctuate the structure, rooted in both Swahili communal performance tradition and Arabic orchestral practice, with instrumental interludes allowing violin, oud, or qanun to restate and elaborate melodic material between vocal sections, closing with a cadence that resolves the maqam's melodic tension.

## Instruments

The classical taarab ensemble (firqa), modeled on the large orchestral format of 1930s-40s Egyptian film music, combines multiple violins, oud, qanun (a large plucked zither capable of the quarter-tone inflections central to maqam performance), ney, and hand percussion (riqq, dumbak/darbuka) in a lush, heterophonic texture supporting a solo vocalist. Modern taarab ya kisasa, emerging from the 1990s, adds keyboards, electric bass, and drum machines while retaining the genre's call-and-response chorus structure and Swahili sung-poetry tradition.

## Sound Design

Classical taarab's sound design is entirely acoustic, built on the natural heterophonic blend of violin, oud, and qanun each elaborating the same maqam-based melodic line slightly differently at once — vocal melisma and quarter-tone maqam inflection function as the genre's primary expressive devices, comparable to "modulation" in more production-heavy genres. Recordings favor minimal processing and natural room ambience, preserving the ensemble's acoustic blend, while taarab ya kisasa introduces keyboard textures, drum machines, and moderate studio reverb for a more contemporary, produced sound.

## MIDI Programming

Programming or sequencing convincing taarab requires melismatic, maqam-based melodic writing (Rast, Bayati, Hijaz, and related modes) with quarter-tone-aware pitch bending where possible, since equal-tempered major/minor scale writing misrepresents the genre's Arabic modal foundation. Hand-percussion patterns should provide a steady, supportive pulse beneath free, rubato-inflected vocal phrasing, with substantial humanization essential throughout — the genre's heterophonic ensemble texture depends on natural, non-rigid timing rather than quantized precision.

## Mixing

Mixing classical taarab means preserving the natural timbre of violin, oud, and qanun within the heterophonic ensemble blend through balanced EQ, with clear presence given to the lead vocal above the instrumental texture, and light compression that preserves the vocal's natural melismatic dynamic range. Natural room ambience suits classical/orchestral recordings, while modern taarab ya kisasa productions may add moderate reverb for contemporary polish, with a moderate-to-wide stereo image reflecting the full firqa ensemble arranged around a central vocalist.

## Mastering

Classical/orchestral taarab reissues typically target -14 to -12 LUFS integrated, preserving wide dynamic range that honors the vocal's melismatic expression, while modern taarab ya kisasa productions target a more consistent -11 to -9 LUFS suited to dance-oriented contexts. Frequency balance favors warm midrange presence for violin, oud, and qanun, with a natural, non-harsh top end preserving the genre's acoustic, heterophonic ensemble character.

## Reference Artists

Siti binti Saad's late-1920s Swahili-language recordings stand among the first commercial East African vocal recordings and remain the genre's foundational reference point, having brought taarab from Zanzibar's palace tradition to ordinary Swahili speakers. Bi Kidude, who performed extensively with the Culture Musical Club and recorded the album Zanzibar (featuring "Machozi Ya Huba") with The Twinkling Stars, carried the tradition into the late 20th and early 21st centuries and received international recognition including a WOMEX lifetime achievement award. Modern taarab ya kisasa groups such as East African Melody represent the genre's electric, contemporary-dance-oriented evolution.

## Production Notes

Build melodic material from Arabic maqamat rather than Western major/minor scales — this modal foundation is taarab's core musical identity, and departing from it fundamentally misrepresents the genre. Center any arrangement on a strong lead vocalist with melismatic, ornamented delivery supported by a heterophonic instrumental texture, and clearly distinguish classical/orchestral taarab from the electric, drum-machine-driven taarab ya kisasa substyle rather than blending their production approaches indiscriminately. Avoid writing taarab melodies in equal-tempered scales, treating the genre as generic "Arabic music" without its specific East African/Swahili-coast and Zanzibar-specific history, or applying modern electronic production to a classical-style arrangement without acknowledging that shift as a genuine substyle change.
