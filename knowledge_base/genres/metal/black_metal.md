## History

Black Metal originated in the early 1980s (the "First Wave") with bands like Venom and Bathory, who combined thrash metal with satanic imagery and harsh vocals. However, the genre was truly defined by the "Second Wave" in early-1990s Norway (Mayhem, Burzum, Darkthrone, Emperor). These bands violently rejected the polished, technical production of Death Metal in favor of a raw, primitive, "lo-fi" aesthetic, creating a cold, hypnotic, and atmospheric sound. The genre later splintered into Symphonic Black Metal (featuring lush orchestrations) and Atmospheric/Depressive Black Metal.

## Music Theory

Unlike the atonal chaos of Death Metal, Black Metal is highly melodic and deeply rooted in minor keys (Aeolian and Harmonic Minor). The defining technique is "tremolo picking" full minor or diminished chords, creating a continuous, swirling wall of sound. The harmonic movement is often slow and hypnotic, providing a dark, melancholic, or majestic atmosphere beneath the extreme speed of the drums.

## Arrangement

Black metal arrangements prioritize atmosphere and hypnosis over traditional pop structures. Songs are often long, repetitive, and linear, designed to trance-induce rather than provide catchy hooks. Blast beats provide a continuous bed of white noise, while the sweeping guitar melodies slowly shift and evolve overhead. Sudden acoustic guitar interludes or ambient synthesizer passages are common.

## Instruments

The acoustic drum kit is played with relentless speed, but often recorded poorly on purpose. Guitars are heavily distorted and treble-focused. Bass is usually neglected. Synthesizers (choirs, strings, pads) are frequently used to provide a gothic, orchestral backdrop, becoming the dominant feature in Symphonic Black Metal.

## Sound Design

Traditional Black Metal "sound design" was a deliberate pursuit of bad audio quality (the "necro" sound), achieved by recording on cheap 4-track cassette recorders, using terrible microphones, and removing all the bass from the amplifiers. The goal was to sound cold, distant, and inhuman.

## MIDI Programming

Programming Black Metal drums requires a focus on the cymbals rather than the kick. The blast beats should sound like a continuous wash of high-frequency noise. Tremolo-picked guitars are difficult to sequence authentically; live guitars are essential. Synthesizer programming usually involves holding long, sustained minor chords with 90s-era digital choir or string patches.

## Mixing

A traditional Black Metal mix intentionally breaks all the rules of good audio engineering. The low-end is gutted to make the track sound cold and brittle. The guitars are mixed like a swarm of bees in the high-midrange. The vocals are buried in cavernous reverb, pushed back to sound like an eerie wind rather than a front-and-center lead.

## Mastering

Mastering is usually chaotic. It is loud and compressed, but intentionally lacks the polished, punchy dynamics of modern metal. The EQ curve is heavily skewed towards the treble, resulting in a harsh, abrasive listening experience that fans of the genre consider essential to the atmosphere.

## Reference Artists

Mayhem and Darkthrone defined the raw, primitive, lo-fi Norwegian sound. Burzum pioneered Atmospheric Black Metal, using heavy repetition and ambient synthesizers. Emperor and Dimmu Borgir introduced complex, lush orchestral arrangements, creating Symphonic Black Metal. Deafheaven later fused the genre's techniques with the beautiful, washed-out textures of Shoegaze to create "Blackgaze."

## Production Notes

If you are aiming for an authentic 90s Black Metal aesthetic, you must fight your instinct to make things sound "good." Roll off the low-end of your guitars. Turn up the splashy cymbals. Drench the shrieking vocals in the longest, darkest reverb you can find. The genre is not about technical perfection or punch; it is entirely about creating a cold, dark, and hypnotic atmosphere.
