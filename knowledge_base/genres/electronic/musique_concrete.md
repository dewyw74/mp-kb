## History

Musique concrète was developed around 1948 by French composer Pierre Schaeffer and his associates at the Studio d'Essai ("Experimental Studio") of French radio (RTF) in Paris. Schaeffer's earliest experiments involved recording real-world sounds — most famously bringing a disc recorder and microphone to the Batignolles train station — and manipulating the resulting recordings using variable-speed playback, reversal, and locked-groove looping on turntables, before the studio transitioned to magnetic tape as the primary medium. His 1948 "Cinq études de bruits" ("Five Studies of Noises") is considered the genre's founding work. In 1950, Schaeffer collaborated with composer Pierre Henry on the ten-movement "Symphonie pour un homme seul" ("Symphony for One Man Only"), the first major concrete composition, built primarily from manipulated recordings of the human voice and body. Schaeffer later formalized the movement's aesthetic theory in his 1966 "Traité des objets musicaux," introducing the concept of the objet sonore (sound object) and "reduced listening" (écoute réduite) — attending to a recorded sound's inherent acoustic qualities independent of its real-world source or cause. Musique concrète developed in parallel rivalry with the German elektronische Musik tradition (Stockhausen, WDR Cologne studio), which insisted on purely electronically generated tone rather than recorded/found sound; the two approaches would later converge and cross-pollinate. The genre's core technique — treating recorded sound as raw compositional material to be cut, looped, reversed, and layered — became foundational to virtually all subsequent sample-based and electronic music, directly influencing industrial music's tape-collage techniques, early hip-hop sampling, plunderphonics, and the granular-synthesis approaches central to modern IDM and sound design.

## Music Theory

Musique concrète operates entirely outside conventional tonal-harmonic theory, having emerged specifically as a rejection of the pitch-and-duration-based compositional systems (including serialism) dominant in mid-20th-century art music. Schaeffer's replacement framework organizes sound not by pitch class or scale but by the sound-object's morphology — its attack, sustain, grain, mass, and dynamic envelope — cataloguing these qualities as the genre's true "vocabulary." Where pitch does appear in a piece, it is typically an emergent byproduct of a recording's variable-speed manipulation rather than a chosen compositional starting point, meaning musique concrète has no functional concept of key, chord, or scale in the conventional sense.

## Arrangement

Structure in musique concrète is achieved through montage — the deliberate sequencing and layering of discrete recorded sound-objects — rather than through repeating song-form sections. Schaeffer and Henry's "Symphonie pour un homme seul" exemplifies the genre's structural approach: ten discrete movements, each built from a different family of manipulated vocal/body recordings, organized as a through-composed whole rather than a verse-chorus structure. Tension and climax are achieved by increasing the density and layering of sound-objects or accelerating the rate of montage cuts, and pieces frequently end on an abrupt cut or an isolated sound-object rather than a conventional musical cadence.

## Instruments

The genre's "instrument" is the recording and playback chain itself: initially a disc recorder and turntables capable of variable-speed and locked-groove playback, then magnetic tape recorders alongside custom devices built at Schaeffer's studio — the Phonogène (a variable-speed tape playback keyboard) and the Morphophone (a multi-head tape loop/delay device). Sound sources were recorded from the real world — trains, footsteps, prepared piano, human voice and breath — and treated as compositional material on equal footing with (and eventually in place of) conventional musical instruments.

## Sound Design

Sound design in musique concrète is inseparable from its editing process: variable-speed tape playback (altering pitch and duration together, since the two could not historically be manipulated independently), reversed playback, splicing and montage assembly, and echo-chamber reverb captured through a physical chamber rather than an electronic effect. The Morphophone's multiple tape-playback heads generated rhythmic repetition and delay effects directly from the tape medium, making the recording apparatus itself a primary compositional tool rather than a neutral capture device.

## MIDI Programming

The genre predates MIDI entirely and has no native sequencing practice; a modern producer working in its idiom replaces programmed note data with manipulated field recordings and sampled sound-objects triggered and edited within a sampler or audio editor, prioritizing manual, non-quantized placement of sound fragments in time over any grid-based rhythmic programming.

## Mixing

Mixing was accomplished through physical tape splicing and live mixing-desk fader performance during tape-to-tape transfer, since no automation or non-destructive editing existed. EQ and filtering were used to clarify and sculpt individual sound-objects so their distinct timbral identity remained legible within a dense montage, and echo-chamber reverb helped unify recordings captured in acoustically dissimilar real-world environments into a coherent sonic space.

## Mastering

There is no historical concept of loudness mastering in musique concrète — the practice predates loudness normalization standards by decades — and modern transfers of Schaeffer-era works preserve the wide dynamic range and era-authentic frequency limitations (tape hiss, limited high-frequency extension, disc surface noise) of the original recordings rather than applying contemporary loudness maximization.

## Reference Artists

Pierre Schaeffer is the genre's founder, with "Cinq études de bruits" (1948) as its foundational work; his collaboration with Pierre Henry on "Symphonie pour un homme seul" (1950) stands as the first major concrete composition and the piece most frequently cited as the genre's definitive early statement. Karlheinz Stockhausen represents the parallel, electronically-generated elektronische Musik tradition that both rivaled and eventually cross-pollinated with musique concrète. Luc Ferrari extended the tradition into the 1970s with a more naturalistic, less overtly manipulated use of environmental recording, exemplified by "Presque rien No. 1" (1970).

## Production Notes

The essential discipline of musique concrète is Schaeffer's concept of "reduced listening" — treating every recorded sound as an objet sonore defined by its own grain, envelope, and timbral character, independent of the real-world source that produced it — and building compositions from a deliberately assembled catalogue of such sound-objects rather than from synthesized tones or ready-made samples. Variable-speed playback and reversal should be used as primary transformative tools, and montage/editing treated as the genre's true compositional act, equivalent to what harmony and melody serve in conventional composition. A common failure mode is mistaking the genre for passive ambient field-recording atmosphere rather than the rigorously manipulated and assembled sound-object montage it actually is; another is over-cleaning source recordings in a way that removes the tape hiss and recording imperfections authentic to the era and medium. Modern producers can recreate the genre's techniques with a DAW sampler loaded with original field recordings for tape-style variable-speed and reversed playback, granular synthesis plugins as a direct extension of the sound-object concept, and multitrack DAW layering in place of the historical sound-on-sound tape transfer process.
