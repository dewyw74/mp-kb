## History

Halftime is a variant of drum and bass that crystallized as a distinct, named subgenre in the early 2010s, though isolated precedents for half-tempo drum and bass programming stretch back to the early 1990s, with tracks such as DJ Phantasy & DJ Gemini's "Switch To 33" (1992) sometimes cited as an early example of the underlying idea. The modern halftime movement drew together influences from neurofunk's dense sound design, dubstep's weight and space, wonky and the broader UK bass scene's unconventional rhythmic sensibility, and hip-hop's spacious drum programming, producing a style built around programming drum patterns that read as half-time (roughly 80-86 BPM) against an underlying full drum and bass tempo (160-172 BPM) rather than simply writing at a slower tempo outright. Producers and groups including Om Unit, Kahn, Pinch, Ivy Lab, dBridge, and Instra:mental (the latter two closely associated with the earlier, related "Autonomic" drum and bass movement) became key figures in developing and popularizing the sound through the 2010s, cementing halftime as a recognized, if still relatively specialized, corner of the broader drum and bass ecosystem.

## Music Theory

Halftime's harmonic approach is minimal, favoring static drones or pedal tones over developed chord progressions, consistent with drum and bass's broader tendency to subordinate harmony to rhythm and bass weight. What distinguishes halftime theoretically is its central rhythmic-perceptual device: writing a drum pattern that reads as a half-time pulse (around 80-86 BPM) against an underlying, unstated full drum and bass tempo, making tempo perception itself — rather than harmonic or even purely rhythmic complexity — the genre's defining compositional idea. Melodic material, where it appears, favors sparse, dissonant, or intentionally "wonky" phrases that reinforce this off-kilter character rather than smooth, extended melodic development in the manner of liquid drum and bass.

## Arrangement

A halftime track establishes its half-time pulse and atmospheric texture from the outset, immediately distinguishing it from both liquid DnB's continuous double-time roll and neurofunk's dense, fast breakbeat chopping. Verses stay groove-focused and often sparse, leaving deliberate space around the half-time hits in a more hip-hop-adjacent arrangement style than the busier verses typical of other drum and bass subgenres, and builds favor sub-bass swells and off-kilter percussion fills over accelerating breakbeat density. The drop delivers weighty, spaced-out half-time hits paired with a heavy, often unconventionally sound-designed sub-bass hit, prioritizing weight and space over continuous rolling energy or dense rhythmic complexity, and outros return to the sparse half-time pulse, often dissolving into atmosphere rather than looping a clean groove.

## Instruments

Drums are sparse and weighty, programmed at roughly half the rhythmic density of standard drum and bass breakbeats and often carrying hip-hop-influenced swing and space rather than a continuously chopped, rolling break. Bass is heavy and frequently distorted or unconventionally sound-designed, generally more experimental and "quirky" in tone than neurofunk's aggressive but controlled growl-bass or liquid's warm, clean sub-bass approach. Synths and pads lean dissonant, textural, or lo-fi, favoring atmosphere and unconventional timbre over neurofunk's polished, evolving synthesis or liquid's jazzy warmth, and melodic instrumentation of any kind remains a secondary concern throughout.

## Sound Design

Halftime's sound design centers on unconventional, deliberately "wonky" bass and drum textures — irregular, non-symmetrical modulation on bass tones (distinct from neurofunk's more controlled, aggressive modulation) and hip-hop-influenced drum sample chopping combined with drum-and-bass-scale sub-bass weight. Heavy distortion and saturation on sub-bass provide weight and texture, while lo-fi/tape-style degradation effects, borrowed from hip-hop production sensibility, contribute to the genre's atmospheric, less-polished character. The single essential production technique is programming a half-time drum pattern against a standard 160-172 BPM drum and bass tempo — this tempo-perception relationship is what actually defines the genre, more than any specific synthesis method.

## MIDI Programming

The genre's defining programming technique is writing a drum pattern that reads as half-time (roughly an 80-86 BPM feel) against an underlying full drum and bass tempo of 160-172 BPM, producing a spacious, weighty groove clearly distinct from liquid's continuous double-time roll and neurofunk's dense, fast breakbeat chopping. Bass patterns are typically simpler and more sustained than neurofunk's rhythmically complex growl-bass sequences but louder and heavier than liquid's smoother sub, often carrying unconventional, off-kilter accents. A degree of hip-hop-influenced swing or looseness is common on half-time patterns, distinct from both liquid's smooth, precisely rolled breaks and neurofunk's mechanically tight, aggressive precision.

## Mixing

Sub-bass is isolated below roughly 100-120 Hz as in all drum and bass, but halftime's sparse half-time drum hits leave considerably more mix space than the density-driven approach neurofunk or liquid require for their busier breakbeats. Heavy compression and distortion on sub-bass and kick deliver weight and impact, while overall dynamics stay more spacious than neurofunk's dense, compressed wall of rhythm; reverb is generous on atmospheric textures and breakdowns but drier and more direct in the weighty drop sections, in contrast to liquid's consistently lush reverb treatment. Sub-bass stays mono and centered as in all drum and bass, while atmospheric textures and off-kilter percussion elements spread wider for the genre's spacious character.

## Mastering

Halftime masters typically sit around -9 to -7 LUFS integrated, preserving more dynamic range between sparse and weighty sections than neurofunk's consistently dense, compressed character, though drop sections still hit with significant impact. Frequency balance emphasizes deep, heavy, often distorted sub-bass and forward kick presence, with an overall tonal character leaning darker and more unconventional or lo-fi than liquid's warm clarity or neurofunk's aggressive but clean modern polish.

## Reference Artists

Om Unit is among the most frequently cited figures in halftime's development and popularization through the 2010s. Ivy Lab's catalog and associated label output represent a defining, dedicated halftime aesthetic, while dBridge and Instra:mental's earlier "Autonomic"-era drum and bass work is closely associated with the spacious, atmospheric sensibility that halftime built upon. Kahn and Pinch's early-2010s catalog further represents the genre's crossover with dubstep-adjacent weight and space.

## Production Notes

Program the drum pattern at a genuine half-time feel against the underlying full drum and bass tempo — this specific tempo-perception relationship, not simply "slow drum and bass," is what actually defines the genre and should be the first design decision made. Lean into unconventional, "wonky" bass sound design and off-kilter percussion rather than smoothing patterns into liquid's clean roll or neurofunk's aggressive precision, and draw deliberately on hip-hop's spacious drum programming sensibility, since halftime's identity depends specifically on this cross-genre hybrid feel. The most common mistakes are simply slowing down a standard drum and bass breakbeat without addressing the half-time-versus-full-tempo relationship (losing the genre's core trick), over-polishing the bass and drum sound design in a way that contradicts halftime's deliberately unconventional aesthetic, and conflating halftime with either liquid DnB's smooth continuous roll or neurofunk's dense breakbeat chopping when its spacious arrangement is a distinct design choice from both.
