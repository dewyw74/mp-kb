## History

Miami soul developed in the late 1960s and early 1970s around Miami's Overtown and Liberty City neighborhoods and, crucially, around producer and label owner Henry Stone, whose TK Records (founded with Steve Alaimo in 1972 in Hialeah, Florida) and its sister labels (Alston, Glades) became the era's defining regional hit factory. Stone's operation built stars out of Betty Wright ("Clean Up Woman," 1971), Timmy Thomas ("Why Can't We Live Together," 1972), and siblings George and Gwen McCrae, cultivating a house sound built on gritty, gospel-rooted vocals, syncopated funk rhythm sections, and Latin percussion drawn from Miami's Cuban and Bahamian communities. George McCrae's TK single "Rock Your Baby" topped the Billboard Hot 100 in May 1974 and is widely cited as one of the earliest disco records, marking the moment the Miami soul sound began evolving directly into disco — a transition completed commercially by TK houseband members Harry Wayne "KC" Casey and Richard Finch, who formed KC and the Sunshine Band and scored five Billboard No. 1 singles through the mid-to-late 1970s.

## Music Theory

Miami soul harmony is straightforward — simple I-IV-V changes or vamped one- and two-chord grooves in the funk tradition — with musical interest carried by the syncopated rhythm section and Latin percussion rather than harmonic complexity. Minor-key vamps under gritty, testifying vocals (Betty Wright, Clarence Reid) sit alongside brighter major-key material as the sound moves toward its later disco-crossover phase.

## Arrangement

Tracks typically open on a syncopated bass, percussion, or organ hook, keep verses lean and groove-focused, and build hooks through the interlocking rhythm section and backing vocals rather than a dramatic string-driven crescendo. Instrumental breaks spotlight congas, horns, or organ, and outros favor an extended rhythmic vamp — the same continuous-groove impulse that TK's later disco records would push even further.

## Instruments

The TK house rhythm section (anchored by bassist/producer Richard Finch) and Lowrey/Hammond organ carry the harmonic bed, with congas and Latin hand percussion — a direct fingerprint of Miami's Cuban and Bahamian communities — providing the genre's distinctive groove. Notably, Timmy Thomas's 1972 hit "Why Can't We Live Together" replaced a full drum kit with an early rhythm box, a stripped-down production choice years ahead of drum machines becoming common in soul production elsewhere.

## Sound Design

The genre's core period (1967-1974) uses no synthesizers; its sonic identity comes from warm, small-studio recording at TK's Hialeah facility, natural organ vibrato, and the percussive articulation of live congas. Synthesizers entered only as the sound transitioned toward disco after 1974.

## MIDI Programming

Program a syncopated, funk-rooted bass line locked to a lean drum pattern — consider a sparse rhythm-box feel for period authenticity, following Timmy Thomas's example — and treat conga and percussion patterns as a co-equal melodic-rhythmic layer rather than mere texture. Keep the feel loose and human; Latin percussion in particular should never sit on a rigid grid.

## Mixing

EQ should stay warm and vocal-forward while giving congas and hand percussion enough top-end presence to cut cleanly, with light-to-moderate compression preserving the TK house sound's natural dynamics rather than heavy uniform loudness. Reverb should reflect a warm, small-studio character, and the stereo image should stay modest, giving percussion, horns, and organ individual space around a centered vocal and bass.

## Mastering

Modern streaming or reissue masters typically target -11 to -9 LUFS integrated, preserving the moderate dynamic range of the era's independent Southern studio mastering, with a warm low end and a present, natural vocal-and-percussion midrange.

## Reference Artists

Betty Wright's "Clean Up Woman" (1971) and Timmy Thomas's stripped-down "Why Can't We Live Together" (1972) define the genre's gritty, organ-and-percussion-driven core, while George McCrae's "Rock Your Baby" (1974) marks the pivot point toward disco. Gwen McCrae and Clarence Reid round out the TK Records roster central to the sound.

## Production Notes

Study Henry Stone's TK Records operation as the genre's central production hub — a label-driven regional sound comparable to Motown or Stax — and don't skip the Latin percussion, which is what most distinguishes Miami soul's groove from other Southern soul scenes. Avoid conflating Miami soul with disco outright: it is the grittier, funk-and-soul-rooted regional scene that predates and directly seeds the fully synthesized, continuous-groove national disco sound, with "Rock Your Baby" and KC and the Sunshine Band marking the hinge between the two eras.
