## History

Indian classical music traces its roots to ancient Vedic chant and devotional traditions, developing over centuries into two related but distinct classical traditions — Hindustani (North Indian), shaped significantly by Persian and Islamic court music influences, and Carnatic (South Indian), which retained a more direct continuity with earlier Sanskrit musical treatises — each with its own repertoire, terminology, and performance conventions. Figures like Ravi Shankar (sitar) and Ali Akbar Khan (sarod) brought Hindustani classical music to major international audiences from the 1950s-60s onward, including influential collaborations with Western musicians (The Beatles, John Coltrane, and later Zakir Hussain's fusion work) that opened significant cross-cultural musical exchange.

## Music Theory

Indian classical music's theoretical foundation is entirely distinct from Western harmony: raga governs melody (a complex framework combining a specific scale, characteristic ascending/descending phrases, ornamentation rules, and even traditional time-of-day or seasonal associations), while tala governs rhythm (a cyclical beat framework, such as the 16-beat teentaal, divided into specific sub-sections). There is no chord progression in the Western sense — a tanpura drone (typically sustaining the tonic and fifth) provides the only continuous harmonic reference, with all musical development happening through melodic and rhythmic improvisation within the raga and tala's rules.

## Arrangement

A traditional performance unfolds in a well-defined arc: the alap (a slow, completely unmetered exploration of the raga's melodic character) opens the performance, followed by the introduction of rhythmic pulse (jor/jod) and then a metered composition (gat) within the tala, over which extensive improvisation develops in both speed and complexity. The tihai — a rhythmic phrase repeated exactly three times, landing precisely on the tala cycle's first beat — provides recurring structural punctuation, while the jhala (a fast, climactic closing passage) brings the performance to its conclusion.

## Instruments

Tabla (Hindustani) or mridangam (Carnatic) provide the rhythmic tala foundation with extraordinary timbral and rhythmic sophistication, while a tanpura sustains a continuous drone throughout the performance as the tradition's sole harmonic reference. Melodic instruments — sitar, sarod, bansuri flute, violin (adapted for Carnatic practice), or the voice itself — carry the raga's melodic content, often accompanied by harmonium in Hindustani vocal performance.

## Sound Design

As with other purely acoustic world-music traditions, Indian classical music's expressive character comes entirely from vocal and instrumental technique rather than electronic processing — meend/gamaka (continuous pitch glides and oscillations between notes) is the tradition's defining melodic-expressive device, central to both vocal and instrumental raga performance. Traditional recordings preserve the continuous tanpura drone and fine ornamental detail through close, natural miking rather than studio effects processing.

## MIDI Programming

Melodic programming must follow the specific raga's ascending/descending note rules and characteristic phrases, with extensive pitch-bend/glide programming essential to capture the meend ornamentation central to authentic character — standard fixed-pitch MIDI approaches are fundamentally inadequate to this tradition. Rhythmic (tabla/mridangam) programming must follow the specific tala's cyclical beat structure rather than any Western time signature, and the tanpura drone should be programmed as a continuous, unchanging harmonic reference rather than a rhythmically active bass part.

## Mixing

Mixing should stay natural and warm, preserving the tanpura's continuous harmonic presence and the lead instrument or voice's full ornamental detail, with minimal compression to protect the tradition's very wide dynamic range across a full alap-to-jhala performance arc. Natural room ambience and close, natural ensemble placement reflect the tradition's small-group performance format far better than heavily processed or artificially widened stereo treatment.

## Mastering

Masters preserve very wide dynamic range, typically -16 to -13 LUFS integrated, essential to the tradition's full structural and emotional arc from the alap's hushed exploration to the jhala's driving climax, with a warm, natural frequency balance supporting the tanpura's harmonic richness and the lead performer's fine ornamental detail.

## Reference Artists

Ravi Shankar's "Raga Jog" and Ali Akbar Khan's "Raga Marwa" represent Hindustani classical music's sitar and sarod traditions respectively at their most internationally influential; M.S. Subbulakshmi's "Kurai Onrum Illai" showcases Carnatic classical vocal tradition; Zakir Hussain's collaborative fusion work demonstrates the tradition's continued cross-cultural relevance and evolution.

## Production Notes

Understand that raga and tala are the tradition's true organizing principles, entirely replacing Western key/scale and time-signature frameworks — never force Western harmonic thinking onto this material. Preserve the tanpura drone as a continuous, unbroken harmonic reference, and give pitch ornamentation (meend/gamaka) genuine expressive priority, since it is as central to the tradition's melodic identity as the raga's note choices themselves. Avoid applying Western chord concepts to raga-based melody or quantizing tabla/mridangam rhythms to a Western time signature — both fundamentally misrepresent how this music actually works.
