## History

Minimalism emerged in 1960s New York and California as composers including Terry Riley, Steve Reich, and Philip Glass rejected serialism's dense complexity in favor of radically simplified, repetitive musical material, drawing inspiration from non-Western cyclical traditions like Balinese gamelan and West African drumming. Riley's "In C" (1964) and Reich's tape-phasing experiments established the genre's core techniques, which Reich and Glass then developed into a mature, highly influential concert-music practice through the 1970s and beyond, with John Adams later extending minimalist technique into large-scale orchestral and operatic writing.

## Music Theory

Minimalist harmony is deliberately static or very slowly evolving — simple diatonic or modal chords sustained over long durations, functioning as a stable harmonic canvas for rhythmic and textural process rather than a vehicle for tonal drama. Where harmonic change occurs, it typically happens through gradual, additive processes (adding one new pitch to a pattern at a time) rather than functional cadential movement.

## Arrangement

Minimalist works are frequently process-based rather than divided into discrete movements — a single continuous section undergoes gradual transformation (phasing, additive/subtractive variation) over an extended duration, with structural interest generated by the accumulation and resolution of rhythmic complexity rather than a traditional exposition-development-recapitulation arc.

## Instruments

Mallet percussion (marimba, vibraphone), piano, and electric organ frequently carry the genre's core repeating patterns, with synthesizers and sequencers becoming increasingly central from the 1970s onward. Ensemble size ranges from Reich's and Glass's dedicated small chamber ensembles to full symphony orchestra in later, larger-scale minimalist and post-minimalist works.

## Sound Design

Tape delay and looping (Riley's "time-lag accumulator") historically enabled the genre's signature phasing technique — two identical patterns performed at very slightly different tempos, gradually drifting in and out of alignment — which remains minimalism's single most influential compositional device. Modern digital looping and sequencing tools recreate or extend this technique with far greater timing reliability than the original tape-based methods.

## MIDI Programming

Repeating patterns must be programmed with extremely precise, steady timing, especially in phase-based works where the entire compositional effect depends on carefully controlled (or carefully drifting) rhythmic relationships between parts. Humanization should generally be minimal, since the genre's hypnotic effect depends on steady, often deliberately mechanical-sounding execution rather than expressive rubato.

## Mixing

Mixing must keep interlocking rhythmic patterns individually distinguishable even at high pattern density, using clean EQ, light compression, and moderate (not excessive) reverb that would otherwise blur the rhythmic clarity central to the style. Spreading patterns across the stereo field helps the ear track individual layers within a dense, highly repetitive texture.

## Mastering

Masters preserve moderate, consistent dynamics (typically -16 to -14 LUFS integrated) supporting the genre's steady, hypnotic character, with a clean, balanced frequency profile that prioritizes rhythmic and textural clarity over tonal drama.

## Reference Artists

Terry Riley's "In C" is the genre's foundational open-form work; Steve Reich's "Music for 18 Musicians" exemplifies mature phase-based ensemble minimalism; Philip Glass's "Glassworks" showcases the genre's more melodically accessible, arpeggio-driven strand; John Adams' "Shaker Loops" demonstrates minimalist technique applied to string orchestra with a more expressively varied, post-minimalist sensibility.

## Production Notes

Treat the compositional process itself — phasing, additive or subtractive pattern variation — as the actual musical substance rather than mere repetition of a fixed melody, and keep execution extremely steady and precise, particularly in phase-based works where rhythmic drift (unless deliberate) undermines the intended effect. Use the stereo field deliberately to help distinguish interlocking pattern layers, and avoid excessive dynamic shaping or reverb, both of which work against the genre's steady, hypnotic, process-driven character.
