## History

Mathcore developed during the 1990s as a fusion of hardcore punk and metalcore with math rock's odd meters, grindcore's extremity, post-hardcore's structural elasticity, and noise rock's abrasion. Before the genre had its current name, the style was referred to as "chaotic hardcore" or "noisecore." Botch, formed in 1993 in Tacoma, Washington by guitarist Dave Knudson, bassist Brian Cook, drummer Tim Latona, and vocalist Dave Verellen, quickly challenged conventional hardcore structures with intricate compositions. The term "mathcore" itself was coined around the release of The Dillinger Escape Plan's 1999 debut album Calculating Infinity — the band, formed in 1997 in Morris Plains, New Jersey, released the record the same year Botch released We Are the Romans, and both albums are now Hall of Fame-inducted and regarded as landmark releases that established the genre's template.

## Music Theory

Mathcore largely abandons conventional harmonic thinking, with early bands playing almost completely atonal, polyphonically dissonant guitar parts. Odd time signatures (7/8, 11/16, 5/4) shift abruptly within songs, compounded by polymeters that layer conflicting rhythmic ratios (such as 3:2 or 5:4) over a primary pulse, producing dense, disorienting, overlapping grooves. Tension and interest come entirely from rhythmic unpredictability and intervallic dissonance rather than any conventional chord progression or harmonic center.

## Arrangement

Songs resist conventional verse/chorus form almost entirely, favoring a chaotic, whirlwind structure of sudden stops, start-stop riffing, and abrupt tempo or meter shifts. Riffs rarely repeat after a single section — the genre's sense of "build" comes from structural unpredictability itself rather than a conventional layering or intensity increase — and where a hook-like moment occurs, it typically emerges from a sudden stop or an unexpectedly locked-in groove functioning as release rather than a repeated melodic hook.

## Instruments

Complex, polymetric drum patterns are central to the genre, with pioneering mathcore drummers frequently drawing on jazz, orchestral, or academic musical backgrounds to execute rhythms that appear mathematically impossible at speed. Frantic, dissonant, often near-atonal guitar work — high, screeching notes and polyphonic dissonance — forms the genre's core instrumental voice, with bass typically interlocking with the guitar's rapidly shifting riffs rather than holding a stable, independent rhythmic anchor.

## Sound Design

Mathcore's sonic identity comes almost entirely from aggressive guitar distortion and genuinely difficult, precisely-executed performance rather than studio effects or processing. Pioneering releases in the genre are noted for production that stays raw and immediate — "crystal clear but with just enough of a raw edge" — with the constantly shifting meter and tempo functioning as the genre's real structural "modulation" device.

## MIDI Programming

Program constantly shifting odd time signatures and polymetric layers rather than settling into a predictable, repeating groove — unpredictability is the genre's central rhythmic value. Bass should interlock tightly with the guitar's dissonant, rapidly shifting riffs, and riff-writing should be treated as through-composed rather than cyclical, since riffs rarely repeat after a single section. Performance should read as tight, precise, controlled chaos rather than loose or sloppy — the genre's disorientation is compositional, not a looseness effect.

## Mixing

Keep guitar midrange present and aggressive for riff clarity amid constant rhythmic and harmonic shifts, and give drums clear definition so complex polymetric patterns remain legible. Moderate, punchy compression supports aggression without smoothing over the genre's raw, sometimes deliberately harsh edge, and reverb should stay minimal, favoring a dry, immediate, in-your-face character over spaciousness.

## Mastering

Masters target roughly -9 to -7 LUFS integrated, loud and aggressive while preserving enough transient clarity for complex, rapid riff and drum patterns to remain distinguishable rather than collapsing into noise. The frequency balance favors an aggressive guitar midrange for riff definition, a controlled low end, and enough high-end clarity for cymbal and drum-pattern legibility.

## Reference Artists

The Dillinger Escape Plan's "43% Burnt" (1999, from Calculating Infinity, ranked by Rolling Stone among the 97th greatest metal songs of all time) and Botch's We Are the Romans (1999) — both Hall of Fame-inducted albums released the same year — stand as the genre's defining, template-setting recordings. Converge and Coalesce round out the genre's most influential pioneering acts.

## Production Notes

- Write through-composed riffs that rarely repeat, rather than cyclical song structures.
- Layer polymeters (e.g., 3:2 or 5:4 ratios) over a primary pulse rather than staying in a single time signature throughout a section.
- Use sudden stops and abrupt start-stop riffing as structural punctuation, creating tension-and-release without conventional chorus repetition.
- Prioritize genuinely difficult, precisely-executed performance — the genre's chaos should read as controlled virtuosity.
- Avoid settling into a predictable groove for too long or confusing looseness with the genre's intentional, precisely-composed chaos.
- Modern hybrid approach: use extensive tempo/time-signature automation for accurate meter shifts, and keep production relatively raw and immediate to preserve the genre's aggressive character.
