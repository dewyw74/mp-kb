# Drift (Ableton Live)

Drift is Ableton Live's lightweight, first-party subtractive/wavetable-hybrid synth introduced in Live 12, positioned as the fastest, lowest-CPU-cost way to sketch a synth idea inside Live. Unlike Wavetable, Operator, or Analog — each aimed at meaningful sound-design depth — Drift's design goal is speed and approachability, and it's included with every edition of Live rather than being gated to Suite.

## Sound Character and Strengths

Drift's modeled analog-style oscillator character gives it more warmth than a purely clean digital oscillator, without the deeper per-component modeling (and correspondingly higher CPU cost) of Analog. Its very low CPU footprint and fast, simple interface make it the practical default for quickly sketching a bassline, chord part, or lead idea before deciding whether the track actually needs a more elaborately designed patch — a workflow role none of Ableton's other stock synths are specifically optimized for.

## Weaknesses

Drift's simplicity is a real ceiling: it doesn't offer Wavetable's custom wavetable content, Operator's FM depth, or Analog's dual-signal-path modeling flexibility. A patch that needs genuine sound-design depth will need to graduate to one of those other instruments (or a third-party synth) rather than staying in Drift. Being newer than Ableton's other stock synths, it also has less established patch-recipe documentation and third-party preset content built up around it.

## Choosing Drift Over Ableton's Other Stock Synths

Drift is the right tool specifically for fast sketching and simple, low-CPU-cost background parts — not for a track's featured, sound-design-critical elements. Once an idea sketched in Drift proves out, the practical next step is often rebuilding the part in Wavetable, Analog, or a third-party synth for the deeper control a finished patch usually needs.

## Common Mistakes

**Treating Drift as a full replacement for Wavetable or Analog on a sound-design-critical patch.** Its simplicity is a deliberate tradeoff for speed — a track's featured lead or bass patch usually needs more depth than Drift is built to provide.
