## History

Franz Liszt invented both the term and the form of the symphonic poem, using thematic transformation to parallel the emotional arc of an extra-musical program in works such as *Les Préludes*, composed in 1848 and first performed under that title in Weimar in 1854; Liszt first publicly used the term "symphonische Dichtung" (symphonic poem) at a Weimar concert on 23 February 1854. Building on Hector Berlioz's earlier programmatic symphony, Liszt established the single-movement, narrative-driven orchestral form as a legitimate vehicle for literary and philosophical subject matter outside opera or ballet. Both Richard Strauss and Jean Sibelius were directly influenced by Liszt's symphonic poems and developed the genre further in their own distinct directions: Strauss's *Also sprach Zarathustra* (1896), after Nietzsche, pursued vivid philosophical and pictorial orchestration, while Sibelius's *Finlandia* (1899-1900) channeled the form toward Finnish nationalist expression.

## Music Theory

The symphonic poem's defining harmonic-formal device is thematic transformation: a single musical idea is reshaped in harmony, rhythm, and orchestration across the work to represent different states of a protagonist, idea, or landscape, rather than the contrasting, independently developed themes of sonata form. Because the music tracks a continuous extra-musical narrative, harmony develops continuously and avoids the periodic, symmetrical cadences of Classical-era form, with the work's overall large-scale key scheme frequently chosen to mirror the program's own emotional or narrative arc.

## Arrangement

Symphonic poems unfold as a single continuous movement rather than a multi-movement symphonic structure, opening by establishing the program's central subject before developing it through continuous thematic transformation as the narrative progresses. Climaxes arrive as the theme's most dramatically reshaped statement, corresponding to the program's central dramatic event, and contrasting episodes representing different characters or narrative turns appear as shifts in orchestration, tempo, or thematic guise within the same unbroken span.

## Instruments

A full, often expanded Romantic orchestra is deployed specifically for its pictorial and narrative capacity — full percussion battery for depicted storms or battles, weighty low strings and brass intensifying at dramatic climaxes — with Strauss's orchestration for works like *Also sprach Zarathustra* and *Don Juan* prized precisely for its vivid, narrative-driven tone-painting rather than abstract orchestral color.

## Sound Design

"Sound design" in this genre is achieved entirely through orchestration choice — instrument, register, and doubling selected specifically for what a passage depicts in the underlying program — functioning as the direct historical ancestor of modern film-score tone-painting technique. Thematic transformation across harmony, rhythm, and orchestration serves as the genre's primary structural-coloristic device.

## MIDI Programming

Program a single core theme and its successive transformations — rhythmic augmentation or diminution, harmonic reharmonization, changing register and instrumental color — rather than introducing new, unrelated melodic material, since recognizing the same theme across its various guises is central to how these works communicate their program. Low strings and brass should intensify specifically at the program's dramatic high points, and substantial rubato tracking the unfolding narrative is essential to avoid rigid, metronomic results.

## Mixing

Mixing should maintain full-spectrum orchestral balance with particular clarity for the core theme's recurring appearances, so a listener can track its transformation across the work, while keeping compression minimal to preserve the genre's wide, programmatically-driven dynamic range. Wide, full-orchestra stereo imaging supports the genre's large-scale pictorial ambitions.

## Mastering

Masters should preserve high dynamic range (roughly -15 to -12 LUFS integrated) to support the contrast between intimate and overwhelming programmatic states that is central to the genre's storytelling function, with a full-range, richly balanced frequency profile supporting the complete orchestral palette.

## Reference Artists

Liszt's *Les Préludes* (1854) is the genre's foundational work and clearest demonstration of thematic transformation technique; Strauss's *Also sprach Zarathustra* (1896) and *Don Juan* (1888) showcase the form's capacity for vivid philosophical and character-driven orchestration; Sibelius's *Finlandia* (1899-1900) demonstrates the genre's use for nationalist and landscape-driven expression outside the German tradition.

## Production Notes

Choose or invent a clear extra-musical program and let its narrative structure directly drive the work's continuous single-movement form, building the piece around thematic transformation of one central idea rather than multiple unrelated themes. Avoid treating "symphonic poem" as merely a descriptive title for any orchestral work — the genre specifically requires this continuous, narrative-driven, thematic-transformation-based structure — and study Strauss's orchestration directly for models of how instrumentation choices can serve pictorial and narrative ends.
