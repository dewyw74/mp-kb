## History

Colour bass was coined around 2016 by Leeds-based producer Chime (Harvey Goldfinch) to describe his own music, which sat between the supersaw-driven melodic dubstep of artists like Seven Lions and Illenium and the heavier, more aggressive dubstep sound of the era — aiming to combine the rich tonality and musicality of the melodic side with the aggression and impact of the heavier side. Chime founded the Rushdown label and collective in 2016 as a home for emerging melodic bass producers, and the term was adopted first by other Rushdown artists before spreading more widely across the bass-music scene. Rushdown's *Colour Bass* compilation series (Volume 1 in 2020, followed by Volume 2 and Volume 3, alongside a parallel *New Colours* compilation series) gave the style a recognizable, dedicated home and helped establish a roster of associated producers. From 2020 onward, the term and approach crossed into more mainstream bass-music production, picked up by established artists such as Virtual Riot, Flux Pavilion, and Au5, and by the early 2020s colour bass had crystallized as a recognizable microgenre within the broader dubstep/bass scene, with Chime himself later releasing dedicated sound-design education (a "Fundamentals of Colour Bass Sound Design" masterclass) and, with collaborator Ace Aura, a "Melodic Riddim: Colour Bass" sample pack documenting the style's core techniques. Rushdown was active as a label through the end of 2022.

## Music Theory

Colour bass restores genuine, often bright major-key harmony to dubstep's rhythmic and bass-weight framework — I-IV-V-I progressions colored with add9 and maj7 tones, and a notable fondness for Lydian #11 movement, giving the genre its cinematic, "colourful" quality, in contrast to riddim and much of brostep's harmonically static, minor-key minimalism. Minor-key variants (i-VI-III-VII) exist and lean closer to riddim's darker character, but even these retain more harmonic motion than riddim's typical single-chord vamps. The genre's central theoretical idea is the fusion of melody, harmony, and bass into one instrument — the same "chord-lead" concept future bass popularized — but written so that voice can also carry the growled, aggressive character inherited from brostep and riddim.

## Arrangement

A colour bass track typically opens with a cinematic chord or piano motif stated early and melodically complete, in the future-bass tradition of front-loading the hook rather than withholding it for the drop. Builds incorporate the chord progression into the riser and snare-roll vocabulary, signaling the melodic payoff to come. The drop is frequently structured as a "switch-up" between two contrasting sections — a melodic passage where the colour-bass voice sings a cinematic hook, and an aggressive passage where the same or a related patch growls — and this alternation (or, in more advanced writing, layering) between the two timbral worlds is the genre's signature structural device. Breakdowns return to the emotional chord motif before a final drop that often combines both bass characters, and outros typically resolve musically rather than ending as a pure DJ tool.

## Instruments

The drum pattern occupies dubstep and riddim's half-time rhythmic space, with a strong snare/clap on beat 3 and dense hi-hat/trap-style rolls, occasionally opening into four-on-the-floor sections during more melodic passages for extra lift. The defining instrument is the colour-bass voice itself — a wavetable/FM patch built to move between a bright, vowel-rich melodic "chord-lead bass" state and a harsher, formant-distorted growl state. Supersaw chord stabs, glassy plucks, and sparkling arpeggios build the genre's prismatic texture around that central voice, and piano is frequently used in intros and breakdowns for emotional, cinematic motifs.

## Sound Design

The core sound-design discipline is building a single bass-lead patch (typically in Serum, Vital, or Pigments) using wavetable and FM synthesis that can move between vowel/formant-rich melodic tones and growled aggression via automated filter cutoff, formant-shift automation, and subtle FM-amount automation — this "talking bass" morph is the genre's most identifying signature. Chord stabs and pads use detuned supersaw/wavetable layering with lush reverb and wide chorus for glossy width, deliberately pulled back on the growl-bass sections to keep them forward and impactful by contrast. Layering multiple detuned instances of the colour-bass patch with slightly offset automation builds a thicker, more three-dimensional voice.

## MIDI Programming

The colour-bass voice is programmed with real melodic content — pitch, filter, and formant automation carrying a hummable line — rather than the largely static texture-first bass writing typical of riddim. Drum programming stays in half-time kick/snare/clap territory with dense hi-hat rolls, shifting to four-on-the-floor during melodic sections. Melodic writing is built as genuine call-and-response, alternating a bright motif with a harsher growl answer within the same section, and velocity is programmed for maximum punch on drop hits while staying more expressive and dynamic through melodic and breakdown passages.

## Mixing

The melodic colour-bass voice and any secondary growl bass are carved into distinct frequency pockets so both stay intelligible, with clean sub-bass kept separate beneath both. Lush, wide reverb builds the genre's glossy character on chord stabs and pads, pulled back sharply during growl-bass sections to preserve forward impact — a clear contrast to riddim and brostep's consistently drier, more aggressive mix aesthetic. Formant and filter automation on the bass voice functions as the primary expressive mixing tool, working alongside width/reverb automation to distinguish melodic sections from aggressive ones.

## Mastering

Colour bass masters sit around -8 to -6 LUFS integrated, mastered for festival, club, and streaming competitiveness similar to modern dubstep and riddim, while retaining enough transient detail for the bass voice's morph between melodic and growled states to read clearly. Frequency balance favors a wide, present midrange for the chord-lead/colour-bass voice, a clean and controlled sub, and a bright but controlled top end that supports the reverb-heavy melodic sections without introducing harshness into the growl sections.

## Reference Artists

Chime, the producer who coined the term and founded Rushdown, remains the genre's foundational reference — his collaboration with Skybreak, "Pyroclastic," exemplifies the melodic-to-aggressive bass-voice contrast at the genre's core. Rushdown's *Colour Bass, Vol. 1* compilation (2020) documents the style's early crystallization across a roster of associated producers, and artist Ace Aura is closely associated with Chime in both production collaboration and sound-design education around the style.

## Production Notes

Design the bass voice as a single instrument capable of two states — melodic/vowel and aggressive/growl — using formant and filter automation to morph between them, rather than building two unrelated patches and switching between them; that morph is what makes a track read as colour bass rather than generic melodic dubstep. Write a genuine, singable chord-lead motif before adding aggression, since the genre's identity depends on real harmonic and melodic content the way future bass's does, not on texture alone the way riddim's does. Use the melodic-versus-aggressive contrast as the core structural drama of the drop. Common mistakes include writing a static, unautomated growl bass and simply layering a chord pad on top (producing melodic dubstep with an aggressive bass bolted on, not a genuinely fused colour-bass voice), neglecting Lydian/major-key cinematic harmony in favor of purely minor-key riddim vamps, and overusing reverb/chorus on growl-bass sections in a way that softens the impact the genre is specifically designed to retain.
