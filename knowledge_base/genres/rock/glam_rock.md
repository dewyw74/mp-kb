## History

Glam rock emerged in the United Kingdom around 1970-1971, led by Marc Bolan's T. Rex, who transitioned from acoustic psychedelic folk into electrified, boogie-fuzz-driven rock and roll paired with androgynous, glitter-and-satin stage costuming. David Bowie's Ziggy Stardust persona (1972) brought glam rock's theatrical, gender-bending performance art to a mass audience, while Slade, Sweet, and Roxy Music each contributed their own strand — from Slade's football-terrace stomp anthems to Roxy Music's art-school avant-pop. In the United States, the New York Dolls channeled glam's flamboyance through a rawer, proto-punk lens that would directly influence the punk rock movement later in the decade. Glam rock's theatricality, makeup, and larger-than-life stage persona later resurfaced in the 1980s "glam metal" (hair metal) scene, a distinct but lineage-connected phenomenon built on hard rock's heavier sound rather than glam rock's original boogie-pop foundation.

## Music Theory

Glam rock harmony stays deliberately simple and immediate — I-IV-V and Mixolydian I-bVII-IV progressions in bright major keys, favoring big, hooky, shout-along vocal melodies over blues-derived complexity or extended improvisation. The emphasis is on the hook: a simple, repeated vocal phrase (frequently the song's title) delivered with maximum charisma and theatrical flair, riding a stomping, handclap-reinforced groove. Where hard rock's harmonic ambiguity comes from power chords, glam rock frequently uses full major triads and unison guitar/bass lines, giving it a brighter, poppier harmonic character distinct from its heavier rock siblings.

## Arrangement

Songs open with an instantly memorable riff or the genre-defining "stomp" groove — a big, emphatic kick-kick-snare-clap pattern — designed to grab attention within seconds. Verses ride this groove with theatrical, half-sung/half-shouted vocal delivery, moving quickly (via a short pre-chorus lift) into a big, gang-vocal or heavily doubled chorus built around a simple, maximally repeatable hook. Guitar solos are short and melodic rather than extended technical showcases, prioritizing catchiness over display, and outros typically repeat the chorus hook with ad-libbed vocal flourishes toward a dramatic, theatrical ending.

## Instruments

The stomping drum groove — big kick and snare reinforced with handclaps or tambourine — is glam rock's rhythmic signature, distinct from both hard rock's power and blues rock's shuffle. Electric guitar delivers fuzzed, boogie-driven riffs (Marc Bolan's T. Rex tone is the genre's archetype) that are simpler and more repetitive than hard rock riffing, built for catchiness rather than technical display. Bass locks tightly to the stomp groove with simple, driving root-fifth patterns, often doubling the guitar riff. Piano glissandos and rhythmic chordal stabs, along with real or synthesized string arrangements, add theatrical drama to choruses in the genre's more produced records.

## Sound Design

Glam rock's guitar tone centers on a fuzzed, boogie-woogie midrange bite — brighter and less scooped than hard rock's high-gain aggression — achieved through fuzz pedals pushing a moderately driven amp. Flanger and phaser effects give guitar parts a slightly psychedelic, glam sheen distinctive to the era. Vocal production leans heavily on doubling and artificial double-tracking (ADT) to create a bigger, glossier lead vocal, reinforced with plate reverb for a larger-than-life theatrical presence. Handclaps and tambourine layering function almost as a sound-design element in their own right, core to constructing the genre's signature stomp groove.

## MIDI Programming

Program the "glam stomp" first: an emphatic kick-kick-snare pattern with handclap or tambourine layered on the backbeat, kept big, simple, and instantly recognizable rather than rhythmically intricate. Bass should play simple, driving root-fifth patterns locked tightly to this groove. Because the vocal hook is the genre's centerpiece, sketch and prioritize that melody first, then build a simple, repetitive guitar riff to frame it rather than the reverse. Velocity should stay high and consistent on stomp elements for maximum physical impact, with vocal and guitar hits peaking hard on chorus impacts for theatrical emphasis; moderate humanization keeps the groove tight but not sterile, and heavy vocal doubling benefits from slight timing/pitch variation between takes for a natural chorus blend.

## Mixing

The mix should read bright and present, favoring upper-midrange and top-end clarity for vocal and guitar sheen over hard rock's heavier low-mid weight, with a controlled, punchy (not sub-heavy) kick anchoring the stomp groove. Vocal-forward compression keeps the lead vocal big and consistently present, while drum bus compression emphasizes snap and clap impact. Plate reverb applied liberally to vocals delivers the genre's glossy, larger-than-life presence, and slap-back delay adds classic glam vocal thickness. Wide vocal doubling/harmony stacks and spread handclaps build a big stereo picture, while kick, snare, and bass stay centered for stomp-groove punch.

## Mastering

Glam rock masters target roughly -10 to -8 LUFS integrated with a bright, punchy character — loud and radio-ready without fully sacrificing the stomp groove's percussive impact. Frequency balance favors a bright, glossy top end and present upper-mids for vocal and guitar sheen, paired with a tight, punchy low end (not sub-bass-heavy) that keeps the stomp groove feeling physical rather than boomy.

## Reference Artists

T. Rex, led by Marc Bolan, defined the genre's boogie-fuzz guitar sound and androgynous stage image ("Get It On (Bang a Gong)"). David Bowie's Ziggy Stardust era brought theatrical, gender-fluid performance art to glam's mainstream breakthrough ("Rebel Rebel"). Slade ("Cum On Feel the Noize") and Sweet ("Ballroom Blitz") delivered glam's stomping, football-terrace-anthem strand, while Roxy Music brought an art-school avant-pop sensibility and the New York Dolls channeled glam's flamboyance through a rawer, proto-punk American lens.

## Production Notes

Lead with the hook — write the chorus/vocal hook first and build the riff and arrangement to frame it, since glam rock prioritizes immediacy and charisma over harmonic or technical complexity. Track or program a genuine stomp groove rather than a generic rock beat; the emphatic kick-kick-snare-clap pattern is the genre's rhythmic fingerprint. Lean into theatrical, larger-than-life vocal delivery and doubling — an understated vocal performance undersells the genre's essential persona. Common mistakes include treating glam rock as interchangeable with hard rock and over-heavying the guitar tone (losing the brighter boogie-fuzz character), underusing handclaps/tambourine and vocal doubling (leaving the mix sounding thin rather than glossy and larger-than-life), and writing overly technical guitar solos that undercut the genre's focus on catchy immediacy. Modern doubling/ADT plugins and layered handclap sample stacks make it straightforward to recreate glam's thick, glossy vocal and percussion textures without needing multiple live takes.
