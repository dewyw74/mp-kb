## History

Witch house's stylistic sound and aesthetic was pioneered by Salem, formed in 2006 in Traverse City, Michigan by John Holland, Heather Marlatt, and Jack Donoghue, who emerged publicly in 2008 with the EP Yes I Smoke Crack. The term "witch house" itself was coined as a joke in December 2009 by Travis Egedy, performing as Pictureplane, who explained he and a friend were joking about the "occult-based house music" they made; despite Egedy's own rejection of the label, it stuck once Pitchfork and other music press picked it up, leading to the observation that "the genre does exist now, for better or worse." Salem's 2010 debut LP King Night, ranked #8 on NME's 50 Best Albums of 2010 for its innovative blend of hip-hop, goth, and electronic elements, remains widely regarded as the genre's most significant and lasting recorded work — critical consensus holds that "witch house itself wouldn't have existed at all without them." White Ring, formed in 2009, and oOoOO further developed the sound, with CRIM3S and Unison also contributing to the genre's nocturnal, murky, occult- and horror-themed aesthetic.

## Music Theory

Witch house minimizes conventional harmonic development, built instead on dark, droning synth textures and minimal harmonic movement — where directly sampling other tracks, harmony is often inherited from slowed and pitched-down source material via chopped-and-screwed-derived technique. Ethereal, high-pitched keyboard melodies contrast sharply against heavily layered, booming basslines, while vocals — usually sampled and heavily manipulated through pitch-shifting, reversing, and chopping — function as textural, often unintelligible elements within the mix rather than a conventional lead vocal line.

## Arrangement

Tracks typically open with a dark, droning atmospheric texture or a slowed, pitched-down sample fragment establishing a nocturnal, occult mood before the trap-derived beat enters. Rather than a conventional dynamic build, the genre favors sustained murky atmosphere, with a repeated hook or sample fragment functioning as the track's structural anchor within an otherwise hazy, disorienting texture, and songs frequently dissolve into atmosphere rather than resolving cleanly.

## Instruments

A hip-hop/trap-derived drum kit — booming 808 kicks, snappy claps, sparse snares, restrained hi-hats — typically sourced from or emulating the Roland TR-808, provides the rhythmic foundation at a slow, booming, half-time-feeling tempo. Dense, layered synthesizers create dark, droning soundscapes and ethereal high-pitched melodies, establishing the genre's defining icy-bright versus murky-dark sonic contrast; guitars, acoustic keys, and orchestration are entirely absent.

## Sound Design

The genre's signature technique is slowing and pitching down sampled material — often from pop or hip-hop songs — a direct extension of the chopped-and-screwed technique, layered with dense reverb and delay to create the "drag" or "zombie rave" sound witch house is known for. Distortion and bitcrushing rough up clean samples, and heavily manipulated vocal fragments (pitch-shifted, reversed, chopped) serve as textural rather than conventionally melodic vocal content.

## MIDI Programming

Program booming, sustained 808-derived bass and trap-derived drum patterns (kicks, claps, sparse snares, restrained hi-hats) at a slowed tempo of 80-120 BPM, or half-time at 100-150 BPM, for the genre's weighty, hypnotic feel. Layer dark, droning pad textures against bright, ethereal high-pitched keyboard melodies for the signature tonal contrast, keeping dynamics restrained and atmospheric rather than dramatically varied.

## Mixing

Keep the low end dark and bass-heavy from 808s, leaving the midrange hazy and murky rather than clear to support the genre's disorienting atmosphere, while giving high-pitched keyboard elements enough presence to cut through. Heavy, dense reverb and delay are central to the genre's dreamlike soundstage, particularly on vocal samples, and moderate compression preserves weight without eliminating the genre's deliberately hazy, lo-fi character.

## Mastering

Masters target roughly -11 to -9 LUFS integrated, moderate, dense, and murky in keeping with the genre's atmospheric, lo-fi-leaning character. The frequency balance favors heavy sub-bass weight, a deliberately hazy midrange, and bright, ethereal high-frequency keyboard elements cutting through the overall murk.

## Reference Artists

Salem's King Night (2010) stands as the genre's single most significant and lasting recorded work, without which — per critical consensus — witch house would not have existed. White Ring and oOoOO further developed the genre's sound following Salem's foundational influence, and Pictureplane (Travis Egedy), who coined the genre's name, remains a key figure despite rejecting the label himself.

## Production Notes

- Slow and pitch down sampled material for the genre's signature murky, "drag" atmosphere.
- Contrast dark, droning synth textures against bright, ethereal high-pitched keyboard melodies.
- Use trap-derived drum programming at a slowed, half-time-feeling tempo rather than a full-speed hip-hop groove.
- Heavily process vocal samples via pitch-shifting, reversing, and chopping, treating them as textural rather than conventionally intelligible.
- Avoid clean, full-speed beats or overly intelligible vocal samples — both undercut the genre's disorienting, murky identity.
- Modern hybrid approach: use pitch/time-stretching plugins to authentically slow sampled material, and layer heavy reverb/delay sends for the genre's signature dense soundstage.
