## History

Swing emerged in the early-to-mid 1930s as hot jazz's small-group improvisational language was scaled up into large, arranged dance orchestras. Fletcher Henderson's arrangements (later adopted by Benny Goodman) established the sectional call-and-response writing — brass answering reeds — that became the genre's core vocabulary, while Kansas City territory bands under Count Basie developed a looser, riff-based, blues-soaked approach built around a famously light, propulsive rhythm section. Benny Goodman's 1935 Palomar Ballroom engagement is conventionally cited as swing's breakout moment into mainstream popularity, launching a decade in which big bands (Goodman, Ellington, Basie, Miller, Shaw, Webb) were the dominant popular music of the United States, filling ballrooms nationwide with dancers doing the Lindy Hop and Jitterbug. Swing's commercial dominance faded by the mid-1940s under pressure from wartime economics, a musicians' union recording ban, and the rise of small-group bebop, but it never disappeared — it persists as a living dance-music tradition and as the rhythmic and harmonic foundation every later jazz style reacted to or built upon.

## Music Theory

Swing harmony draws on Tin Pan Alley popular song form (AABA, 32 bars) and the 12-bar blues, both harmonized with functional diatonic progressions — I-vi-ii-V turnarounds, ii-V-I cadences — that stay comfortably inside the key rather than venturing into the chromatic substitutions later bebop players would add. "I Got Rhythm" changes became such a common vehicle for improvisation that "rhythm changes" is now a standard jazz term in its own right. Melodies and solos are diatonic and blues-inflected, favoring clear, singable phrases over harmonic complexity — the emphasis is on rhythmic drive and ensemble interplay rather than the dense reharmonization that would come to define bebop a decade later.

## Arrangement

A swing chart typically opens with a short vamp or shout figure, states the tune's head as a full, harmonized ensemble passage (brass and reeds trading or combining), moves through one or more improvised solo choruses accompanied by riffing horn backgrounds, and builds to a written "shout chorus" — a high-energy, high-register ensemble passage designed as the arrangement's climax — before a tag ending or return to the head closes the tune. This head-solos-shout-out structure, built for a dancing audience, is the direct ancestor of the head-solos-head form bebop would later adopt for a listening (rather than dancing) audience.

## Instruments

The big band — typically 3-4 trumpets, 3-4 trombones, and 4-5 saxophones (doubling clarinet) — is the genre's defining instrumental force, arranged in harmonized sectional writing with brass and reeds in call-and-response. The rhythm section (upright bass walking quarter notes, acoustic guitar chunking four-to-the-bar chords Freddie Green-style, piano comping sparsely, and drums driving with brushes or a light ride/hi-hat pulse) provides a steady, danceable foundation rather than a soloistic voice, though piano and drums do take occasional solo choruses.

## Sound Design

Swing's "sound design" is entirely acoustic and mechanical: brass mutes (Harmon, cup, plunger) provide the era's signature tonal color palette, wide continuous vibrato on saxophone and trumpet lead lines gives the ensemble its warm, vocal-like character, and section blend is achieved through physical bandstand seating and player balance rather than any electronic processing — swing predates both the electric guitar's popularization for lead work and any form of synthesis.

## MIDI Programming

When programming a swing arrangement in a DAW, prioritize a consistent triplet-based swing feel (roughly 58-67% depending on tempo) across all eighth-note material — this is the single most important element for authenticity. Use big-band sample libraries with true legato/section articulations for harmonized brass and reed writing rather than stacking single-hit patches, program a steady walking quarter-note bass with slightly detached note lengths to emulate upright pizzicato, and keep the ride/hi-hat carrying an unbroken four-to-the-bar pulse under sparse, disciplined snare comping until the shout chorus arrives.

## Mixing

Give brass presence and bite in the 2-5 kHz range while keeping reeds warmer in the low-mids so they support rather than compete, use light bus compression to glue ensemble hits without flattening a soloist's natural dynamic arc, and place horn sections in a wide stereo image evoking physical bandstand seating (brass split left/right, reeds center-wide) with the rhythm section anchored in the center. Moderate room or hall reverb should evoke a ballroom or theater space without smearing fast unison horn figures.

## Mastering

Swing masters should preserve a wide dynamic range (roughly -11 to -9 LUFS integrated) so the contrast between restrained solo choruses and full-band shout choruses remains audible and impactful — that contrast is a foundational expressive device of big-band arranging, not an incidental artifact to be compressed away. Frequency balance favors a warm low end, clear midrange horn definition, and a natural, non-hyped top end consistent with acoustic recording practice of the era.

## Reference Artists

Benny Goodman, "The King of Swing," brought Fletcher Henderson's sectional arranging to mass popularity and led one of the era's defining bands ("Sing, Sing, Sing"). Count Basie's Kansas City-rooted band built its identity on a famously light, riff-driven rhythm section and blues-soaked head arrangements ("One O'Clock Jump"). Duke Ellington elevated big-band writing to a compositional art form with sophisticated voicings and tone-color orchestration ("It Don't Mean a Thing"), while Glenn Miller achieved the era's greatest commercial success with tightly arranged, immediately recognizable reed voicings ("In the Mood"). Chick Webb's Savoy Ballroom house band, featuring a young Ella Fitzgerald, exemplified the genre's dance-hall energy and vocal integration ("A-Tisket, A-Tasket").

## Production Notes

Get the swung eighth-note feel locked in before anything else — every other element of the style, from horn writing to walking bass to comping guitar, depends on that consistent, propulsive pulse. Write horn parts as harmonized blocks moving in parallel motion rather than unison lines to capture the authentic big-band ensemble sound, and save the shout chorus for the arrangement's true emotional peak so its impact lands by contrast. The most common mistake is using straight, unswung eighth notes, which instantly reads as generic rather than authentically swing; a close second is treating the rhythm section like a modern backbeat-driven band instead of the walking, four-on-the-floor dance pulse the genre actually runs on. Modern productions can convincingly recreate the era by layering sampled big-band sections with true legato and swing-quantized MIDI, applying convolution reverb from ballroom or theater impulse responses, and referencing period Columbia, Victor, or Decca masters for horn balance and dynamic-range targets.
