## History

Drone music's roots reach back to La Monte Young, whose work with the Theatre of Eternal Music in New York in the early 1960s established sustained, just-intonation tones as a legitimate compositional focus in their own right — most famously in the decades-long *Dream House* sound installation and *The Well-Tuned Piano*. Young's work drew directly on Indian classical music's tanpura drone tradition (he studied under Pandit Pran Nath) and on the broader minimalist movement alongside Terry Riley, Tony Conrad, and Charlemagne Palestine. Through the 1970s, Éliane Radigue developed an independent, deeply meditative strand of electronic drone using the ARP 2500 synthesizer, exploring similarly glacial timescales. Drone also has a parallel lineage through rock and metal: Tony Conrad's collaborations with Faust, and later the doom/sludge metal scene, produced heavily amplified, feedback-driven guitar drone, culminating in acts like Sunn O))), who fused drone's sustained-tone philosophy with extreme volume and distortion. From the 1990s onward, drone increasingly became an electronic-production genre in its own right, closely related to but distinct from ambient — where ambient is about layered, evolving texture, drone is about the sustained tone itself as the entire subject of the piece.

## Music Theory

Drone's harmonic language centers on a single sustained fundamental (or a fundamental plus a small number of harmonically-related tones, often a perfect fifth) rather than chord progressions. The genre's interest comes not from harmonic movement but from the acoustic phenomena that occur within a sustained tone: beating (the audible pulsing that results from two close-but-not-identical frequencies), overtone interaction, and the way the ear's perception of a static sound subtly shifts over long listening durations. Many drone composers, following La Monte Young and Éliane Radigue, work in just intonation — tuning intervals to simple whole-number frequency ratios (3:2 for a fifth, 5:4 for a major third) rather than the compromises of equal temperament — because these ratios produce cleaner, more resonant overtone alignment and more pronounced, intentional beating when slightly detuned.

## Arrangement

There is effectively no arrangement in the conventional sense: a drone piece is a single sustained state that may last anywhere from several minutes to several hours. What structure exists is glacial — a piece might spend its entire duration on one sustained interval, or it may contain a single dramatic harmonic shift used as the only "event" in an otherwise unchanging work. Because there are no sections to signal to the listener, the piece's beginning and end are often treated as somewhat arbitrary points within a continuous, theoretically infinite tone (Young's *Dream House* installation, for instance, was designed to run continuously for years).

## Instruments

Any sustain-capable sound source works: bowed strings (cello, double bass, viola) using extended techniques, feedback-driven electric guitar (often in alternate/drone tunings), pipe organ or drawbar organ synths exploiting rich harmonic content, and analog synthesizer oscillators tuned to just-intonation ratios. Percussion and anything with a percussive attack is essentially excluded, since transient attacks contradict the genre's focus on unbroken sustain.

## Sound Design

Sound design in drone is about spectral shaping of a static signal rather than dynamic synthesis programming. Multiple oscillators are tuned to precise harmonic ratios and slightly detuned against each other to generate audible beating; filters are set once (or swept across timescales of many minutes) rather than modulated rhythmically. Feedback — whether amp/guitar feedback or synth self-oscillation — is a legitimate and historically central sound source, not just an effect to be avoided. Reverb and delay, when used, often function as sustain-reinforcement tools (letting a bowed or plucked note ring far longer than its natural decay) rather than as spatial effects in the conventional mixing sense.

## MIDI Programming

Conventional MIDI programming plays a smaller role in drone than in almost any other electronic genre — many drone works are built from live, sustained instrumental performance (bowing, feedback control) rather than sequenced notes, because the micro-variation a human performer introduces into a sustained tone is often the entire point. Where MIDI is used, it typically consists of a single held note per voice, sometimes with slow pitch-bend automation for microtonal drift, and velocity is kept constant to preserve the illusion of one continuous, unbroken tone.

## Mixing

Mixing decisions in drone are primarily about harmonic sculpting: EQ is used to bring out or suppress specific overtones of the fundamental, shaping the tonal character of the piece more than balancing discrete instrument layers as in other genres. Compression is minimal, since it can flatten the natural swell and decay that give a sustained tone its sense of life and breath. The stereo field is often widened by detuning duplicate voices hard left and right, which produces spatial beating as an additional layer of movement within the static harmonic core.

## Mastering

Because a drone piece's power comes from sustained presence rather than dynamic events, mastering avoids aggressive limiting, which would create audible pumping against an unchanging signal and undermine the sense of an unbroken tone. Loudness targets skew quieter and more dynamic than most electronic genres, and frequency balance favors the fundamental and its near overtones without excessive brightness, which can make an intentionally ancient- or organic-sounding drone read as artificially synthetic.

## Reference Artists

La Monte Young is the genre's foundational figure through his work with the Theatre of Eternal Music and *The Well-Tuned Piano*. Éliane Radigue represents the electronic/ARP-synthesizer lineage of meditative, slowly-evolving drone. Sunn O))) brought drone into extreme-volume, guitar-and-amp-feedback territory, bridging drone with doom metal. Phill Niblock's decades of massed, microtonally-layered instrumental drone recordings are a key reference for harmonic density. Stars of the Lid and Tim Hecker represent contemporary crossovers between drone and ambient.

## Production Notes

The single most common mistake in producing drone is underestimating duration and event density — a genuinely effective drone piece typically runs far longer than a standard track (15 to 40+ minutes is normal) and resists the temptation to add "interesting" variation that would pull it toward ambient. Tuning choices matter enormously: using true just-intonation ratios rather than equal-tempered intervals produces a noticeably richer, more resonant beating pattern that is central to the genre's characteristic sound. Modern producers increasingly use granular time-stretching to turn short acoustic recordings into extremely long, organically evolving drones, and microtonal tuning plugins (such as MTS-ESP) to achieve historically accurate just-intonation tuning within a standard DAW workflow.
