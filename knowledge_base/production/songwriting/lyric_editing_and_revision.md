# Lyric Editing and Revision Technique

Unlike `knowledge_base/production/songwriting/lyric_writing_and_thematic_content.md` and `knowledge_base/production/songwriting/rhyme_scheme_syllable_density_and_flow.md`, which are grounded in specific claims documented across this knowledge base's genre files, no genre file addresses the *editing/revision* stage of lyric writing directly. This entry reasons from general lyric-craft practice rather than citing a documented knowledge-base source, per this project's rule against presenting assumptions as documented fact — treat it as general guidance to apply alongside, not instead of, the genre-specific thematic and flow guidance in the two entries above.

## Separate the Idea Draft from the Fit Draft

Write a first pass focused only on the idea, image, or story — without worrying yet whether syllable counts match the beat or every line rhymes cleanly per `rhyme_scheme_syllable_density_and_flow.md`. Do the rhythmic/rhyme fitting pass second, once the content is right. Solving both problems in the same pass (what to say, and how to make it scan) tends to produce lyrics that are metrically correct but say nothing interesting, because the meter constraint wins every close call.

## Read/Perform the Draft Against the Actual Track

A lyric that scans correctly on paper can still fail once it's actually sung or rapped against the instrumental — stressed syllables landing on weak beats, awkward consonant clusters at tempo, or a line that's technically the right syllable count but doesn't sit naturally in the mouth at the track's tempo. Test every revision by performing it against the actual instrumental (even a rough scratch vocal), not by reading it silently — silent reading hides timing and phrasing problems that only surface when the words are actually sung or rapped.

## Cut Before Adding

When a section isn't working, the default move should be cutting the weakest line(s) rather than immediately writing new material to fix it. A verse that's dragging is more often fixed by removing a redundant or filler line than by adding a clarifying one — extra lines usually compound a pacing problem rather than solving it, especially in verse sections that `lyric_writing_and_thematic_content.md` documents as needing to stay narrower than the chorus to preserve intelligibility.

## Flag and Revisit Placeholder Lines

It's normal for a first draft to contain a placeholder line ("dummy lyric") written purely to lock in a melodic/rhythmic shape before the real words exist. The risk is forgetting to revisit it — a placeholder that scans well enough to not sound obviously wrong can get left in through mixing and mastering. Explicitly flag placeholder lines during the drafting pass so they get a deliberate final pass rather than surviving by default.

## Cliché and Genre-Fit Check as a Separate Pass

Run a dedicated pass checking for overused phrases or images that don't add anything specific to this song, and cross-check thematic choices against the genre-authenticity guidance in `lyric_writing_and_thematic_content.md` — a line can be well-crafted and still be wrong for the track if it doesn't fit the genre's documented thematic lineage (e.g., writing a generically upbeat pop lyric over a track built in a genre where `lyric_writing_and_thematic_content.md` documents a specific, narrower thematic tradition).

## Common Mistakes

**Editing for rhyme/meter and content simultaneously.** Splitting these into separate passes, as above, tends to produce stronger results than trying to solve both at once.

**Trusting a silent read-through over an actual performed take.** Meter and rhyme that look correct on paper can still fail once sung or rapped at tempo; always test against the real instrumental before finalizing.

**Letting a placeholder ("dummy") lyric survive into the final version by accident.** Explicitly flagging placeholder lines during drafting prevents this.
