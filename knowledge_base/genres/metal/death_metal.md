## History

Death Metal evolved from Thrash Metal in the late 1980s, pushing the boundaries of speed, aggression, and dark lyrical themes. Originating heavily in Florida (with bands like Death, Morbid Angel, and Cannibal Corpse) and Sweden (Entombed, Dismember), the genre abandoned traditional singing entirely in favor of guttural growls. The genre quickly splintered into highly complex Technical Death Metal, brutally slow Doom-Death, and Swedish Melodic Death Metal, which reintegrated classic heavy metal guitar harmonies.

## Music Theory

Traditional death metal is largely atonal and highly chromatic, intentionally avoiding traditional major or minor key resolutions to create a constant sense of unease, chaos, and brutality. It relies heavily on diminished intervals and the Phrygian Dominant scale. Riffs are constructed using fast tremolo picking of single notes and down-tuned power chords.

## Arrangement

Arrangements eschew traditional pop structures. Songs are often a linear journey through a labyrinth of complex, shifting riffs. "Blast beats" (extreme, relentless drum patterns) provide the foundation, while slower, heavier "groove" sections or breakdowns are inserted to provide contrast and maximize the heaviness when the fast parts return.

## Instruments

The defining elements are down-tuned electric guitars (often tuned down to B or A), heavily distorted electric bass, and an acoustic drum kit played with extreme physical prowess (double-kick pedals are mandatory). Synthesizers are largely absent, keeping the focus on raw, aggressive instrumentation.

## Sound Design

The tone of Death Metal is defined by extreme distortion. The classic Swedish "buzzsaw" tone (e.g., Entombed) was created by maxing out all the knobs on a Boss HM-2 Heavy Metal distortion pedal. The Florida sound relied on solid-state amplifiers or heavily boosted tube amps. In all cases, the distortion is pushed to the absolute limit of intelligibility.

## MIDI Programming

Programming death metal requires intense scrutiny of the drum track. "Blast beats" involve hitting the kick, snare, and ride cymbal simultaneously (or in rapid alternation) at tempos exceeding 200 BPM. Careful velocity adjustment is required so it doesn't sound like a machine gun, though the genre accepts a highly triggered, synthetic drum sound as standard. 

## Mixing

Mixing death metal is an exercise in extreme frequency management. The guitars, bass, and kick drum are all fighting for the exact same low-end frequencies because the guitars are tuned so low. The solution is aggressive EQ: the kick drum is given a sharp, unnatural "click" in the high frequencies, the bass guitar is distorted to provide midrange "clank," and the guitars are high-passed to leave room for the actual sub-bass.

## Mastering

Death metal is mastered for maximum loudness and impact. It is one of the most heavily compressed genres in music, often sacrificing dynamic range to ensure the relentless wall of distorted guitars and blast beats never drops in intensity.

## Reference Artists

Death (led by Chuck Schuldiner) is the genre's pioneer and namesake. Cannibal Corpse defines the brutal, heavy, and rhythmically complex American style. Morbid Angel introduced sludgy, doom-laden riffs into the speed. At The Gates and In Flames pioneered the highly influential Swedish Melodic Death Metal sound, which heavily influenced later American Metalcore.

## Production Notes

The key to a modern, crushing death metal production is absolute, metronomic tightness. The bass, rhythm guitars, and kick drums must hit exactly together. If they are even slightly out of time, the extreme distortion and speed will cause the mix to turn into unintelligible mud. Aggressive gating, heavy EQ carving, and drum sample replacement are standard, required tools in the death metal producer's arsenal.
