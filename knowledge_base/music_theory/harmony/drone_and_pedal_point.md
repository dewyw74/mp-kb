# Drone and Pedal-Point Harmony: A Sustained Tonal Reference as Harmonic Foundation

A drone — a continuous, unchanging tone (or interval, typically tonic-and-fifth) sustained beneath melodic or rhythmic activity — is documented across this knowledge base as ranging from a whole tradition's *entire* harmonic system to a deliberate, genre-specific texture choice within an otherwise harmonically active piece. What every citation shares is the same underlying function: a fixed tonal reference point that melodic material is heard *against*, rather than a harmony that itself moves or progresses.

## The Drone as a Tradition's Entire Harmonic System

`indian_classical.md` documents the most complete version of this: "Not applicable — the tradition is melodic and rhythmic rather than harmonic; a drone (typically tuned to the tonic and fifth) provides the only sustained harmonic reference." The tanpura, a dedicated drone instrument, "sustains a continuous drone throughout the performance as the tradition's sole harmonic reference" — there is no chord progression to speak of; the raga's melodic content is heard entirely against this fixed tonic-fifth reference rather than against any moving harmony. The file's mixing guidance treats this as load-bearing rather than optional: "Preserve the tanpura drone as a continuous, unbroken harmonic reference throughout a recording or arrangement."

## Drone as Foundation Beneath an Ornamented Melodic Line

`celtic.md` documents a related but structurally different use: "Modal drone/pedal-point harmony beneath a highly ornamented melodic line." Unlike Indian classical music's drone-as-sole-harmonic-content, Celtic drone/pedal-point harmony coexists with a modal melodic tradition that has its own scalar identity — the drone provides a stable reference tone that the ornamented melody's modal color is heard against, without the drone itself carrying the tradition's full harmonic information the way the tanpura does.

## The Drone as a Deliberate Genre-Texture Choice

Several electronic genre files use drone not as an inherited traditional practice but as a chosen textural/harmonic device. `jungle.md`: "Single sustained minor chord/drone with no harmonic movement" — here the drone is a specific arrangement choice within a genre that could easily use moving harmony, made specifically to keep harmonic content out of the way of the track's actual rhythmic focus. `speedcore.md` makes the same choice for a different reason: "Static single-chord drones (harmony is largely absent; tension comes from rhythm and distortion)" — the drone exists so that tension-generation can be handed entirely to rhythm and processing rather than harmonic movement. `drone.md` (the genre literally named after the technique) documents the most theoretically deep version: "just intonation," "microtonal," and "overtone series (harmonic drones)" as common scales, with "multi-oscillator just-intonation patches... for historically accurate harmonic beating" — using precisely-tuned (rather than equal-tempered) intervals specifically so the drone's sustained tones produce audible acoustic beating and overtone interaction, a physical phenomenon a moving, equal-tempered harmony wouldn't showcase.

## Why Drone Functions Consistently Across Such Different Contexts

The common mechanism across all these citations: removing harmonic *movement* shifts a listener's attention to whatever *is* moving — melodic ornamentation (Celtic, Indian classical), rhythm (jungle, speedcore), or the drone's own internal acoustic properties (drone.md's just-intonation beating). A drone isn't an absence of harmonic interest; it's a deliberate redirection of where that interest is placed.

## Common Mistakes

**Treating a drone as simply "no harmony" rather than a specific harmonic choice with its own function.** As the citations above show, a drone actively redirects listener attention toward melody, rhythm, or acoustic texture — it's a positive choice, not a default fallback for a track that hasn't decided on a chord progression.

**Using equal-tempered tuning where genuine just-intonation drone beating is the actual goal.** `drone.md`'s specific citation of just-intonation tuning plugins for "historically accurate harmonic beating" shows that the acoustic phenomenon depends on precise, non-equal-tempered interval tuning — a standard equal-tempered drone won't produce the same beating character.

**Breaking a drone's continuity in a tradition where it functions as the sole harmonic reference.** `indian_classical.md`'s explicit instruction to preserve the tanpura "as a continuous, unbroken harmonic reference" reflects that any interruption removes the only harmonic anchor the entire performance has.
