## History

Drum and bass crystallized between 1993 and 1996 out of the jungle scene as UK producers refined the chopped-breakbeat, heavy-bass template into a broader and more technically varied genre, gradually shedding some of jungle's overtly ragga/reggae vocal culture in favor of a wider range of stylistic directions. Goldie's "Timeless" and the Metalheadz label pushed the genre toward cinematic ambition; Roni Size / Reprazent's "New Forms" (winning the UK's Mercury Prize in 1997) brought live-instrumentation jazz influence into the mix; LTJ Bukem's Good Looking label developed the smoother, atmospheric "intelligent" strand that would evolve into liquid; and a harder, more mechanical lineage running through Ed Rush & Optical and later Noisia established what became neurofunk. By the 2000s and 2010s drum and bass had diversified into a wide ecosystem of subgenres — liquid, neurofunk, jump-up, jungle-revival — held together by the shared tempo range and breakbeat/sub-bass foundation while diverging enormously in mood, harmony, and bass sound design.

## Music Theory

As a genre umbrella, drum and bass spans the full harmonic spectrum: liquid draws on jazz-influenced extended chords and soulful voice-leading, while darker and more aggressive subgenres like neurofunk minimize harmonic content almost entirely in favor of rhythmic and textural intensity. What's constant is that harmony, where it exists, serves the groove and bassline rather than driving the track — the chopped break and sub-bass are the compositional foundation, and melodic/harmonic material is built around and in support of that rhythmic core rather than the reverse.

## Arrangement

Across nearly all drum and bass subgenres, arrangement follows a broadly similar shape inherited from jungle: an atmospheric or filtered intro establishes tempo and mood before the full break and bassline enter at the first drop; breakdowns strip back to expose melodic, atmospheric, or purely rhythmic "roller" content; short builds (given the fast tempo) compress tension via snare rolls and pitched fragments; and drops deliver the full break/bass/hook combination at peak density. Outros mirror intros for DJ-friendly transitions, reflecting the genre's continued club and mix-set functionality even as production has grown increasingly detailed and studio-focused.

## Instruments

The chopped breakbeat remains the genre's rhythmic backbone across all subgenres, though its treatment varies enormously — clean and tightly edited in liquid, mangled and layered with programmed elements in neurofunk. Sub-bass is the universal low-end anchor, always present and always distinct from whatever mid-bass or lead-bass character defines the specific subgenre (clean and rounded, distorted and growling, wobbling and resonant). Melodic instrumentation is the single biggest genre-differentiator: jazzy Rhodes and warm strings in liquid versus aggressive FM/wavetable growl-bass and metallic textures in neurofunk.

## Sound Design

Sound design methodology spans from largely sample-based breakbeat manipulation (constant across the genre) to synthesis approaches that vary hugely by subgenre — subtractive sine-wave sub-bass is near-universal, while mid-bass and lead textures range from smooth Reese-style phased saw pairs to complex FM/wavetable growl patches built for maximum aggression. Reverb and distortion usage scale directly with a subgenre's intended mood: lush and spacious for liquid's atmospheric target, tight, dry, and heavily saturated for neurofunk's forward, aggressive target.

## MIDI Programming

Breakbeat programming across the genre typically starts from sliced source material reassembled into denser, more syncopated patterns than the original recording, with ghost snares and fills inserted for momentum — the degree of chopping and aggression scales with subgenre intensity. Sub-bass is generally locked rhythmically to the kick within the break regardless of subgenre; what varies is the secondary mid-bass or lead-bass layer, which ranges from smooth sustained lines in liquid to rapid, heavily modulated stabs in neurofunk.

## Mixing

The one mixing constant across all drum and bass subgenres is isolating sub-bass below roughly 100-120 Hz from mid-bass and break content, keeping it mono and centered for soundsystem translation. Above that point, mix decisions diverge sharply by subgenre — warm and spacious in liquid, forward and aggressively compressed in neurofunk — making "generic drum and bass mixing" a less useful frame than mixing to the specific subgenre's sonic target.

## Mastering

Loudness and dynamics targets vary by subgenre and era, generally landing around -9 to -7 LUFS integrated, with atmospheric/liquid material typically retaining more dynamic range than harder-hitting neurofunk or jump-up masters pushed for maximum club impact. Deep, controlled sub-bass and forward break presence are constants across the genre regardless of overall brightness or aggression.

## Reference Artists

Goldie's "Inner City Life" and Roni Size / Reprazent's "Brown Paper Bag" represent the genre's foundational, jazz-and-cinema-influenced era. Andy C's "Roll On" showcases the genre's technical rollout-and-drop DJ-tool tradition, while Chase & Status' "Blind Faith" and Pendulum's crossover work demonstrate drum and bass's later expansion into festival-scale, rock- and pop-influenced territory.

## Production Notes

Before specializing, get comfortable with breakbeat chopping fundamentals — nearly every subgenre depends on this skill regardless of how different the final sound is. Decide on a subgenre lane early, since bass tone, breakbeat treatment, and harmonic approach diverge radically across the drum and bass umbrella, and reference tracks should come from within that specific lane rather than the genre broadly. A near-universal mixing discipline is keeping sub-bass mono and cleanly separated from mid-bass content. Common mistakes include treating "drum and bass" as one monolithic sound rather than a broad umbrella of distinct subgenre identities, over-compressing breaks until natural swing is lost, and letting sub/mid-bass overlap and mask each other. Modern workflows lean on sampler slice-to-MIDI tools for flexible break re-sequencing and combine vintage-sampled breaks with modern synthesized sub-bass for authenticity paired with contemporary low-end weight.
