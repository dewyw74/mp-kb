# Modal Interchange and Borrowed Chords: The bVII-bVI Rock Vamp Family

Modal interchange — borrowing a chord from the parallel major or minor (or from a parallel mode) rather than staying strictly within one key's diatonic chords — shows up across this knowledge base's rock and electronic genre files most consistently in one specific family of progressions: vamps built from bVII and bVI borrowed from the parallel natural-minor/Aeolian or Mixolydian mode.

## The bVII-bVI Minor-Modal Vamp

`idm.md` documents "i - bVII - bVI (dark modal minor movement)," `space_rock.md` documents "i-bVII-bVI (minor modal vamp)," and `progressive_rock.md` documents "extended modal vamps (i-bVII-bVI in Aeolian/Dorian)." `hardcore_punk.md` uses the same shape from a different genre angle: "i-bVI-bVII vamp." All four are the same underlying borrowed-chord move — building a static or slow-moving vamp from the tonic minor plus its two "borrowed" (relative to major-key diatonic expectations) neighboring chords a whole step and a minor third below. The consistency of this exact shape across such different genres (ambient electronic, space rock, progressive rock, hardcore punk) suggests it functions as a genuinely stable harmonic color rather than a genre-specific quirk — dark, open, and modally ambiguous in a way a strictly diatonic minor-key progression isn't.

## The I-bVII-IV Mixolydian Rock Vamp

A related but distinct family appears across classic and hard rock: `classic_rock.md`'s "I-bVII-IV (Mixolydian rock vamp)," `hard_rock.md`'s "I-bVII-IV (power-chord Mixolydian vamp)," `arena_rock.md`'s "I-bVII-IV Mixolydian riff vamp," and `glam_rock.md`'s "I-bVII-IV Mixolydian stomp." Here the borrowing works from a major tonic rather than a minor one — bVII is borrowed from Mixolydian (or, equivalently, from the parallel minor) against an otherwise major-key context, producing the bright-but-slightly-untethered quality closely associated with riff-driven, power-chord rock. The near-identical progression appearing across four separate rock subgenre files, each independently naming it as a "vamp" or "stomp," indicates this is one of the most load-bearing borrowed-chord shapes in the entire rock genre family.

## Why These Specific Borrowed Chords Recur

Both families borrow chords a whole step (bVII) and/or a minor third below the tonic (bVI) — pulling from the darker, "flatter" end of the parallel mode rather than the brighter end (which would involve raised scale degrees). This consistent direction of borrowing is worth noting as a pattern in itself: rock and electronic genres reaching for modal interchange in this knowledge base overwhelmingly borrow *downward* in brightness (toward Aeolian/Mixolydian color) rather than upward (toward Lydian/major-with-raised-4th color), which tracks with the grittier, more open-ended harmonic character those genres are generally going for.

## Common Mistakes

**Treating bVII or bVI as a full key change.** These borrowed chords function as color within a still-clearly-tonic-centered vamp, not as a modulation — the progressions above all return to (or center on) the tonic chord rather than establishing a new tonal center.

**Using the wrong borrowed-chord family for the target brightness.** The i-bVII-bVI minor-modal vamp (`idm.md`, `space_rock.md`, `progressive_rock.md`) and the I-bVII-IV Mixolydian vamp (`classic_rock.md`, `hard_rock.md`, `arena_rock.md`) produce different tonal characters — minor-rooted and dark versus major-rooted and open/bright — despite both borrowing a bVII chord; picking the wrong root quality undermines the intended mood.
