## History

Turntablism's technical foundations trace to late-1970s New York breakbeat DJing (Kool Herc, Grandmaster Flash), but it consolidated into its own named, standalone performance art in the Bay Area scratch scene beginning in the late 1980s. DJ Qbert began DJing at age 15 with a stack-record turntable and a Radio Shack mixer that lacked a crossfader, an equipment limitation that led him to invent reverse scratching. In 1989, Qbert, Mix Master Mike, and DJ Apollo formed the Invisibl Skratch Piklz; performing as the "Rock Steady DJs," the same trio won the 1992 DMC World DJ Championships, and Qbert and Mix Master Mike (as "Dreamteam") went on to win further DMC World titles in 1993 and 1994, redefining what was technically possible in competitive turntable performance and establishing the Invisibl Skratch Piklz as the dominant force in DJ battle culture of the era.

## Music Theory

Turntablism operates outside conventional harmony and composed melody almost entirely — it is a rhythmic and textural art built from live manipulation of pre-existing vinyl recordings rather than newly composed musical material. Whatever pitch or melodic content appears in a routine is a property of the source record being scratched or juggled, manually pitch-bent and rhythmically re-triggered by the DJ's hand technique rather than composed in a traditional key/scale sense.

## Arrangement

A turntablism routine typically establishes a breakbeat via beat-juggling before layering in scratch technique, building through increasing technical complexity (additional turntables, combined juggling-and-scratching) toward a climactic combination trick, in a structure closely tied to battle/competition performance conventions. Structural contrast within a routine often comes from shifting between primarily scratch-focused and primarily juggle-focused sections, or introducing a new source record.

## Instruments

The turntable, mixer, and crossfader together function as turntablism's central live instrument, with vinyl breakbeats (typically drawn from funk/soul source records) providing the raw rhythmic and melodic material via beat-juggling — alternating and re-triggering the same break across two copies of a record on two turntables. There is no separately composed bass, synth, or orchestration layer; all musical content is sourced from and shaped live from the vinyl being manipulated.

## Sound Design

Turntablism's "sound design" is physical and manual rather than electronic: named scratch techniques — the crab (developed by DJ Qbert), the flare (invented by DJ Flare in 1987), the chirp (invented by DJ Jazzy Jeff), and the transformer — each describe a specific, reproducible hand-and-crossfader coordination pattern that shapes the sampled material's rhythmic and tonal character in real time. Mixer-channel EQ kill switches are used as an active performance tool during routines, not just a static mix-engineering setting.

## MIDI Programming

Classic turntablism involves no MIDI programming — beat-juggling is the live, physical equivalent of drum programming, and scratch technique is the live, physical equivalent of melodic/rhythmic sequencing, both performed by hand with no quantization. Modern digital-vinyl-system (DVS) setups can incorporate MIDI-mapped samples and loopers alongside traditional vinyl control, but the core technique remains manually performed rather than programmed.

## Mixing

Live mixer EQ is used as an active scratching tool, isolating or emphasizing frequency content from the source record during performance, while stereo imaging is simply a function of how multiple turntables are physically panned across the mixer rather than a studio decision. Recorded turntablism releases may receive conventional post-production mixing/mastering, but the live performance itself involves no compression, reverb, or automation in the studio sense.

## Mastering

Recorded turntablism releases are typically mastered around -12 to -9 LUFS integrated, preserving wide dynamic range to capture the nuance of scratch technique and beat-juggling; frequency balance depends entirely on the vinyl source material being manipulated.

## Reference Artists

The Invisibl Skratch Piklz (DJ Qbert, Mix Master Mike, DJ Apollo) — winners of the 1992 DMC World Championship as the Rock Steady DJs, with Qbert and Mix Master Mike also winning as "Dreamteam" in 1993 and 1994 — represent the genre's defining competitive and technical benchmark. DJ Qbert's Wave Twisters (1998) stands as a landmark solo turntablist album/film release.

## Production Notes

Learn named scratch techniques (crab, flare, chirp, transformer) individually and understand their specific hand/crossfader coordination before combining them into routines, and study DMC World Championship performances by the Invisibl Skratch Piklz/Rock Steady DJs as the reference standard for combining scratching, beat-juggling, and multi-turntable arrangement. Avoid conflating turntablism with general hip-hop DJing or mixing — turntablism specifically treats the turntable as a live-performed lead instrument through scratching and juggling technique, not simply a playback or blending tool for continuous music.
