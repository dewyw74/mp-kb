## History

Future Funk emerged around 2012-2013 as an offshoot of the Vaporwave scene, largely on Japanese and international internet music forums and Bandcamp/YouTube communities. Where Vaporwave slowed and warped elevator-music and 80s corporate media into a hazy, ironic soundscape, Future Funk took the opposite tempo direction: producers pitched up and sped up disco, funk, boogie, and especially Japanese City Pop records (Tatsuro Yamashita, Mariya Takeuchi, Anri) into upbeat, dancefloor-ready filter-house edits. The genre owes a direct structural debt to early-2000s French House/filter house (Daft Punk's "One More Time," Stardust's "Music Sounds Better With You"), which had already established the technique of looping and filtering disco samples into new tracks. Saint Pepsi's 2013 mixtape *Hit Vibes* is widely considered the genre's foundational release, and the meme-driven, community-remix culture around it (heavy sharing on Tumblr, 2chan, and later YouTube reaction culture) accelerated its spread. Night Tempo in particular became a bridge to the actual City Pop artists being sampled, licensing and re-releasing original Japanese masters alongside his own flips.

## Music Theory

Because the harmonic content is almost entirely inherited from the sampled source, Future Funk's "theory" is really source selection and re-contextualization. Disco and funk source material leans on extended jazz-funk harmony — major 7ths, dominant 9ths and 13ths, and ii-V-I turnarounds — carried over from the genre's roots in 1970s soul and funk session musicianship. City Pop sources add Japanese pop's fondness for chromatic passing chords and secondary dominants within otherwise diatonic major-key progressions. Producers rarely alter the harmony of a sample; the compositional work happens in rhythm — re-chopping a vocal or instrumental phrase to land on different beats than it did originally, effectively re-composing the melodic phrasing without touching the pitch content.

## Arrangement

Future Funk arrangement is loop-and-edit rather than traditionally composed. A typical track identifies a 4-8 bar loop from the source (bass + keys + guitar, sometimes with the original drums stripped out), then builds a DJ-tool style arc around it: cold-open chop, groove section, filtered breakdown (a hallmark borrowed directly from French filter house), and a "drop" where the filter reopens. Tracks are often shorter and more repetitive than a fully composed song, since the goal is a seamless dancefloor edit rather than a verse/chorus narrative.

## Instruments

The instrumental palette is whatever the source record contains — funk/disco rhythm guitar, clavinet or Rhodes, horn and string stabs, slap bass — captured entirely through sampling rather than replaying. Producers augment this with a small set of new elements: a reinforced 808-style sub bass, a four-on-the-floor kick, and open hi-hats, all programmed to sit underneath and support the vintage sample rather than compete with it.

## Sound Design

Sound design in Future Funk is really sample manipulation: pitch-shifting and time-stretching to fit a target BPM and key, resampling through a lowpass filter for the signature "filtered disco" sweep, and heavy use of tape saturation, vinyl crackle, and wow/flutter to either preserve or emulate the source's vintage character. Talkbox and vocoder textures — either sampled directly or newly performed — reference the disco/funk-era use of the effect (Zapp, Roger Troutman lineage).

## MIDI Programming

New elements (kick, bass reinforcement, hats) are programmed with real disco-drummer swing (roughly 54-58%) so they lock with the sampled groove rather than fighting its natural human timing. Chopped vocal or instrumental hooks are laid out in the piano roll/sample-chop grid as their own "instrument," re-triggered and re-pitched to create call-and-response phrasing that didn't exist in the source.

## Mixing

The central mixing challenge is gluing a compressed, decades-old sample to newly programmed, modern-fidelity drums and bass without one sounding out of place next to the other. This is solved with high-pass filtering on new low-end elements, parallel compression for density, tape/saturation plugins across the bus, and short reverbs kept off the lead sample to avoid smearing its vintage tone.

## Mastering

Masters run loud (-8 to -6 LUFS) and often lean into rather than away from analog-style saturation and mild clipping, since a pristine, ultra-clean master would clash with the deliberately vintage-colored sample content. Low-mid warmth is emphasized while harsh top-end artifacts from compressed vinyl sources are tamed.

## Reference Artists

Saint Pepsi (Skylar Spence) is the genre's originator; Macross 82-99, Yung Bae, Nu:Logic, and Bézier established the sound through the mid-2010s; Night Tempo's work bridging the scene to official City Pop reissues (with Tatsuro Yamashita, Anri, and Mariya Takeuchi) brought mainstream visibility and legitimacy to the source material.

## Production Notes

Practically, a Future Funk producer's workflow is: source selection, stem isolation/cleanup, chop-and-rearrange in a sampler or DAW's audio-warp engine, layer new rhythm section elements underneath, filter-sweep the arrangement, then glue everything with saturation and bus compression at the mix stage. The most common beginner mistake is over-quantizing the chopped material, which kills the human swing that made the source groove compelling in the first place.
