# Chromatic Harmony, Secondary Dominants, and Tension Devices

Chromatic harmony — using notes and chords outside a passage's basic diatonic scale — is documented across this knowledge base as a spectrum rather than a single technique, ranging from occasional coloristic passing tones to a defining structural principle. The genre files here show a consistent underlying logic: chromaticism is reached for specifically to complicate or delay harmonic resolution, and how far a genre pushes that complication is itself a genre-defining stylistic choice.

## Secondary Dominants and Deceptive Cadences: Delaying Resolution

`romantic.md` documents the classic 19th-century harmonic toolkit for delaying and complicating resolution most completely: "Extended chromatic harmony with frequent secondary dominants and diminished-seventh chords" and "Deceptive cadences (V - vi) used for emotional/expressive delay of resolution." A secondary dominant briefly tonicizes a chord other than the main tonic (treating it as its own temporary "I" via its own "V"), adding chromatic tension that resolves back into the main key; a deceptive cadence sets up the expectation of a full resolution (V leading to I) and then substitutes a different chord (typically vi), creating a moment of surprise or emotional delay rather than closure. `romantic.md`'s body text extends this into the era's most extreme case: "Wagner's late-Romantic chromatic voice-leading (exemplified by the 'Tristan chord') blurs functional tonal centers almost to the point of atonality, directly influencing the harmonic language of the following century" — showing the endpoint of the secondary-dominant/deceptive-cadence toolkit pushed to its logical extreme, where chromatic complication becomes so dense that a stable tonal center barely holds.

## Bossa Nova: Chromatic Passing Chords for Sophistication Without Losing Song Form

`bossa_nova.md` shows chromatic harmony used more sparingly and specifically, layered onto otherwise conventional song structures for a sophisticated color rather than as a structural principle in its own right. It documents "Chromatic descending chord movement (e.g. Fmaj7-Fm7-Em7-Ebdim7-Dm7)" and "Circle-of-fifths turnarounds with substitute dominants (tritone subs)" — the tritone substitution being a specific chromatic device where a dominant chord is replaced by another dominant chord a tritone away, sharing the same tension-creating tones (the third and seventh) but approaching the resolution chromatically from a different direction. Notably, `bossa_nova.md` ties its build technique directly to this chromatic vocabulary rather than to dynamics: "Builds are extremely subtle — dynamics rarely rise above mezzo-forte; intensity is created through harmonic richness (added chromatic passing chords) and rhythmic subtlety rather than volume or added instrumentation" — a clear example of chromatic harmony substituting for the dynamic/arrangement-based tension-building more common in other genres.

## Metal: The Tritone as the Genre's Foundational Dissonance

`metal.md` documents chromatic tension used for an entirely different expressive purpose — raw heaviness and menace rather than Romantic-style emotional delay. "The tritone and other dissonant intervals, inherited directly from Black Sabbath's foundational innovation, remain a key harmonic device for generating tension and heaviness across the genre's many subgenres," alongside "Chromatic/tritone-based riff motion for tension and heaviness" as a specific progression type. This is worth contrasting directly against `romantic.md`'s use of chromaticism: Romantic harmony uses chromatic chords (secondary dominants, diminished sevenths) as *transitional* material within a still-functional tonal framework, while metal's tritone-based riffing treats the dissonance itself as a stable, repeated harmonic color rather than something moving toward resolution.

## Contemporary Classical and Extreme Genres: Chromaticism as the Entire Harmonic System

At the far end of the spectrum, several genres treat chromatic/atonal material not as a tension device within a tonal system but as the harmonic system itself. `contemporary_classical.md` documents "Twelve-tone/serial rows" and "Microtonal and extended just-intonation systems" as replacing functional tonality entirely rather than complicating it. `dark_ambient.md` similarly documents "chromatic/atonal clusters" and "unsettling intervals (minor seconds, tritones) used deliberately" where "melody is largely absent or fragmentary" — here chromaticism isn't creating tension against an implied resolution, because there's no functional tonal center for it to resolve against in the first place.

## Distinguishing "Chromatic as Tension Device" From "Chromatic as System"

The practical distinction worth drawing from across these genre files: in Romantic harmony, bossa nova, and bebop's chromatic passing tones, chromatic notes/chords create tension *specifically because* they're heard against an underlying diatonic/tonal expectation that they eventually resolve back into — the chromaticism is meaningful because of the stable key center it's deviating from. In metal's tritone riffing and especially in contemporary classical's serial/microtonal systems, chromatic or non-diatonic material stops functioning as deviation-from-a-center and starts functioning as the actual stable harmonic material in its own right. Applying "tension and resolution" thinking to a genre that's actually working in the second mode (or vice versa) will produce writing that misunderstands what the harmony is doing.

## Common Mistakes

**Adding secondary dominants/chromatic passing chords without an underlying diatonic center for them to create tension against.** These devices only function as *tension* if the listener has an implied "home" key to hear the chromatic material as deviating from — without that context, a secondary dominant just reads as an arbitrary chord change rather than an expressive delay.

**Treating metal's tritone-based riffing as if it needs to resolve, in the Romantic-harmony sense.** As `metal.md` documents, the tritone functions as a stable, repeated color in metal riffing, not a transitional dissonance en route to a consonant resolution — forcing a resolution where the genre doesn't call for one flattens its intended harmonic heaviness.

**Applying functional-tonal tension/resolution logic to serial or microtonal contemporary-classical material.** `contemporary_classical.md`'s twelve-tone and microtonal systems are organized by entirely different principles (tone rows, interval class, just-intonation ratios) than tonal chromaticism, and evaluating them by whether they "resolve" misunderstands the compositional framework.
