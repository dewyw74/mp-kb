## History

Harsh noise developed through the 1980s and 1990s primarily within Japan's "Japanoise" scene, which grew out of the Kansai region's no-wave and underground rock movements. Merzbow — the solo project of Masami Akita, started in 1979 and named after Dada artist Kurt Schwitters' Merzbau assemblage-artwork, reflecting Akita's junk-art aesthetic — became the genre's most prolific and influential figure, releasing over 500 recordings since 1980. Other early Japanese noise acts, including Hijokaidan, Incapacitants, and C.C.C.C., helped establish the sound alongside Merzbow. A pivotal moment in Merzbow's development came during a 1989 European tour, where he could bring only simple, portable gear, pushing him toward the pedal-feedback-driven harsh noise style he became best known for through the 1990s on albums like Venereology (1994) and Pulse Demon (1996). European power electronics (Whitehouse and its lineage) developed in close parallel and exerted a strong mutual influence on harsh noise, though the two genres diverged in emphasis — power electronics remained vocal- and confrontation-centric, while harsh noise (especially the Japanoise strand) leaned toward pure, largely instrumental sonic maximalism. The style was subsequently popularized in the United States by artists such as the Haters and Daniel Menche, and later diversified into related sub-styles including "harsh noise wall" (HNW), which pushes toward a nearly static, continuously undifferentiated wall of maximum-density noise with minimal internal variation.

## Music Theory

Harsh noise operates entirely outside conventional tonal-harmonic and even conventional noise-genre theory — there is no key, scale, chord, or fixed rhythmic pulse. The genre's "content" is instead the internal motion of an otherwise continuous, dense noise mass: shifting register and frequency emphasis, feedback squeals rising and falling in pitch, and dynamic swells within the wall. Where power electronics organizes around screamed vocal performance layered over a noise bed, harsh noise in the Merzbow/Japanoise tradition treats the noise mass itself as the entire musical substance, with no vocal or lyrical focal point required.

## Arrangement

Harsh noise pieces frequently begin already at high density and volume, in contrast to power electronics' more patient drone-based opening — there is often no gradual build to a wall of sound, but an immediate immersion in one. Structural movement happens internally: shifting EQ emphasis, added noise-source layers, and dynamic swells create variation within a continuously dense texture, rather than the track building toward and releasing at a discrete climactic section. Pieces typically end on an abrupt cut, though a slower reduction in density also occurs; conventional musical resolution is absent either way.

## Instruments

The genre's primary "instrument" is a chain of effects pedals (fuzz, distortion, delay) fed back into themselves and into further processing — a technique central to Merzbow's practice — supplemented by contact microphones on junk and metal objects, homemade or circuit-bent electronics, and simple oscillators used purely as tone/noise sources. Unlike power electronics, conventional drums, bass, and vocals are essentially absent; the noise mass generated by the feedback chain is the entire instrumental content.

## Sound Design

Sound design centers on live, physical feedback (pedal-to-pedal, mixing-desk-to-speaker) as the primary generative technique, more than programmed or envelope-based modulation. Extreme, multi-stage distortion and fuzz are stacked to build a denser noise mass, resonant filters are driven into self-oscillation for piercing tonal content, and — particularly in the "harsh noise wall" sub-style — mastering-stage gain is deliberately pushed into clipping, a hallmark first strongly associated with Merzbow's mid-1990s work (Pulse Demon in particular was noted for its unusually loud mastering).

## MIDI Programming

Conventional MIDI sequencing is largely inapplicable — harsh noise is predominantly a live-performance and hardware-feedback practice performed and recorded in continuous takes rather than programmed in a DAW. Where electronic tools are used at all, sustained noise-generator patches and live automation of gain/feedback parameters replace note-based programming entirely; there is no drum pattern, bassline, or melody to program in the conventional sense.

## Mixing

Mixing embraces extremity: EQ reshapes the noise wall's character over time rather than pursuing tonal balance, and extreme compression/limiting is used as a primary sound-shaping tool to intensify and flatten the mass into a denser, more overwhelming whole — a defining technique of the "harsh noise wall" sub-style. Reverb use is inconsistent by design: some pieces add it for scale and disorientation, while much classic harsh noise uses none at all, favoring a dry, suffocating intensity instead.

## Mastering

Mastering targets are highly variable and often intentionally extreme — many releases sit at or near digital full-scale, embracing clipping and distortion as an artistic choice rather than a flaw to correct, continuing a lineage traceable to Merzbow's unusually loud mid-1990s masters. Dynamics are frequently already crushed at the source through pedal and mixing-desk overload, and mastering typically preserves or intensifies this rather than restoring conventional dynamic range.

## Reference Artists

Merzbow (Masami Akita) is the genre's most prolific and influential figure, with Venereology (1994) and Pulse Demon (1996) — the latter mastered at unusually loud, "apocalyptic" levels — standing as essential reference points. Hijokaidan, Incapacitants, and C.C.C.C. represent the broader Japanoise scene from which Merzbow emerged, while the Haters and Daniel Menche extended the sound into the American harsh noise scene from the 1990s onward.

## Production Notes

Build harsh noise from live, physical feedback chains — pedal-to-pedal and mixing-desk-to-speaker routing — rather than relying purely on programmed DAW synthesis, since much of the genre's most respected work is generated exactly this way. Treat volume, density, and register as the compositional parameters themselves, since the genre's internal motion comes from shifting these qualities within a continuous wall rather than through conventional arrangement. The most important distinction to preserve is from power electronics: harsh noise (the Merzbow/Japanoise lineage) is predominantly instrumental and abstractly textural, with the noise mass as the entire content, while power electronics is vocal- and performance-centric, built around screamed confrontational invective over a noise bed. Common mistakes include introducing conventional song structure or melody (which pulls the work toward industrial rather than harsh noise) and over-cleaning the mix in a way that removes the density and physical overload central to the genre's impact. Modern approaches favor Eurorack/modular feedback patching for extended routing flexibility, granular and spectral processing for novel evolving textures, and circuit-bent/homemade electronics for unique noise sources true to the genre's DIY, junk-art roots.
