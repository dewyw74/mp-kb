## History

The sports montage score's modern template was set by Bill Conti's "Gonna Fly Now" for Rocky (1976), which combined "gladiator"-style trumpet fanfare with a funky, disco-adjacent rhythm section rooted in the Philadelphia soul sound of Kenny Gamble and Leon Huff, scoring the film's now-iconic training montage up the steps of the Philadelphia Museum of Art. The song reached number one on the Billboard Hot 100 in 1977, establishing the genre's defining dual identity as both a film cue and a standalone chart single. A second template emerged with Rocky III (1982): after Stallone requested "something street" with a pulse matching the film's boxing scenes (having been denied rights to use Queen's "Another One Bites the Dust"), Survivor's Frankie Sullivan and Jim Peterik wrote "Eye of the Tiger," with Peterik reportedly building the song's guitar riff live while watching the edited montage footage. The song spent six weeks at number one in 1982, cementing the rock-anthem variant of the genre alongside Conti's original fanfare-funk template.

## Music Theory

The genre favors simple, repetitive verse-chorus pop/rock progressions written to function as a radio single as much as a film cue, rather than the through-composed harmonic development typical of orchestral cinematic genres. In the fanfare-funk variant, tonic-dominant brass figures sit over a syncopated disco/funk rhythm-section groove; in the rock-anthem variant, a driving, riff-based ostinato is repeated and intensified rather than harmonically developed — in both cases, the genre's harmonic simplicity is a deliberate structural choice supporting instant recognizability and radio playability.

## Arrangement

A short, immediately identifiable instrumental hook opens the cue, after which lower-intensity material scores the montage's early training beats. Layers are then added progressively — heavier drums, added horns, thickening vocal harmony, rising tempo — timed edit-by-edit to the montage's escalating visuals, culminating in the song's full-arrangement chorus/hook return at the montage's peak visual moment. Unlike orchestral cinematic cues, the arrangement typically carries through at full energy past the visual payoff rather than resolving back to a calmer close.

## Instruments

A song-band arrangement — full rock/funk rhythm section, electric bass, and either brass fanfare or a driving electric guitar riff as the central hook — replaces the full symphony orchestra found in other cinematic genres. Congas and hand percussion add Latin-funk color in the Conti/Rocky tradition specifically, while a distorted electric guitar riff carries the equivalent hook role in the Survivor/rock-anthem variant.

## Sound Design

The genre favors bright, radio-mix-ready production polish — plate or spring reverb on vocals and snare, doubled guitar or vocal tracking, layered vocal harmonies for chorus impact — over the large cinematic scoring-stage reverb used elsewhere in the cinematic category. Progressive arrangement layering, adding instrument groups cue-by-cue in sync with the montage's visual escalation, functions as the genre's primary production-escalation device in place of sound-design processing.

## MIDI Programming

Program a syncopated, funk-influenced bass line locked tightly to the kick, and a steady rock/funk drum backbeat with congas or hand percussion for Latin-funk color, increasing density and energy as the arrangement builds. Write one memorable hook early and repeat it with intensifying arrangement rather than introducing new melodic material, and favor tight, groove-locked timing over orchestral rubato.

## Mixing

Mix for radio-single clarity and punch — bright vocal/lead presence, present snare and kick, and a clear brass or guitar hook — using moderate-to-heavy compression on drums, bass, and vocals in the manner of contemporary pop/rock mixing rather than orchestral scoring restraint. Automation is primarily arrangement-driven, muting and unmuting layered instrument groups in sync with the montage's escalating visuals.

## Mastering

Standalone single/radio releases typically target a loud -9 to -7 LUFS integrated, while film-mix context runs somewhat lower at -12 to -10 LUFS; both maintain a comparatively narrow, consistently high-energy dynamic range appropriate to a radio single rather than the wide dynamic swings of orchestral cinematic genres. Frequency balance favors a bright, present mix with clear vocal/hook presence and punchy low end.

## Reference Artists

Bill Conti's "Gonna Fly Now" (Rocky, 1976) established the fanfare-funk template and reached number one on the Billboard Hot 100. Survivor's "Eye of the Tiger" (Rocky III, 1982), written at Sylvester Stallone's direct request and reportedly composed against the film's edited montage footage, established the genre's rock-anthem variant and spent six weeks at number one on the same chart.

## Production Notes

- Write the central hook first and build the cue's escalation around repeating and layering it rather than developing new melodic material.
- Time each new arrangement layer to a specific visual beat in the edited montage.
- Treat the cue as a potential standalone single — mixing and structuring for independent radio release is a genre-defining convention.
- Escalate primarily through arrangement density and tempo rather than harmonic development.
- Avoid scoring a training montage with an orchestral epic-music approach — the genre's song-based, verse-chorus structure with a repeating hook is a distinct convention.
- Avoid mixing for wide orchestral dynamic range instead of the genre's punchy, consistently high-energy radio-single loudness profile.
