## History

Ragtime emerged in the 1890s from African American musical traditions in the American Midwest, particularly around St. Louis, Missouri, before spreading across the United States and into Europe. Composer Scott Joplin became the genre's central figure, celebrated for pieces like "Maple Leaf Rag" and "The Entertainer"; "Maple Leaf Rag," published in 1899, became a national sensation and sparked a broader ragtime craze, combining African musical syncopation traditions with 19th-century European romantic harmonic language and march-like formal structure. Ragtime is widely regarded as jazz's direct musical precursor, with its syncopated rhythmic vocabulary and piano technique feeding directly into stride piano and early New Orleans jazz in the following decades.

## Music Theory

Ragtime's defining musical trait is a specific type of syncopation in which melodic accents occur between metrical beats — anticipating or following the beat rather than landing squarely on it — creating a melody that appears to "rag" or displace against the underlying steady pulse. Harmonically the genre is diatonic and march-derived, built on functional I-IV-V-I progressions with secondary dominants and passing chords for interest, providing a stable, conventional harmonic bed specifically so the syncopated melodic rhythm can be the piece's primary point of musical tension and interest.

## Arrangement

Ragtime compositions follow march-form structural convention, typically composed of three or four contrasting sections ("strains"), each 16 to 32 measures long, presented and repeated before moving to the next strain rather than following a verse-chorus pop structure. The trio section frequently modulates to a new key and introduces the piece's most memorable melodic material, functioning as the composition's formal and emotional highlight within this multi-strain form.

## Instruments

Ragtime is fundamentally a solo piano genre — the pianist's left hand supplies the bass function directly, alternating a low bass note on beats one and three with a mid-register chord on beats two and four, while the right hand delivers syncopated, ornamented melody. This two-handed technique constitutes the entire essential ragtime piano vocabulary, though ensemble arrangements for dance orchestras and early jazz bands, sometimes including banjo, also existed.

## Sound Design

As a genre predating both electronic instruments and modern studio processing, ragtime's "sound design" is entirely a function of natural acoustic piano timbre and the performer's touch, dynamics, and rhythmic precision. Period recordings and piano rolls (many Joplin performances survive this way) capture natural, unprocessed piano performance, and modern recordings should aim to preserve this same natural acoustic character.

## MIDI Programming

Program the left hand's alternating bass-note-and-chord pattern as the genre's essential rhythmic-harmonic foundation, then write syncopated right-hand melody with accents deliberately falling between the steady pulse's metrical beats — this specific rhythmic-displacement technique, not general bounciness, is what defines authentic ragtime syncopation. Natural piano touch and timing nuance on syncopated accents is essential, since mechanically perfect quantization flattens the rhythmic tension between the two hands that gives ragtime its character.

## Mixing

Mixing should stay natural and minimal — full-range piano EQ preserving the instrument's complete tonal character, minimal to no compression preserving natural acoustic dynamic range, and natural room ambience appropriate to a parlor or small recital space reflecting the genre's period performance context.

## Mastering

Modern recording/reissue masters typically target -16 to -13 LUFS integrated, preserving high dynamic range appropriate to the genre's fundamentally acoustic, unamplified solo piano character, with a full-range, natural piano tonal balance.

## Reference Artists

Scott Joplin's "Maple Leaf Rag" and "The Entertainer" remain the genre's most enduring and widely recognized compositions; James Scott's "Frog Legs Rag" and Joseph Lamb's "Sensation Rag" showcase the broader ragtime repertoire beyond Joplin, representing the genre's other major composers of the classic ragtime era.

## Production Notes

Master the specific left-hand alternating bass-and-chord pattern first, since it is the genre's essential rhythmic-harmonic engine providing the steady pulse against which syncopated melody creates its defining tension. Write genuine syncopation — precise rhythmic displacement, not just an upbeat feel — and structure compositions in the multi-strain march form rather than a pop verse-chorus structure, avoiding over-quantized MIDI performance that flattens the essential rhythmic interplay between the hands.
