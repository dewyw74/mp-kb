## History

Christian metal within black metal's specific musical vocabulary crystallized as "unblack metal" (also called Christian black metal or white metal) starting in 1994, when Australian musician Jayson Sherlock — previously of Mortification and Paramaecium — released Hellig Usvart under the solo project name Horde, using the pseudonym "Anonymous," a likely play on Mayhem guitarist Euronymous's name. Recorded, played, mixed, and produced entirely by Sherlock over four days in July 1994, Hellig Usvart ("Holy Unblack," a deliberate inversion of Darkthrone's "unholy black metal" slogan) brought the concept and term to wide attention and proved deeply controversial within the secular black metal scene, provoking death threats directed at Nuclear Blast Records demanding the anonymous musician's identity be revealed. Norway's Antestor, formed in 1990, released the highly influential The Return of the Black Death in 1998; vocalist Kjetil Molnes stated the band identified with black metal purely as a musical style rather than an ideology, and the band preferred to describe its own music as "sorrow metal" rather than accept the "unblack metal" label outright — illustrating that the scene's own self-description was, and remains, contested even among its foundational acts.

## Music Theory

Unblack metal's harmonic and melodic vocabulary is drawn essentially unchanged from second-wave black metal: hypnotic, swirling tremolo-picked minor and diminished chord arpeggios in natural or harmonic minor. The genre does not introduce a distinct musical theory of its own; its identity operates almost entirely at the level of lyrical and thematic content, inverting black metal's foundational anti-Christian, Satanic ideology into explicitly Christian, worshipful, or redemption-themed lyrics delivered over musically orthodox black metal.

## Arrangement

Song structures follow second-wave black metal convention closely — hypnotic, repetitive verses built from continuous tremolo-picked riffing under blast beats, long evolving atmospheric movements in place of conventional choruses, and occasional dirge-like or dynamically contrasting bridge sections. Some unblack acts introduce a clean, hymn-adjacent melodic passage against the genre's otherwise raw sonic backdrop as a way of musically underscoring lyrical themes of hope or redemption, a subtle but genuine structural device distinct from typical black metal.

## Instruments

Instrumentation is essentially identical to second-wave black metal: treble-heavy, tremolo-picked electric guitar, a raw or thin-sounding acoustic drum kit played with relentless blast beats, minimized or blended-in bass, and atmospheric synth pads or choir patches. The genre does not innovate instrumentally; its distinguishing feature is entirely a matter of the lyrical and ideological content layered onto this shared musical foundation.

## Sound Design

Sound design choices mirror black metal's raw, lo-fi "necro" aesthetic tradition, including deliberately thin guitar tone and cavernous vocal reverb, though production quality varies more widely across the unblack scene than within orthodox second-wave black metal — Horde's Hellig Usvart, recorded and produced single-handedly in four days, stands as a historically important example of this raw, DIY approach applied to the genre's inverted lyrical stance.

## MIDI Programming

Program continuous tremolo-picked guitar riffing (16th/32nd notes) and relentless, loosely washy blast beats in the second-wave black metal tradition, favoring raw human feel over tight, mechanically precise rhythm. Bass typically follows the guitar's root notes, and synth/choir parts sustain long, evolving chords, occasionally shifting toward more consonant or major-key material at points where lyrical content emphasizes hope or redemption.

## Mixing

Mixing follows black metal convention closely: thin, treble-forward guitar tone with de-emphasized low end, light compression preserving raw dynamics, and cavernous, dark reverb on vocals and drums, all regardless of the genre's inverted lyrical content. This is a deliberate departure from the more polished, low-mid-heavy approach typical of mainstream Christian rock/metal crossover acts.

## Mastering

Masters are typically compressed in the same raw, cassette-esque manner as second-wave black metal, at roughly -10 to -12 LUFS integrated, rather than the louder, more polished masters common in mainstream Christian metal or rock. The frequency balance stays cold and treble-heavy, matching black metal's harsh EQ curve rather than a warmer, more radio-friendly one.

## Reference Artists

Horde's Hellig Usvart (1994) remains the genre's foundational, most historically significant recording, both for its music and for the controversy it generated within the black metal scene. Antestor's The Return of the Black Death (1998) is similarly influential, from a band that identified with black metal as a musical style while preferring the term "sorrow metal" for its own work — a useful illustration that the unblack metal label is contested even among the scene's key acts.

## Production Notes

The core production principle is that unblack metal's musical vocabulary should stay faithful to second-wave black metal's raw, treble-forward, tremolo-picked sound — the genre's distinguishing feature is lyrical and ideological, not musical or sonic. Avoid softening the aggression or production rawness in pursuit of a more conventionally "Christian" or radio-friendly sound, since that abandons the genre's actual musical identity. Be aware that the scene is not monolithic in its self-description (Antestor's preference for "sorrow metal" over "unblack metal" is a case in point), and that the genre's history includes real hostility from the broader black metal scene, given second-wave black metal's explicitly anti-Christian founding ideology.
