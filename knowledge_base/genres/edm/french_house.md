## History

French house crystallized in Paris in the mid-1990s around a small, tightly-knit scene of producers — Daft Punk, Stardust, Cassius, Alan Braxe, DJ Falcon, and Motorbass — who built a new dance style on the bones of disco and Chicago house. The catalytic move was the "filter disco" technique: taking a loop from a disco, funk, or boogie record and sweeping a resonant lowpass filter across it to create movement and arrangement, rather than writing new chord parts. Daft Punk's *Homework* (1997) and Stardust's "Music Sounds Better with You" (1998) became the genre's defining statements, taking the sound from Paris clubs to mainstream chart success. The scene was small enough that its key players frequently collaborated and traded remixes, giving the genre a tight sonic identity in a short window. By the early 2000s the "filter house" wave had crested, but its DNA — filtered loops, sidechain pumping, disco-sourced warmth — resurfaced repeatedly in the 2010s nu-disco and "French touch" revival scenes and remains a foundational reference point for melodic and disco-house producers today.

## Music Theory

Harmonic movement in French house is almost always inherited rather than composed: producers loop a 1-4 bar chord or vocal phrase from a disco or funk record, so the "progression" is whatever the source material already contains — commonly simple i-VI-VII vamps or major-key ii-V-I disco-chorus loops. This makes music theory in the genre less about writing new harmony and more about *selection* — picking loop material with a satisfying harmonic loop point and a chord voicing rich enough to sound interesting even when heavily filtered. Where original material is composed (bassline riffs, new stabs), it stays deliberately simple and repetitive — one or two-bar funky vamps in natural minor or Dorian, mirroring the source disco material's harmonic language rather than introducing new complexity.

## Arrangement

French house arrangement is built around filter state rather than traditional verse/chorus structure. A track typically opens with the core loop heavily filtered — muffled, mostly bass and a hint of the chord loop — before the kick enters and the filter begins a slow, multi-bar sweep toward fully open. The "drop" moment in French house is simply the loop at full brightness, unfiltered, alongside a sidechained bassline and crisp drums. Breakdowns strip back to drums and bass alone, sometimes introducing a vocal chop, before the filter re-opens for a second full-loop section. The outro typically mirrors the intro, closing the filter back down as elements are progressively removed — an audio bookend that makes the whole track feel like a single continuous filter journey.

## Instruments

The core "instrument" in French house is the chopped disco/funk sample loop itself — vintage drum breaks, clavinet or Rhodes chord stabs, and occasionally disco strings or a vocal snippet, all sourced from period-correct records and treated as raw material. Alongside this, a synthesized or sampled bassline (often Moog- or TB-303-adjacent in tone) provides the low-end groove, programmed with short, funky, syncopated patterns. Drums blend the swing and character of sampled breaks with a modern, punchy 909-style kick and clap for club-system impact. Live instrumentation (guitar, strings) appears almost exclusively via samples rather than reperformed parts, keeping the sonic palette anchored in its disco source material.

## Sound Design

The signature sound-design gesture is the resonant lowpass filter sweep, manually automated or LFO-driven, opening and closing across the sample loop to create both texture and arrangement movement — no other single technique defines the genre as clearly. Bitcrushing, sample-rate reduction, and vinyl noise are layered onto loops to preserve or exaggerate a lo-fi, "dug from a crate" character, while analog-style saturation and tape emulation glue sampled and synthesized elements into a cohesive, warm whole. Basslines and stabs are typically built from simple analog subtractive synthesis (sawtooth/square oscillators through a resonant filter) to sit comfortably alongside the vintage sample material without sounding digitally sterile.

## MIDI Programming

Because much of the genre's melodic content comes from audio loops rather than MIDI, programming focuses primarily on drums and bass. Basslines are short, syncopated 1-2 bar patterns with octave jumps, programmed with a swing percentage around 54-58% to match the feel of the sampled breaks they sit alongside — rigid, unswung quantization is one of the fastest ways to kill the groove. Drum programming layers a four-on-the-floor kick with off-beat open hats, claps or snares on 2 and 4, and swung 16th-note hi-hat/shaker patterns. Velocity on sample triggers should preserve the natural dynamic variation of the source performance rather than flattening everything to a uniform level.

## Mixing

Sidechain compression from the kick into the bass and the main sample loop is both a rhythmic effect and a structural mix tool, carving space on every beat and producing the genre's signature pumping groove. Sampled loops are high-passed aggressively (often below 150-200Hz) to leave clean room for the bassline, while a presence boost in the 2-5kHz range keeps filtered loops audible and present even when the cutoff is swept low. Reverb use is deliberately minimal and tight — short room or plate on drums only — favoring a dry, upfront, club-ready groove over spacious ambience. Filter cutoff automation functions as the primary arrangement tool: most section changes are achieved by opening or closing the filter rather than adding or removing many discrete elements.

## Mastering

Target loudness sits around -9 to -7 LUFS integrated, dynamic enough to preserve the pumping impact of the sidechain groove and the drama of filter sweeps rather than being flattened for maximum loudness. Frequency balance carries a warm low-mid character from analog and tape-style saturation, a punchy but controlled sub region, and a smooth (not harsh) filtered top end consistent with the genre's vintage-disco tonal identity — this is not a bright, aggressive master in the modern festival-EDM mold.

## Reference Artists

Daft Punk's *Homework* remains the essential reference for the genre's filtered-loop, sidechain-driven sound; "Around the World" is a masterclass in loop-based arrangement built almost entirely from repetition and filter movement. Stardust's "Music Sounds Better with You" is the genre's most commercially iconic single and a perfect study in the filter-sweep build/drop structure. Cassius, Alan Braxe & Fred Falke, DJ Falcon, and Bob Sinclar round out the essential catalog, each demonstrating variations on the filtered-disco-loop formula with different degrees of warmth, minimalism, or pop crossover appeal.

## Production Notes

Treat the filter sweep as the arrangement itself, not an afterthought effect — plan when the filter opens and closes before worrying about adding new elements, since that movement alone can carry an entire track's structure. Chop loops on their natural musical boundaries and preserve the source material's swing rather than re-quantizing to a rigid grid, which flattens the groove that makes the genre danceable. Sidechain virtually everything against the kick; the pumping compression is as much a genre identifier as the filtered loop itself. Common mistakes include using pristine, unfiltered modern samples throughout (which erases the genre's lo-fi identity), over-designing a modern sub-bass that clashes tonally with vintage disco material, and neglecting groove/swing when programming new parts from scratch. Modern producers often get around sample-clearance issues by writing fully original chord loops in a disco style and applying the same filter-sweep treatment, or by layering a modern sub-bass beneath a filtered vintage-style bassline for extra low-end weight on today's club systems.
