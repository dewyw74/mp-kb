## History

Spectral music (French: musique spectrale, a term coined by composer Hugues Dufourt in 1979) emerged in Paris in the early-to-mid 1970s, centered on the ensemble L'Itinéraire (founded 1973) and closely associated with IRCAM, with Gérard Grisey and Tristan Murail as its most influential figures. The movement arose as a deliberate reaction against integral serialism and the Darmstadt School's abstract, arithmetic approach to pitch organization, which spectral composers viewed as producing perceptually disconnected, "harmonically gray" music; instead, they turned to the direct acoustic analysis of sound itself — most famously, Grisey's Partiels (1975) opens by orchestrally reconstructing a sonogram-derived analysis of a trombone's low E2 attack, assigning each of the first seven overtone partials to a different instrument in a technique Grisey called "instrumental synthesis."

## Music Theory

Rather than serialism's twelve-tone rows or traditional tonal harmony, spectral music derives its pitch and harmonic material directly from the overtone spectrum of an analyzed acoustic sound, approximating each partial to the nearest quarter-tone (or finer) and treating the resulting microtonal "chord" as an orchestrated acoustic spectrum rather than a functional harmonic sonority. Large-scale harmonic movement in spectral works typically consists of a gradual interpolation between harmonic (consonant, overtone-aligned) and inharmonic (dissonant, non-overtone-aligned) spectra, replacing tonal tension-and-resolution with a continuous process of spectral transformation.

## Arrangement

Spectral works are process-based rather than divided into discrete movements with a narrative arc: a piece's structure tracks the degree of transformation applied to an initial analyzed sound-spectrum, often opening with the fundamental and lowest partials before higher partials enter successively (mirroring how a sonogram reveals a sound's overtone structure unfolding over time), and building tension through increasing spectral density or dissonance rather than conventional harmonic or rhythmic means.

## Instruments

Instrumentation in spectral music is chosen specifically to model an analyzed sound's overtone structure, with individual instruments or instrument groups assigned to a single partial — making orchestration itself the genre's primary harmonic-generative technique. Some works, most notably Murail's Désintégrations (1982-83), extend this acoustic "instrumental synthesis" into hybrid territory by combining the acoustic ensemble with pre-recorded electronic/tape material.

## Sound Design

Spectral music's defining "sound design" technique is genuinely analytical rather than synthetic: composers use electronic sonogram analysis of a real recorded sound to derive the overtone/partial data that then determines orchestration and microtonal pitch material, rather than using electronics to generate sound directly (as in much other electroacoustic contemporary classical work). Where electronics do appear (ring modulation, tape playback in Désintégrations), they extend rather than replace this acoustically-grounded compositional method.

## MIDI Programming

There is no conventional melody to program in spectral music — instead, each instrumental part should be mapped to a specific analyzed partial, with careful attention to precise microtonal tuning (often quarter-tone) and to how each partial's assignment and prominence changes as the work's spectrum gradually transforms. Dynamic balance between partials must be graded precisely to mimic natural acoustic decay and emphasis patterns, since the audible "chord" is really an orchestrated overtone spectrum rather than a conventional harmony.

## Mixing

Mixing must preserve the exact balance between partials as scored, since the perceptual illusion of a single, continuously evolving timbre depends on the ensemble's collective spectral blend rather than any single instrument's prominence; excessive compression or artificial reverb both work against this precise partial-balance and should be minimized.

## Mastering

Masters should preserve maximum dynamic range (roughly -18 to -15 LUFS integrated) and a precisely balanced full-spectrum frequency profile, since the entire compositional concept depends on accurately reproducing an orchestrated overtone spectrum rather than a conventional tonal frequency balance.

## Reference Artists

Grisey's Partiels (1975) is spectral music's foundational, most frequently cited work, demonstrating "instrumental synthesis" directly from a trombone sonogram analysis; his cycle Les Espaces Acoustiques (1974-85) extends the technique across an ensemble scaling from solo viola to full orchestra; Murail's Gondwana (1980) and Désintégrations (1982-83, combining acoustic ensemble with electronics) represent the movement's other major compositional voice.

## Production Notes

Ground spectral composition in a genuine acoustic analysis of a real recorded sound rather than an approximated or merely "spectral-sounding" dissonant chord, and treat orchestration itself — assigning instruments to specific analyzed partials — as the primary harmonic-generative act rather than a later coloristic decision. Avoid confusing spectral music with serialism, since spectralism was explicitly a reaction against serialism's abstract, mathematically-constructed pitch rows, deriving its material instead from the acoustic reality of a sound's own overtone spectrum.
