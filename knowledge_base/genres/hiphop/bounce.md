## History

Bounce originated as early as the late 1980s in New Orleans's housing projects, characterized by call-and-response-style party and Mardi Gras Indian chants and dance call-outs, frequently hypersexual and controversial in content. Popular early artists including DJ Jubilee, DJ Jimi, Partners-N-Crime, Pimp Daddy, and Cheeky Blakk established the genre's repetitive call-and-response vocal delivery, inspired by the Mardi Gras Indian chants common at New Orleans festivals and block parties, almost universally rapped over the sampled "Triggerman beat" (drawn from The Showboys' "Drag Rap" and Cameron Paul's "Brown Beats"). Big Freedia, dubbed the "Queen of Bounce," has been a fixture in the New Orleans music scene for over two decades and has been central to popularizing bounce music outside the city, with the genre also serving as an important home for women and queer artists within rap more broadly.

## Music Theory

Bounce largely abandons conventional harmonic movement — tracks are built almost entirely around the sampled Triggerman beat and repetitive rhythmic chant rather than chord progression, with simple, repetitive sample loops providing rhythmic-melodic content in place of conventional harmony. This structural minimalism keeps focus entirely on the genre's real content: call-and-response vocal chant and roll-call-style local reference.

## Arrangement

Tracks establish the Triggerman beat's rhythmic foundation immediately — a near-universal genre convention — with verses frequently taking the form of a "roll call" naming New Orleans wards, neighborhoods, or specific crowd members, reflecting the genre's deeply local, communal identity. The genre favors sustained, danceable repetition over dynamic escalation, functioning primarily as continuous dance-floor music with extended sections built for sustained physical performance and audience participation.

## Instruments

The Triggerman beat is the genre's near-universal rhythmic foundation, used across countless bounce recordings and functioning as the style's most immediately recognizable sonic signature. A minimal, DJ/producer-driven production approach — centered almost entirely on this sampled beat plus call-and-response vocal chant — defines the genre's core instrumental method, with simple, repetitive bass and synth elements playing a purely supportive role.

## Sound Design

The genre favors direct, minimally processed presentation over elaborate sound design, with repetitive vocal chant and call-and-response structure serving as the primary rhythmic-melodic devices rather than instrumental complexity. Hard-hitting, punchy drum bus processing optimizes the track for maximum club and block-party sound-system impact, consistent with the genre's functional, physical dance-music role.

## MIDI Programming

Program the Triggerman beat pattern (or a close variant) as the genre's near-universal rhythmic foundation, since this specific sampled beat unifies the vast majority of bounce recordings across decades. Write repetitive call-and-response vocal chants and roll calls as the genre's primary melodic and structural content, keeping instrumental elements simple and supportive.

## Mixing

EQ should stay bright and punchy, emphasizing the Triggerman beat's rhythmic elements and vocal chant clarity for maximum dance-floor impact, with heavy compression supporting consistent club and block-party sound-system impact. Reverb and delay should stay minimal, keeping the mix tight, direct, and immediate.

## Mastering

Masters typically target a loud -8 to -6 LUFS integrated, club and block-party-ready, with lower dynamic range and hard-hitting, consistent punch throughout the track.

## Reference Artists

DJ Jubilee's "Do the Jubilee All" represents the genre's foundational sound; Big Freedia's "Explode" showcases the genre's modern global visibility, while Partners-N-Crime's "Pussy Cat Glide" and Magnolia Shorty's work demonstrate the genre's classic call-and-response, roll-call structural tradition.

## Production Notes

Build the track around the Triggerman beat as the genre's near-universal rhythmic and structural foundation, and use call-and-response roll-call structure to reflect New Orleans's deeply local, communal cultural identity. Design the track for extended, continuous dance-floor function, and avoid abandoning the Triggerman beat foundation or overcomplicating the instrumental arrangement — both undercut what makes bounce recognizably itself.
