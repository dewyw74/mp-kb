## History

Chopped and screwed was pioneered by DJ Screw (born Robert Earl Davis Jr.) on Houston's Southside in the late 1980s and early 1990s, developing out of informal mixtape sessions where he experimented with pitch-shifted playback — originally using the analog technique of playing 45 RPM vinyl records at 33⅓ RPM, which proportionally slowed the tempo by roughly 26-33% while simultaneously transposing the pitch down by an equivalent amount. DJ Screw began distributing his now-legendary "Screwtapes" in 1993, ultimately releasing over 400 of them through his record shop, featuring slowed, reworked versions of popular tracks alongside new freestyle verses from Houston-area rappers including Big Moe, Lil' Keke, and Fat Pat, all part of DJ Screw's collective, the Screwed Up Click (S.U.C.). His June 27, 1996 freestyle tape — recorded during rapper DeMo's birthday celebration and featuring Big Moe, Big Pokey, Yungstar, Key-C, and Haircut Joe over a 35-minute session — is widely considered his magnum opus and remains a defining document of the style.

## Music Theory

Chopped and screwed doesn't compose original harmonic material — it reworks existing hip-hop recordings, transposing the source track's key downward by roughly 3-4 semitones as a direct byproduct of the tempo-reduction process. All harmonic and melodic content is inherited from the source material; the genre's identity is defined by transformation technique rather than original composition.

## Arrangement

A screwed track retains its source material's underlying structure but reworks it through chopping — cutting, repeating, and stuttering vocal phrases — combined with backspins, brakes, and stop-time pauses that exaggerate groove and space well beyond the original recording's arrangement. DJ Screw's original mixtapes frequently interspersed freestyle sections from Houston-area rappers over the screwed instrumentals, and tracks typically flow directly into the next screwed selection, reflecting the format's continuous-mix mixtape origins.

## Instruments

All instrumentation is inherited directly from the source recording being reworked — chopped and screwed is fundamentally a turntable and mixtape production technique applied to pre-existing hip-hop tracks, not a from-scratch compositional or instrumental style. The pitch-down process amplifies the perceived weight of whatever bass content exists in the source material significantly.

## Sound Design

DJ Screw's signature technique involved playing the same record on two turntables with a one-beat delay between them, then rapidly moving the crossfader back and forth to repeat words or sounds without interrupting the underlying tempo — the genre's defining sound-design device. Backspins, record scratching, and stop-time pauses, all applied live via turntable manipulation, complete the technique's original sonic toolkit; the analog pitch-down itself (originally achieved by playing 45 RPM vinyl at 33⅓ RPM) is the transformation at the genre's core.

## MIDI Programming

When producing original material intended for chopped-and-screwed treatment, write and record vocal and melodic content slightly higher in pitch and faster in tempo than the intended final result, anticipating the downward pitch/tempo shift the screwing process applies — composing at a standard 80-100 BPM hip-hop tempo intended to be slowed to 60-70 BPM replicates the technique authentically. Drum programming should follow the same logic, since the screwing process itself produces the genre's characteristic drag and weight rather than a distinct drum-programming style.

## Mixing

Emphasize and control the amplified low end that results naturally from the pitch-down process, while ensuring vocal clarity survives the slowing and thickening without becoming muddy. Compression should stay light to moderate, since heavy compression can work against the genre's already-thick, syrupy low end. The crossfader-based word/sound repetition technique functions as the genre's signature rhythmic-delay effect, applied live via turntable manipulation in its original form.

## Mastering

Masters target roughly -11 to -8 LUFS integrated, with dynamics generally consistent with the source hip-hop material's own mastering approach, adjusted for the significantly amplified bass weight the screwing process introduces. The frequency balance is defined by this amplified low end alongside maintained vocal clarity despite the thickened, slurred vocal texture central to the genre's identity.

## Reference Artists

DJ Screw's "June 27" (1996) stands as the genre's essential reference recording — a 35-minute freestyle session widely regarded as his magnum opus. Fat Pat, Big Moe, and the broader Screwed Up Click collective, whose members appeared throughout DJ Screw's hundreds of Screwtapes, are the genre's other foundational figures.

## Production Notes

- Slow source material to roughly 60-70 BPM from an original 80-100 BPM range for the genre's signature hypnotic, drawn-out foundation.
- Use the crossfader-delay technique (or its modern digital equivalent) to create repeated words/sounds without interrupting tempo.
- Incorporate backspins, record scratching, and stop-time pauses to exaggerate groove and space.
- If writing original material intended for this treatment, record it slightly higher in pitch than the intended final result.
- Avoid simply time-stretching without pitch-shifting down — the pitch drop is not optional, and chopping and screwing must be combined, not treated as separate techniques.
- Modern hybrid approach: use DAW pitch/time-stretch tools set to reduce tempo and pitch together (replicating analog turntable-speed reduction), and digitally sample-chop vocal phrases to replicate the crossfader-delay technique.
