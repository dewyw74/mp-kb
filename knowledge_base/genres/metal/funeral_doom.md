## History

Funeral doom emerged from Finland in the early-to-mid 1990s as a small number of bands with death metal roots began deconstructing that genre, pushing doom metal's core principles — slow, dark, heavy — to their natural structural extreme. Thergothon was effectively a death metal band on their 1991 demo before releasing Stream from the Heavens in 1994, an album widely cited as the first true funeral doom recording for its plodding tempos, often below 50 BPM, and oppressive, mortuary atmosphere; its opening track, "Everlasting," exemplifies the style. Skepticism followed closely with Stormcrowfleet (1995), the genre's other foundational album — the genre's very name may derive from Skepticism's pipe-organ-like keyboard timbre, which listeners likened directly to funeral music. Alongside Unholy, Thergothon and Skepticism are commonly cited together as the genre's three earliest, foundational bands, and by the late 1990s funeral doom had coalesced into a recognizable idiom built on ultra-slow tempos, minimal harmonic motion, and liturgical or funereal color via organ, choir pads, and tolling bells.

## Music Theory

Funeral doom largely abandons conventional harmonic progression in favor of minimal harmonic motion — long-form drones and sustained pedal tones replace chord changes as the primary compositional material, with movement (such as i-iv) unfolding across extremely long timeframes rather than conventional bar-by-bar changes. Melodic interest comes from the glacial development of simple thematic ideas over very long spans rather than phrase-based melody, and cavernous death-growl vocals, where present, are delivered at the genre's characteristically extreme slow pace.

## Arrangement

Songs are built as long-form structures — often 10-20 minutes or more — that open with extended, organ-led or drone-based passages establishing funereal atmosphere well before any riff or vocal enters. Rather than conventional riff repetition or a build-and-drop structure, the arrangement evolves through gradual textural shifts (an added organ layer, a choir pad, a second guitar drone) unfolding over minutes, with a sparse climax emerging from accumulated weight rather than a conventional chorus, and an extended denouement that dissolves rather than resolves on a defined cadence.

## Instruments

Sustained, heavily down-tuned guitar chords function as long-form drones rather than conventional riffs, paired with cavernous, sustained bass and sparse, isolated drum hits that emphasize weight and space over rhythmic drive. Church organ or organ-emulating keyboard is a genre-defining lead voice, directly responsible for the genre's liturgical, funereal atmosphere, and choir pads or tolling-bell samples appear in many recordings for additional atmospheric, liturgical color.

## Sound Design

The genre's signature keyboard voice — church or pipe organ emulation — is central to its liturgical atmosphere, often credited as the direct source of the genre's name. Expansive reverb with very long decay times is one of funeral doom's most defining production choices, creating the genre's immense, immersive sound field, while distortion coloration on guitars stays comparatively minimal relative to more aggressive metal subgenres, favoring weight and space over raw aggression.

## MIDI Programming

Program long, sustained bass notes reinforcing the guitar's drone rather than rhythmic activity, and treat silence and space in drum programming as deliberately as the hits themselves. Melodic material should develop glacially over very long timeframes — often multiple minutes per idea — rather than following conventional, quickly-repeating phrase structures, and humanization should stay minimal since the genre's power comes from sustained, deliberate stillness rather than performance looseness.

## Mixing

Give the down-tuned guitar and bass deep, weighty low-end presence, with organ/keyboard occupying clear midrange space for its liturgical character and vocals (where present) sitting low and cavernous rather than cutting brightly through the mix. Compression should stay minimal to light, since the genre's structural identity depends on genuine, uncompressed dynamic swells across long timeframes — and expansive, long-decay reverb is essential to realizing the genre's immense, immersive sound field. Automation of level and reverb send across very long, slow timeframes is necessary to realize the genre's gradual dynamic evolution.

## Mastering

Masters target roughly -18 to -14 LUFS integrated, with maximum dynamic range preservation essential — the genre's entire expressive power depends on genuine, uncompressed contrast between near-silence and sparse climactic weight across very long song forms. The frequency balance favors a massive, weighty low end, a present but non-harsh organ/guitar midrange, and a restrained top end consistent with the genre's somber character.

## Reference Artists

Thergothon's "Everlasting" (1994, from Stream from the Heavens) is widely regarded as the first true funeral doom recording and remains the genre's essential foundational reference. Skepticism's Stormcrowfleet (1995) stands alongside it as the genre's other defining album, and Unholy completes the trio of foundational Finnish bands credited with establishing the style.

## Production Notes

- Commit fully to extremely slow tempos (often below 50 BPM) and long song forms (10-20+ minutes) — the genre's patience is structural, not incidental.
- Use church-organ or organ-emulating keyboard as a genuinely central voice for the genre's defining liturgical atmosphere.
- Build dynamic interest through gradual textural layering over minutes rather than conventional riff repetition.
- Preserve maximum dynamic range at the mastering stage — heavy limiting directly undermines the genre's core expressive mechanism.
- Avoid writing at ordinary doom-metal tempo or compressing the master heavily — both work directly against the genre's identity.
- Modern hybrid approach: use pipe organ sample libraries for an authentic central keyboard voice, and apply long-decay convolution reverb for the genre's immense sound field.
