## History

Modern war film scoring bifurcated into two distinct traditions at the end of the 20th and start of the 21st century. John Williams's score for Saving Private Ryan (1998), recorded with the Boston Symphony Orchestra and Tanglewood Festival Chorus, established the elegiac/memorial lineage with "Hymn to the Fallen" — a restrained, hymn-like chorale placed over the closing credits specifically to honor the fallen rather than to celebrate victory, described by Spielberg as showing Williams's "sensitivity and brilliance." Nearly two decades later, Hans Zimmer's score for Christopher Nolan's Dunkirk (2017) established a starkly different tension-based lineage: Nolan explicitly asked Zimmer not to write emotional music, requesting suspense and tension instead, and Zimmer built the score around three simultaneous Shepard-tone scales — an auditory illusion of endless ascending pitch, first described by psychologist Roger Shepard — operating on three different timescales to represent the film's three interwoven timelines (a week, a day, and an hour). These two approaches, elegiac restraint and visceral tension, together define the modern genre.

## Music Theory

Elegiac war-film cues rely on slow, hymn-like diatonic chorale harmony — plagal and authentic cadences in the tradition of a memorial hymn — deliberately avoiding the triumphant resolution common to heroic cinematic genres. Tension-based combat cues instead replace harmonic progression with continuous, non-resolving pitch material, most distinctively the Shepard tone: stacked octave tones moving in perpetual, illusory ascent that never actually resolves, functioning as a structural analogue for "the idea of eternity and endlessness," in Zimmer's own description of the device's intent.

## Arrangement

Combat-focused scores often open with a sparse, mechanical pulse device — a ticking watch, a rhythmic ostinato — establishing dread before any melodic material appears, then escalate tension through layered, simultaneous devices (multiple Shepard scales at different rates) rather than conventional orchestral buildup toward a heroic climax. Rather than a triumphant structural peak, the genre's emotional high point is frequently an aftermath or memorial cue — a restrained brass-and-choir chorale mourning the dead — placed at a film's lowest emotional point or over its closing credits, with a sudden drop to near-silence often separating combat from memorial material to let the human cost register.

## Instruments

Solemn, often muted or restrained brass chorale and mixed choir carry elegiac material, deliberately avoiding fanfare-like bombast; military-style rudimental snare patterns support period-authentic march and memorial cues. Combat and tension material instead draws on extended string technique — high-register sustains, sul ponticello — blended with synthesized tone stacks, replacing conventional full-orchestra tutti with a hybrid acoustic-electronic dread texture.

## Sound Design

Shepard-tone synthesis — stacked octave tones engineered in continuous, illusory ascent — is the genre's signature modern sound-design device for tension writing, alongside processed ticking-clock or watch samples used as a mechanical rhythmic pulse. A defining genre convention, established explicitly on Dunkirk, is the deliberate avoidance of conventional "emotional" scoring devices (swelling strings, resolving harmony) during depicted combat itself, reserving such material for aftermath and memorial cues instead.

## MIDI Programming

Program sustained low pedal tones for elegiac harmonic material, or slow, continuously rising stacked-octave patterns to emulate Shepard-tone construction for tension cues; write restrained, narrow-range, stepwise chorale melodies for memorial material while minimizing or eliminating conventional melody during combat tension cues in favor of sustained texture and pitch automation. Elegiac brass and choir benefit from natural ensemble rubato, while tension-based material is often intentionally mechanical — precise, unwavering timing (the ticking-clock aesthetic) is the desired effect, not a flaw to humanize away.

## Mixing

Elegiac cues should preserve warm, present brass and clear choir diction in a spacious, dignified concert-hall reverb; tension cues should balance synthetic tone stacks against extended string technique in a tighter, more claustrophobic space to support a sense of confinement. Compression should stay light and transparent in both lineages — heavy limiting undermines both the elegiac material's quiet dignity and the tension material's sustained, unresolved pressure.

## Mastering

Film-context masters typically target -18 to -14 LUFS integrated, with standalone soundtrack releases running slightly louder at -14 to -12 LUFS, preserving wide dynamic range essential to both lineages. Elegiac material favors a warm, full brass-and-choir frequency balance, while tension material favors extended low and high frequency extremes with a comparatively sparse midrange — echoing horror scoring's frequency approach in service of dread rather than horror's dissonant intent.

## Reference Artists

John Williams's "Hymn to the Fallen," from Saving Private Ryan (1998), defines the genre's elegiac/memorial lineage, its restrained chorale scored specifically for remembrance rather than triumph. Hans Zimmer's score for Dunkirk (2017), built on three simultaneous Shepard-tone scales representing the film's three interwoven timelines, defines the genre's visceral tension-based lineage, composed at director Christopher Nolan's explicit request for suspense over emotion.

## Production Notes

- Decide early which war-scoring lineage a project calls for — elegiac/memorial restraint or tension-based textural dread — since the two use nearly opposite techniques.
- Reserve melodic or harmonic payoff for aftermath and memorial cues rather than combat sequences themselves.
- Engineer Shepard-tone or similar continuous-tension pitch automation carefully across a cue's full length so the illusion of endless ascent reads seamlessly.
- Use a ticking clock or mechanical pulse sample as a structural backbone for tension cues rather than a conventional rhythm section, where a visceral approach is called for.
- Avoid scoring combat sequences with triumphant, heroic material in the mode of epic or adventure scoring — the modern tradition deliberately withholds this payoff during violence.
- Avoid over-compressing tension material, which destroys the sustained, unresolved pressure a drone or Shepard-tone approach depends on.
