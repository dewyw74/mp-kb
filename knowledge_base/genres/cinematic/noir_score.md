## History

Noir scoring's foundational sound was set by Miklós Rózsa's score for Billy Wilder's Double Indemnity (1944), widely regarded as the ur-noir score. Rózsa deliberately avoided jazz — despite the genre's frequent later association with it — reasoning that the film's characters were sinister rather than jazzy, and instead built a brooding, expressionist orchestral vocabulary announcing the protagonist's fate from the main title's tolling horn-and-timpani statement onward. A decade later, a parallel "crime jazz" scoring tradition emerged: Elmer Bernstein's score for The Man with the Golden Arm (1955) is considered one of the era's finest jazz scores, its angularity mirroring the film's narrative structure, while Henry Mancini's score for Orson Welles's Touch of Evil (1958) — a Stan Kenton-influenced blend of jazz, rhythm & blues, and Latin/exotica color performed by a jazz combo including saxophonist Plas Johnson and drummer Shelly Manne — extended noir and neo-noir scoring firmly into jazz-combo territory. These two lineages, orchestral-expressionist and jazz-combo, together define the genre's musical vocabulary.

## Music Theory

Noir scoring favors minor-key harmony colored by chromaticism and extended jazz voicings rather than either horror's atonal clusters or epic music's functional heroic progressions. The jazz-combo lineage uses minor ii-V-i cadences and extended (9th, 11th, altered) chords borrowed from bebop and cool-jazz harmony, while the orchestral-expressionist lineage favors ominous, tolling pedal-tone harmony and chromatic, non-functional voice-leading that deliberately resists cadential resolution — mirroring a genre built around moral ambiguity and fatalism rather than clear dramatic payoff.

## Arrangement

Scores typically open with a main-title cue stating the film's central "fate" motif over a tolling pedal tone, announcing doom immediately rather than building toward it. Sparse, chromatic tension cues underscore investigation and dialogue scenes, while diegetic jazz-club source cues — a saxophone or small combo heard as part of the story world — provide atmospheric contrast to the non-diegetic orchestral suspense material. Climactic confrontation or reveal cues typically resolve into bleak, unresolved harmony rather than a triumphant orchestral payoff, and scores frequently end ambiguously, reinforcing noir's characteristic lack of easy resolution.

## Instruments

Muted and con-sordino brass, low string textures, and — in the jazz lineage — a prominent solo saxophone or trumpet form the genre's core voice, with full orchestral forces reserved for main-title and climactic cues rather than continuous underscore. Upright bass carries a walking jazz line in combo cues or sustains a tolling pedal tone in the orchestral-expressionist lineage, and piano appears as a recurring sparse, bluesy color in jazz-combo material.

## Sound Design

The classic tradition is essentially acoustic, relying on mute choice (Harmon mute, cup mute, con sordino string technique) rather than electronic processing for its tonal character, giving the genre its claustrophobic, smoky quality. Mixing contrasts dry, intimate jazz-combo source cues — suggesting a small club or interior space — against more reverberant orchestral suspense and main-title material, distinguishing diegetic from non-diegetic scoring.

## MIDI Programming

Program a walking jazz bass line for combo cues or a slow, sustained pedal tone in low strings for orchestral suspense material, and write sultry, rhythmically loose saxophone or muted-trumpet lines using extensive pitch-bend and portamento to emulate authentic jazz phrasing. Swing feel and micro-timing looseness are essential for jazz-combo cues to avoid a quantized, inauthentic read, while orchestral suspense material benefits more from natural rubato than mechanical precision.

## Mixing

EQ should emphasize the muted, midrange-forward character of con-sordino brass and solo saxophone, avoiding excess brightness that would undercut the genre's shadowed tonal identity. Reverb should stay dry and intimate for jazz-combo source cues, suggesting a small club space, while orchestral suspense and main-title cues can use more cavernous space; stereo imaging should stay narrower and more intimate for combo cues versus wider for orchestral material.

## Mastering

Film-context masters typically target -18 to -14 LUFS integrated, with standalone soundtrack releases running slightly louder at -14 to -12 LUFS, preserving moderate-to-wide dynamic range between intimate jazz-combo atmosphere and the genre's comparatively rare full-orchestral climactic statements. Frequency balance favors a midrange-forward presentation emphasizing muted brass, saxophone, and upright bass, with a deliberately less bright top end than most other cinematic genres.

## Reference Artists

Miklós Rózsa's Double Indemnity (1944) established the genre's jazz-free, orchestral-expressionist foundation, deliberately avoiding jazz in favor of brooding, fatalistic orchestral dread. Elmer Bernstein's The Man with the Golden Arm (1955) and Henry Mancini's Touch of Evil (1958) established the parallel crime-jazz lineage now equally central to the genre's identity, bringing jazz-combo instrumentation directly into noir and neo-noir scoring.

## Production Notes

- Choose a lane deliberately — orchestral-expressionist dread or jazz-combo atmosphere — rather than blending both indiscriminately.
- Use a tolling, unresolved pedal tone under a main-title fate statement to announce doom immediately.
- In jazz-combo cues, let the solo saxophone or muted trumpet function as a character's interior voice with genuine swing feel rather than quantized precision.
- End cues and scores ambiguously where appropriate, reinforcing the genre's moral ambiguity.
- Avoid assuming noir scoring is always jazz-based, and avoid confusing it with horror scoring's atonal dissonance — noir relies on tonal, chromatically colored harmony.
- Modern hybrid approach: use a small, intimately mic'd jazz combo for authentic source-music cues, and reference specific mute types when programming brass for tonal accuracy.
