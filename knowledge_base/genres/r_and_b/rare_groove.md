## History

Rare groove originated as a London club and pirate-radio DJ culture in the mid-1980s, built around unearthing and replaying obscure American funk, soul, and jazz-funk singles from the 1960s and 1970s that had gone commercially unnoticed on first release. The term was coined by DJ Norman Jay after his Original Rare Groove Show on the pirate station Kiss FM (the forerunner of Kiss 100 London), though underground DJs Barrie Sharpe and Lascelles Gordon (later of the Brand New Heavies) are credited with developing the scene's crate-digging aesthetic before it had a name. The movement placed particular emphasis on politically charged dance-funk connected to the US Black Power and civil rights era, and its "recovery, repackaging, and retrieval" ethos, carried through underground clubs and pirate radio, provided the foundational repertoire of breakbeats that UK sampling culture — hip-hop production and, by the early 1990s, jungle and breakbeat hardcore — extended into new forms; DJs Norman Jay and Gilles Peterson helped carry the sound to wider audiences and directly into the early UK acid jazz scene.

## Music Theory

Because rare groove is a curatorial DJ practice rather than a new compositional style, its "harmonic vocabulary" is whatever the original 1960s-70s funk, soul, and jazz-funk source recordings contain — vamped one- or two-chord funk grooves and simple I-IV-V soul progressions prized specifically for a strong drum or instrumental break rather than for any harmonic innovation introduced by the rare-groove scene itself.

## Arrangement

Song structure follows the original source recording rather than any rare-groove-specific convention; DJs selected tracks based on a hard-hitting, isolatable break — often a drum or bass passage — near the intro or in an instrumental breakdown, which they would extend, loop, or mix between records rather than the original song's overall arrangement.

## Instruments

Instrumentation is entirely inherited from the 1960s-70s American funk, soul, and jazz-funk recordings being curated — James Brown-style breakbeat drumming, syncopated funk bass, chicken-scratch guitar, and riff-based horn sections — rather than defined by any instrumentation unique to the rare-groove scene itself.

## Sound Design

Rare groove adds no new studio sound design of its own; its sonic identity is the analog character of the original source recordings, later extended through DJ-level sampling and looping of isolated breaks that fed directly into hip-hop and early jungle/breakbeat production in the late 1980s and early 1990s.

## MIDI Programming

When producing new material in a rare-groove-influenced style, program or sample a hard, breakbeat-style funk drum pattern with an isolatable, loop-ready break, paired with a syncopated 1970s-style funk bass line, and prioritize authentic period-accurate groove feel over modern programming conventions.

## Mixing

When remastering or resampling source material, preserve the original recording's warm, midrange-forward funk character and room ambience rather than modernizing the tonal balance; heavier processing is typically reserved for the specific act of resampling a break into a new hip-hop or breakbeat production.

## Mastering

Modern reissue/compilation masters typically target -10 to -8 LUFS integrated, preserving the moderate dynamic range and warm, midrange-forward, breakbeat-ready character of the original 1970s analog source recordings.

## Reference Artists

James Brown's "Funky Drummer," Marlena Shaw's "Woman of the Ghetto," and The Meters' "Cissy Strut" are foundational rare-groove source tracks prized for their breaks, while Rose Royce's "Is It Love You're After" stands as a defining London rare-groove club anthem; DJs Norman Jay and Gilles Peterson were the scene's key tastemakers.

## Production Notes

Understand rare groove primarily as a DJ and curatorial culture coined by Norman Jay, built on unearthing obscure 1960s-70s American funk, soul, and jazz-funk singles rather than as a new studio production style, and prioritize tracks with a strong, isolatable break — the quality that made records valuable to both rare-groove DJs and the hip-hop and jungle producers who later sampled the same source material. Avoid confusing rare groove with Northern soul, a distinct 1970s Northern England ballroom culture built on 1960s Motown-style singles rather than 1970s funk breaks, and recognize rare groove's direct historical role in supplying the breakbeats foundational to UK sampling culture and early jungle/drum and bass.
