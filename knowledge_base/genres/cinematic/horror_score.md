## History

Horror scoring's modern sound was decisively shaped by Bernard Herrmann's score for Alfred Hitchcock's Psycho (1960). Hitchcock originally wanted the film's shower-murder scene to play without any music at all; Herrmann insisted on trying his composed cue, "The Murder," and Hitchcock afterward agreed it vastly intensified the scene — the shrieking, dissonant string figures became one of film history's most influential musical moments. Herrmann's score moves gradually from tonal material toward "mistuned" and eventually fully atonal harmonic material, and its central "Mother Motif" has no stable tonal form, deconstructing further as the narrative's psychological horror deepens. Two decades later, Stanley Kubrick's The Shining (1980) drew on the concert-hall avant-garde tradition directly, using excerpts from Krzysztof Penderecki's Utrenja alongside music by György Ligeti and Béla Bartók, cementing 20th-century atonal classical composition as a lasting horror-scoring reference point. This lineage — dissonant, atonal orchestral writing associated psychologically with "the Other" and with deviation from order — remains audible in horror scoring today.

## Music Theory

Horror scoring largely abandons functional harmony in favor of non-functional dissonance: tone clusters, the tritone, and close intervals like the minor 2nd and major 7th replace conventional chord progressions. Where pitch material has any center at all, it tends to drift chromatically rather than resolve, echoing Herrmann's "mistuned" harmonic technique in Psycho. Melody is frequently subordinated entirely to texture and dissonance, and where recognizable themes exist, they are often progressively deconstructed as a film's tension escalates rather than developed conventionally.

## Arrangement

Cues typically alternate between sparse, unsettling tension-building passages — tremolo, glissando, tight chromatic clusters, often without a fixed pulse — and sudden "stingers," loud dissonant hits precisely synchronized to on-screen jump scares, a structural technique traceable directly to Herrmann's Psycho shower scene. A return to silence or sparse texture typically follows a stinger, exploiting the audience's anticipation of a second shock, and cues frequently end unresolved rather than with a cadential resolution.

## Instruments

Strings are the genre's core instrument, used through extended techniques — tremolo, glissando, sul ponticello, col legno — rather than conventional melodic writing, with full orchestral forces reserved for maximum-impact stingers. Prepared piano, altered with objects placed on or between its strings, is a recurring technique for unsettling, non-piano-like textures, and percussion is used sparingly and irregularly, deliberately avoiding a steady beat that would imply order and safety.

## Sound Design

Horror scoring blends acoustic extended techniques with synthesized drones, granular textures, and sub-bass for low-frequency dread, often processing acoustic sources heavily to obscure their natural timbre into uncanny, unidentifiable textures. Infrasound — very low-frequency content near or below the threshold of conscious hearing — is a well-documented technique for inducing physical unease, and silence itself functions as a deliberate structural device, most powerfully as the setup for a stinger.

## MIDI Programming

Program pitch-bend and portamento automation extensively to emulate string glissando, and favor tight chromatic clusters and rapid tremolo over conventional melodic phrases. Bass and drum parts should avoid rhythmic regularity — dissonant, slowly shifting pitch movement suits the genre far better than a steady groove. Velocity should span an extreme range, from near-silent sustained passages to maximum-velocity stinger hits, and humanization should be applied selectively: looser micro-timing enhances tension passages, while stingers benefit from tight, precisely synchronized hits.

## Mixing

EQ work should emphasize extreme frequency ranges — deep sub-bass drones and high, shrieking string textures — while leaving the midrange comparatively sparse for an unsettling, hollow character. Compression should stay minimal during tension-building passages to preserve raw dynamic extremes, reserving heavier, punchier compression for stingers. Reverb is used contrastively, alternating cavernous wetness with unnervingly dry, close passages, and stereo imaging can be manipulated aggressively — sudden mono-to-wide shifts or panned scares — as a disorientation device unlike the stable imaging of most other cinematic genres.

## Mastering

Masters target roughly -16 to -12 LUFS integrated, preserving an extremely wide dynamic range — heavy limiting actively undermines the genre, since its effectiveness depends on the contrast between near-silence and sudden loud stingers. The frequency balance favors extended sub-bass and extreme high-frequency string content, with a deliberately sparser midrange than most other cinematic genres.

## Reference Artists

Bernard Herrmann's "The Murder," from Psycho (1960), is the genre's foundational reference recording, establishing dissonant string writing and the stinger technique as horror scoring's signature devices. Krzysztof Penderecki and György Ligeti, both drawn from the 20th-century concert-hall avant-garde and used prominently by Stanley Kubrick in The Shining (1980), represent the genre's other major stylistic lineage, bringing atonal orchestral composition directly into horror's sonic vocabulary.

## Production Notes

- Build tension cues around extended string techniques (tremolo, glissando, sul ponticello) rather than conventional melody and harmony.
- Use silence as a deliberate structural tool — a sudden absence of sound heightens the impact of the stinger that follows.
- Reserve full-ensemble dissonant hits (stingers) for precise, frame-accurate synchronization with on-screen shocks, rather than overusing them.
- Consider prepared piano or heavily processed acoustic instruments for unsettling, hard-to-identify timbres.
- Avoid over-compressing the mix — it destroys the wide dynamic contrast between quiet dread and loud stingers the genre depends on.
- Modern hybrid approach: layer processed field recordings and infrasound with orchestral drones, and use granular synthesis for slowly evolving, texturally unstable drone beds.
