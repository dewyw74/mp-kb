## History

Acid house was born in Chicago around 1985-1987 when DJ Pierre, Spanky, and Herb J (recording as Phuture) began experimenting with a Roland TB-303 Bass Line synthesizer — originally designed and marketed as a bass-guitar-accompaniment practice tool that had commercially flopped — discovering that aggressively sweeping its cutoff and resonance controls produced a squelching, otherworldly sound unlike anything the instrument's designers intended. "Acid Tracks" (1987), built from an extended jam session recorded onto tape, became the foundational acid house record and gave the genre its name. The sound spread rapidly through Chicago's house scene (Armando, Adonis) and then to the UK, where it fueled the acid house-driven rave explosion of 1988's "Second Summer of Love," fundamentally shaping the trajectory of UK dance culture and, subsequently, both techno's harder acid-techno strand and the broader trajectory of electronic dance music.

## Music Theory

Acid house essentially inverts conventional harmonic thinking: the TB-303 bassline is not accompanied by chords so much as it *is* the entire harmonic and melodic content of the track, typically implying a single static minor tonal center (A minor, C minor, E minor) rather than moving through a progression. What would be "melody" and "harmony" in conventional theory terms is instead generated through the 303's own pattern (pitch sequence, slide, accent) combined with continuous filter cutoff and resonance manipulation — a form of timbral composition that operates largely outside traditional scale-and-chord thinking, closer to sound-design-as-composition than to conventional songwriting.

## Arrangement

A typical acid house track opens with drums and a relatively closed-filter 303 pattern, then develops entirely through the ongoing mutation of that single pattern — opening the filter, increasing resonance, and adjusting accent/slide parameters over time rather than introducing new melodic or harmonic sections. There is no conventional verse-chorus structure; the "arrangement" is largely a filter-automation performance layered over a steady, restrained drum groove. A breakdown may mute the kick to expose the 303 pattern further, and the track's climax is the point of maximum filter aggression and resonant squelch rather than a harmonic or melodic peak.

## Instruments

The Roland TB-303 Bass Line synthesizer is the singular, genre-defining instrument — its bassline serves simultaneously as bass, lead melody, and the primary object of sound-design manipulation. Drums come from a Roland TR-808 or TR-909 (or emulations), kept deliberately simple — steady four-on-the-floor kick, closed hats, sparse claps — to leave maximum sonic and psychological space for the 303 to dominate. Additional instrumentation (synth pads, keys, guitars) is essentially absent in classic examples of the genre; acid house's sparseness is as definitional as its squelch.

## Sound Design

Sound design in acid house is functionally synonymous with 303 programming: the resonant lowpass filter, driven hard with high resonance, generates the genre's entire sonic identity, with cutoff frequency and resonance manipulated in real time or via automation as the primary creative act. Accent and slide parameters on the 303's sequencer add sharp emphasis and glissando-like note transitions that intensify the squelching, almost vocal quality of the line. Classic acid house largely avoids heavy additional effects processing — delay and reverb are used sparingly, if at all, to preserve the raw, driven character of the filter itself.

## MIDI Programming

The 303's 16-step sequencer (or a MIDI-programmed emulation of it) creates short, often angular or stepwise patterns using slide (glide) and accent (emphasis) parameters, which combine with ongoing filter/resonance automation to produce the genre's constantly mutating character — programming the pattern is only half the work; automating its filter behavior over time is the other, equally essential half. Drums stay deliberately simple and steady, avoiding elaborate variation so as not to compete with the 303's psychedelic focus. There is no separate melodic lead line in the conventional sense — the bassline pattern's own pitch content, combined with its timbral mutation, carries all melodic interest.

## Mixing

Classic acid house mixing is minimal by design: the 303 is often left close to its raw recorded state since its filter-driven character is the entire point, with light compression preserving the natural rise and fall of resonant sweeps rather than flattening them. EQ is used sparingly, mostly to carve modest space for the kick beneath the 303's resonant peaks in modern productions. Reverb and delay are used sparingly to avoid diluting the raw, in-your-face aggression of the filter squelch, and the classic hardware-era sound is largely mono/centered.

## Mastering

Acid house masters sit around -9 to -7 LUFS integrated with moderate dynamics preserved so the 303's filter sweeps retain their full expressive range rather than being squashed by heavy limiting. Frequency balance is midrange-forward, since the 303's resonant peak content lives predominantly in the low-mid to mid range, with sub bass kept comparatively restrained relative to modern bass-focused EDM production.

## Reference Artists

Phuture's "Acid Tracks" is the foundational recording that named and defined the genre. DJ Pierre, as a member of Phuture and through his subsequent solo/collaborative work, remains a central figure in the genre's ongoing legacy. Armando's "Land of Confusion" and Adonis's "We're Rockin' Down the House" represent the wider Chicago acid house scene of the late 1980s, while Josh Wink's "Higher State of Consciousness" (1995) demonstrates the sound's continued vitality and evolution into the following decade.

## Production Notes

Learn the TB-303's core parameter set — cutoff, resonance, accent, slide, and envelope modulation — deeply, since the entire genre's expressive range lives in real-time manipulation of these controls rather than in pattern composition alone. Keep drums and any additional instrumentation minimal and restrained, giving the 303 line maximum sonic space to do its psychedelic work. Record filter and resonance automation as a live, hands-on performance pass where possible, since the slightly imperfect, human sweep of a hand-tweaked knob is central to the genre's feel. Common mistakes include over-arranging with competing synths or chords that dilute the 303's centrality, using a static unautomated pattern (which produces a merely 303-flavored track rather than genuine acid house), and burying the filter squelch under excessive reverb or effects. Modern producers rely on software 303 emulations with detailed modulation/automation lanes for precise, repeatable filter mutation, and often layer a modern sub-bass synth beneath the 303 to add low-end weight the original hardware-limited genre lacked, while keeping the 303 itself dominant in the midrange.
