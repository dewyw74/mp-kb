## History

House music originated in Chicago in the early-to-mid 1980s, named after the Warehouse club where DJ Frankie Knuckles reworked disco records with drum-machine-driven edits and extended breaks for the dancefloor. As disco backlash hit mainstream radio around 1979-1980, Chicago's Black and gay club culture kept the genre's spirit alive underground, and DJs began programming Roland TR-909 and TR-808 drum machines alongside disco and Italo disco records to create longer, more hypnotic club experiences. Marshall Jefferson's "Move Your Body" (1986) and Mr. Fingers' (Larry Heard) deep, jazzy productions established house as its own genre distinct from the disco it descended from. By the late 1980s house had spread to the UK and Europe, fracturing into numerous regional and stylistic strands (acid house, deep house, garage) that form the basis of nearly all modern four-on-the-floor club music. House remains the direct ancestor of the entire EDM "house" family covered in this knowledge base.

## Music Theory

House harmony is groove-first: many classic and modern house tracks ride a single chord or a slow two-to-four-chord vamp, often lifted or sampled from disco, soul, and gospel records, voiced with extended 7th, 9th, or 11th tensions for warmth and sophistication despite the harmonic simplicity. Minor keys (A minor, C minor, F minor) dominate for a moodier, more hypnotic club feel, while major-key, gospel-influenced progressions (vi-IV-I-V) appear in more uplifting, vocal-driven house. Melodic interest is generally delegated to a vocal sample, chord stab riff, or bassline hook rather than a developed lead melody — the loop, not the phrase, is the compositional unit.

## Arrangement

A house track is built for the DJ mix as much as for standalone listening: it opens with drums alone (kick, hats, percussion) for 16-32 bars, giving DJs a clean beatmatching point, before the bassline and then chords/hook layer in gradually. Rather than a pop verse-chorus structure, house uses a "groove section" that establishes the core elements, a breakdown that strips back to pads/vocal/chords, and a rebuild via filter sweeps and percussion rolls back into the full groove — the "drop" in house is a return to full-density groove, not a sub-bass hit like mainstream festival EDM. Outros mirror intros, peeling elements away back to drums-only for clean mixing out.

## Instruments

The TR-909 and TR-808 (hardware or emulated) define house's drum sound: punchy analog kick, crisp off-beat closed hats, claps or rimshots on 2 and 4. Basslines are either sampled disco/funk bass guitar riffs, simple root-octave synth bass, or (in acid house) a Roland TB-303 line. Organ and Rhodes/Wurlitzer electric piano stabs, often sampled directly from disco and soul records, provide the harmonic "chord" identity that defines the classic house sound, alongside string-machine pads and the occasional sampled guitar loop for texture.

## Sound Design

House sound design spans analog/virtual-analog subtractive synthesis (Juno/Jupiter-style pads and basses) and heavy use of sampling — chopping loops and one-shots from disco, soul, and funk records remains a defining production technique. Resonant lowpass filter sweeps are the genre's signature movement device, used both as a sound-design element on pads/stabs and as an arrangement tool for building and releasing tension. Plate/room reverb and tape saturation add warmth and space without diluting the groove's tightness, and vinyl-style lo-fi processing is often applied to samples to preserve an authentic, dusty character.

## MIDI Programming

The four-on-the-floor kick (quarter notes) anchors everything; closed hats sit on the off-beat 8ths with deliberate velocity variation and swing (roughly 54-58%) to create house's characteristic shuffle, distinguishing it from the stricter, less-swung feel of techno. Claps or snares land on 2 and 4, with an open hat accent typically on the last 16th of alternating bars. Basslines are simple and repetitive, locked to the kick with syncopated off-beats for groove. Chord stabs and vocal hooks are programmed as short, repeated loops rather than developed melodic phrases, with call-and-response between vocal sample and instrumental hook a common device.

## Mixing

Sidechain compression ducking bass and pads against the kick is house's signature rhythmic mix technique, producing the genre's characteristic "pumping" groove feel. EQ carves a single clear low-end pocket for kick and bass, keeping stabs and pads out of the low-mids. Reverb (plate/room) sits moderately on vocals and stabs for depth without washing out groove tightness, while syncopated delays on percussion and vocal chops add rhythmic complexity. Kick and bass stay centered/mono for club translation; hats and chord stabs are spread wider for texture.

## Mastering

House masters target roughly -9 to -7 LUFS integrated, preserving enough dynamic range for the sidechain pump and groove punch to read clearly on club systems rather than being flattened by aggressive limiting. Frequency balance favors warm, present low-mids and a controlled (not over-extended) sub region suited to club PA systems, with a bright but not harsh top end on hats and percussion.

## Reference Artists

Frankie Knuckles is the foundational figure — "Your Love" and his Warehouse sets essentially codified the genre. Marshall Jefferson's "Move Your Body" is a canonical uptempo, piano-driven house anthem, while Larry Heard's work as Mr. Fingers ("Can You Feel It") defines the deeper, jazzier strand that would become deep house. Modern house is well represented by Disclosure, who blend classic sampled-soul house DNA with contemporary production polish.

## Production Notes

Build house tracks with the DJ mix in mind: functional drums-only intros/outros are not optional filler, they are structurally essential. Sidechain the kick into bass and pads for the pumping groove feel that is as much a genre identifier as any specific sound. Favor filter-sweep builds over white-noise-riser-into-sub-drop builds to keep house's groove-first character distinct from festival EDM. Common mistakes include over-extending the sub bass into festival-EDM territory (which erodes house's club-focused low end), skipping swing/groove templates on hats (producing a stiff, un-shuffled feel), and over-composing melodically instead of trusting a strong, repetitive hook. Modern producers recreate vintage swing using groove-pool/quantization templates and layer sampled 909/808 kits with modern synthesized kicks for extra low-end weight while retaining vintage character.
