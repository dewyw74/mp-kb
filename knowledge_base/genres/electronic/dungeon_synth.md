## History

Dungeon synth emerged in the late 1980s and early 1990s from the early Norwegian black metal scene, where musicians created instrumental, ambient synthesizer projects as solo endeavors that juxtaposed black metal aesthetics and themes against the genre's typical tremolo-picking, blast beats, and shrieked vocals — material commonly used as intros, interludes, or outros on black metal, death metal, and heavy metal albums through the 1980s and 1990s. Mortiis, founded by Håvard Ellefsen (bassist of Emperor), and Burzum, Varg Vikernes' black metal solo project, are the genre's progenitors. Ellefsen cited Tangerine Dream, Klaus Schulze, Skinny Puppy, and Enigma as influences when starting Mortiis, and the second iteration of his early demo tape marked the first use of the phrase "dark dungeon music" to describe the emerging style. Mortiis's 1994 debut album Født til å Herske ("Born to Rule") — a single 53-minute composition split into two tracks, drawing on horns, piano-like keys, string emulation, some percussion, and electronic choir — is regarded as "ground zero" for dungeon synth as a standalone genre, the first release built entirely around the style rather than as brief accompaniment on a black metal record. Though the genre gained momentum in the 1990s, it saw no significant resurgence until the early 2010s, when a new wave of dungeon synth popularity emerged.

## Music Theory

Dungeon synth favors slow, minimal harmonic movement anchored by sustained pads and drones, built on simple, medieval-evoking minor-key progressions (i-iv-v) rather than complex harmonic development. Melodies are slow and haunting, creating a melancholic, eerie, or contemplative mood, with temporal organization tending toward the gestural rather than metrically rigid — expressive timing, elongated phrases, and loosely articulated sections define the genre's compositional approach rather than tightly quantized repetition.

## Arrangement

Pieces typically open with an extended, drone-based or atmospheric passage establishing a medieval or otherworldly mood before clearer melodic material — often emulating horns, strings, or choir — develops gradually over a sustained harmonic bed. Rather than a conventional dynamic build toward a climactic chorus, the genre's deepest atmospheric moments emerge from the accumulated weight of slowly-evolving, layered orchestral-synth textures, with pieces often dissolving back into atmospheric drone rather than resolving cleanly.

## Instruments

Synthesizers emulating strings, flutes, pipe organs, and choirs form the genre's entire instrumental palette — Mortiis's Født til å Herske drew specifically on horns, piano-like keys, classical string emulation, sparse percussion, and electronic choir. Guitar is deliberately absent despite the genre's black metal origins, and percussion, where present, stays sparse and slow, supporting the genre's contemplative pace rather than driving rhythmic momentum.

## Sound Design

Intentional lo-fi degradation — crackle, warble, hiss — is a defining, deliberate aesthetic choice contributing to the genre's aged, ancient atmosphere, achieved historically through cassette recording and budget synthesizer technology and replicable today through tape emulation and frequency-degradation plugins. Synthesizer patches emulating strings, horns, flutes, and electronic choir provide the genre's orchestral-adjacent atmosphere without any real acoustic instrumentation.

## MIDI Programming

Program sustained low drones or pedal tones as harmonic foundation rather than a rhythmically active bassline, and keep percussion sparse and slow where used at all. Melodic lines should carry slow, haunting character with gestural, expressive timing rather than tight quantization — elongated phrases and loosely articulated sections are central to the genre's compositional identity, and significant humanization is essential throughout.

## Mixing

Keep the overall balance warm and low-fidelity, avoiding excessive brightness or clarity that would contradict the genre's aged, ancient sonic character. Compression should stay minimal, preserving the genre's raw, unpolished dynamics, and reverb should contribute to a cavernous, ancient atmosphere often combined with intentional lo-fi degradation rather than pristine, modern reverb treatment. Stereo imaging is often narrow or mono in period-authentic productions, reflecting the genre's raw, home-recorded origins.

## Mastering

Masters target roughly -18 to -14 LUFS integrated, preserving a wide, natural dynamic range consistent with the genre's contemplative, unpolished character rather than any modern loudness approach. The frequency balance stays warm, low-fidelity, and intentionally imperfect, embracing crackle, warble, and hiss as part of the genre's aged atmosphere rather than flaws to correct.

## Reference Artists

Mortiis's Født til å Herske (1994) stands as the genre's foundational, defining recording — "ground zero for dungeon synth music" and the first release to build an entire standalone work in the style. Burzum, Varg Vikernes' black metal solo project, represents the genre's other essential early progenitor, both emerging from the same early Norwegian black metal scene.

## Production Notes

- Embrace lo-fi, imperfect production — crackle, warble, hiss, and cassette-style degradation are defining aesthetic choices.
- Use synthesizer patches emulating strings, horns, flutes, and choir as the genre's entire instrumental palette, omitting guitar entirely.
- Write with gestural, expressive timing rather than tight quantization.
- Build atmosphere through slow, sustained drones and gradually layered orchestral-synth textures.
- Avoid over-polishing the production or adding guitar/conventional metal instrumentation — both directly contradict the genre's essential lo-fi, guitar-free identity.
- Modern hybrid approach: use cassette tape emulation and frequency degradation plugins for authentic lo-fi character, and record with deliberately loose, expressive timing rather than quantizing to a rigid grid.
