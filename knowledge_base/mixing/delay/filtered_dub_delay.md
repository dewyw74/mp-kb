# Filtered/Dub Delay

Filtered, feedback-heavy "dub delay" is one of the most explicitly lineage-traced techniques in the knowledge base — genre entry after genre entry attributes it directly to reggae/dub production and King Tubby/Lee Perry-style mixing-desk practice, even in genres (bleep techno, jungle, post-punk) with no other obvious reggae connection. The defining sonic ingredients are consistent: filtered (usually darkening) repeats, high feedback, and a tape-echo-modeled source character.

## The Reggae/Dub Origin

`reggae.md` documents the technique as foundational rather than incidental: "Heavy spring reverb and tape/analog delay, especially on vocals and lead instruments," and its sound-design summary states the historical claim directly — "These techniques — reverb throws, delay feedback, drop-outs — originated in reggae/dub production and went on to profoundly influence remix culture and electronic music production more broadly." `dancehall.md` describes the effect in its full psychedelic intensity: "Dub delay (long tape echo with heavy feedback) is used extensively as a creative, psychedelic sound design tool on vocals and snares," specifically calling out "Heavy use of Dub delay (long, feedback-heavy tape delays) on specific snare hits or vocal shouts." `ambient_dub.md` names the specific hardware lineage and describes feedback pushed to its structural limit: "long, dense tape-delay-style echo (Roland Space Echo emulation) on the bassline or skank chord is the genre's single most defining effect," with feedback chains "pushed toward self-oscillation for an evolving, semi-generative echo."

## Filtering as the Defining Character (Not Just an Add-On)

What separates "dub delay" from an ordinary tape delay is the filtering applied to the repeats. `trip_hop.md` names this explicitly: "Dub-style tape delay on vocal ad-libs and guitar/sample stabs, often with filtered, degrading feedback repeats." `downtempo.md` describes the same effect as "Dub-style tape delay with feedback and filtering, often used as a rhythmic accent on vocals, guitar stabs, or percussion." `bedroom_pop.md`'s much lighter "Simple, often filtered delay used sparingly for texture" shows the same filtering principle scaled down to a much subtler pop context, confirming this isn't purely a reggae-genre-exclusive technique — it's a general filtered-delay approach that happens to originate from, and remains strongest in, dub lineage genres.

## Spreading Beyond Reggae Into Electronic and Rock Lineages

Several genres with no direct reggae identity still cite dub delay explicitly as an inherited technique. `bleep_techno.md`: "Dub-style delay and echo on percussion and bleep tones (a direct reggae/dub sound-system influence)... reflecting the reggae/dub sound-system lineage explicitly cited by the genre's originators." `jungle.md`: "Dub-style tape echo on vocal chops and stabs is a genre signature, referencing the reggae soundsystem tradition directly." `post_punk.md` treats it as structurally, not just decoratively, important: "Dub-style delay (echo with feedback, sometimes filtered) is a structurally important texture device, not just an ear-candy effect, reflecting the genre's reggae/dub influence," recommending "Dub-style delay plugins with filtered feedback loops for authentic spacious, rhythmic echo texture." `reggae_rock.md` and `reggae_rap.md` both use it as the explicit signal of reggae lineage within a hybrid genre — `reggae_rap.md`: "Dub-style echo/delay on vocal ad-libs, drawing on reggae/dub production tradition... more prominent here than in typical mainstream hip-hop of the same era."

## Dub Techno's Chamber-and-Delay Signature

`dub_techno.md` documents the technique at its most central: "Long, dense delay (often dub-style tape delay emulation) on the chord stab is the single most defining effect in the genre," paired with "Deep, spacious reverb (plate, hall, or dub-chamber-style algorithmic reverb) layered with the delay for the genre's signature 'chamber' atmosphere." Here dub delay isn't one texture among several — it's named as *the* single most defining effect of the entire genre, with reverb explicitly secondary and complementary to it.

## Common Mistakes

**Using a clean digital delay and calling it "dub delay."** Nearly every citation above ties the effect specifically to filtered, degrading repeats modeled on tape-echo hardware — a pristine digital delay without filtering or saturation misses the character the technique depends on.

**Treating dub delay as purely decorative rather than structural.** `post_punk.md` and `dub_techno.md` both explicitly warn against this framing — in dub-lineage genres, delay is frequently the single most important sound-design element in the mix, not an optional ear-candy layer.

**Setting feedback and filtering statically instead of riding them live.** `ambient_dub.md`'s description of "live 'dub mixing' technique — muting/unmuting channels and riding echo/reverb sends in real time as a performance, not a fixed automation curve" is central to how the technique was originated; a static filtered-delay setting captures the tone but misses the performative, evolving character King Tubby and Lee Perry built the technique around.
